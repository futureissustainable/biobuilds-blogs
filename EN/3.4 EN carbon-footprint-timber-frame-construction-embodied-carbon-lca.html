The construction sector is responsible for 34% of global CO2 emissions, according to the UNEP Global Status Report for Buildings and Construction 2024/2025. That figure encompasses both the energy used to operate buildings and the emissions generated by manufacturing the materials from which they are built. Until recently, policy and industry attention focused almost exclusively on the operational side: insulation, heating systems, energy ratings. The material side was largely ignored.

That is changing. Lifecycle assessment research now demonstrates that the materials chosen for a building's structure can represent 10-20% of its total lifetime emissions in the EU, and for high-performance buildings like Passivhaus homes where operational energy is already minimal, embodied carbon becomes the dominant factor. The evidence points consistently in one direction: timber-frame construction produces dramatically lower embodied carbon than concrete or steel alternatives, while actively storing atmospheric CO2 in the building structure itself.

What is embodied carbon in construction

Embodied carbon is the total greenhouse gas emissions produced during the extraction, manufacturing, transportation, installation, maintenance, and end-of-life disposal of building materials. Unlike operational carbon, which accumulates over decades and can be reduced through efficiency upgrades, embodied carbon is effectively locked in at the moment a building is constructed. Once the concrete is poured or the steel is fabricated, those emissions cannot be recaptured.

The distinction matters because as buildings become more energy-efficient, embodied carbon represents a growing share of lifetime emissions. In a conventional building with poor insulation and gas heating, operational emissions dwarf material emissions over a 50-year lifespan. In a Passivhaus-certified home consuming 90% less heating energy, the relationship inverts: the materials used to construct the building contribute more to its total carbon footprint than the energy required to operate it.

The UNEP estimates that embodied carbon from the global building sector contributes approximately 2.9 gigatonnes of CO2 annually. Cement production alone accounts for roughly 8% of global greenhouse gas emissions, and the iron and steel industry adds another 5%. These are not marginal contributions. They represent fundamental industrial processes that release carbon dioxide both through fossil fuel combustion for heat and through the chemical reactions inherent to material production.

Why embodied carbon matters now

Three developments have elevated embodied carbon from a niche academic concern to a regulatory and market priority. First, the EU's revised Energy Performance of Buildings Directive (EPBD 2024/1275) mandates whole-life carbon reporting for all new buildings from 2030. Second, operational emissions are declining as building codes tighten and renewable energy expands, making embodied carbon a proportionally larger share of the total. Third, lifecycle assessment methodology has matured to the point where reliable comparisons between structural materials are possible.

For families building a new home, these developments have a practical consequence: the structural material chosen for their house now carries measurable, regulated, and increasingly valued carbon implications.

Timber vs concrete and steel: the LCA evidence

Lifecycle assessment research consistently demonstrates that mass timber construction produces significantly lower embodied carbon than concrete or steel alternatives. A comprehensive review of 62 peer-reviewed studies found that on average, approximately 43% of greenhouse gas emissions are avoided when reinforced concrete structures are substituted with mass timber, according to research published in the journal Sustainability (MDPI).

The range across studies spans 22-50% reduction in embodied carbon for timber versus concrete buildings, with variation driven by building type, region, forestry practices, and methodological assumptions. While no single study captures every scenario, the direction of the evidence is unambiguous: timber produces less embodied carbon than concrete or steel across virtually all conditions studied.

Material-level emissions

The emissions gap begins at the most fundamental level: how each material is made. Producing one tonne of concrete emits approximately 0.5 tonnes of CO2. Producing one tonne of steel emits roughly 2 tonnes of CO2. Both processes require temperatures exceeding 1,000°C, typically achieved by burning fossil fuels. Cement production additionally releases CO2 through calcination—a chemical reaction where calcium carbonate is converted to calcium oxide, liberating carbon dioxide regardless of the energy source used.

Timber production, by contrast, requires far less energy. The raw material extraction stage emits approximately 12 kg CO2 per cubic metre, according to Ecochain LCA Software. Manufacturing processes add further emissions, but the total remains a fraction of concrete or steel production. More importantly, timber carries a unique advantage that no mineral-based material can match: it stores carbon absorbed during growth.

Carbon storage: how wood sequesters CO2

Trees absorb carbon dioxide from the atmosphere during growth through photosynthesis, converting it into the organic carbon compounds that form wood. When that tree is harvested and the wood becomes a building, the carbon remains stored rather than returning to the atmosphere through decomposition. Each cubic metre of timber stores approximately 0.9 to 1.0 tonnes of CO2 equivalent—carbon that stays locked in the building structure for as long as the building stands.

This storage effect is not trivial. A five-story residential building structured in laminated timber stores up to 180 kg of carbon per square metre of floor area—three times more carbon than the above-ground biomass of high-density natural forests, according to research cited by the Carbon Leadership Forum. The building becomes, in effect, a carbon sink: a structure that contains more atmospheric carbon than was emitted during its production.

The implications become striking when carbon storage is factored into lifecycle assessments. One study of a hybrid CLT building found that the timber structure stored 1,556 tonnes of CO2, offsetting the emissions from product manufacturing and construction and resulting in a net negative emission of 1,222 tonnes of CO2 equivalent. The building did not merely have a lower carbon footprint than a concrete alternative—it actively removed carbon from the atmosphere.

Key Takeaway

Timber is the only mainstream structural material that stores more carbon than it emits during production. Concrete and steel emit CO2 during manufacturing with no storage offset. This fundamental asymmetry explains why lifecycle assessments consistently favor timber: it starts the carbon accounting from a negative baseline rather than zero.

How long does the carbon stay stored?

Carbon remains stored in timber for as long as the wood remains intact. In a well-maintained timber-frame building, this means 100 years or more. If the timber is reused in a new structure at end of life—an approach increasingly encouraged by circular economy principles—the storage period extends further. Carbon is only released if the timber burns or decomposes. The longevity of timber buildings, documented extensively from medieval European examples still standing after 500+ years, makes wood an effective long-term carbon store.

For CLT specifically, each cubic metre retains a net carbon storage of approximately 676 kg CO2 after manufacturing, according to Ecochain's lifecycle data. The manufacturing process consumes some energy and generates some emissions, but these are far outweighed by the biogenic carbon embodied in the wood itself.

Lifecycle stages explained: from forest to demolition

A building's carbon footprint is assessed using standardized lifecycle assessment (LCA) methodology defined by the European standard EN 15978. The framework divides a building's life into distinct modules, each capturing different emission sources. Understanding these stages clarifies where timber's advantages—and limitations—actually occur.

Modules A1-A3: product manufacturing

These modules cover raw material extraction, transport to factory, and manufacturing. For concrete, this includes cement production with its unavoidable calcination emissions. For steel, it includes ore extraction and blast furnace operation. For timber, it includes forestry operations, sawmill processing, and engineered wood manufacturing. Timber's advantage is most pronounced at this stage, with emissions typically 50-75% lower than concrete or steel equivalents.

Modules A4-A5: transport and construction

Transport emissions depend on distance from factory to site. Timber, being lighter than concrete, generally requires fewer truck journeys. Transportation adds approximately 17 kg CO2 per cubic metre of timber for domestic transport, rising to 38 kg CO2/m³ for international shipping, according to Ecochain. Construction-site emissions are minimal for factory-built timber modules compared to cast-in-place concrete, which requires on-site mixing, pouring, and curing.

Modules B1-B7: use phase

The use phase covers maintenance, repair, replacement, and operational energy over the building's service life. Timber requires periodic maintenance of exterior finishes but minimal structural intervention if properly protected from moisture. The operational energy module (B6) is where Passivhaus certification delivers its advantage, cutting heating demand by up to 90% and dramatically reducing lifetime operational carbon.

Modules C1-C4: end of life

End-of-life scenarios significantly affect timber's overall carbon balance. If timber is burned for energy recovery, stored carbon returns to the atmosphere but displaces fossil fuel use. If timber is landfilled, carbon storage continues indefinitely but represents a resource waste. If timber is recycled or reused in new construction, carbon storage is extended and virgin material demand is reduced. LCA results vary considerably depending on which end-of-life scenario is modeled.

Module D: beyond the lifecycle

Module D credits materials for benefits they provide beyond the building's life, such as energy recovery from incineration or avoided emissions from recycling. Timber typically performs well in Module D because wood waste can generate energy through combustion, displacing fossil fuels. However, Module D credits are reported separately from the core lifecycle and are sometimes excluded from comparisons.

Key Takeaway

Timber's carbon advantage is strongest in the production phase (A1-A3), where emissions are 50-75% lower than concrete or steel. The advantage is maintained or extended through the use phase and can be further enhanced through end-of-life reuse or recycling. LCA methodology is standardized under EN 15978 and the EU's Level(s) framework, ensuring comparable results across studies.

EU regulation: the EPBD and whole-life carbon

The revised Energy Performance of Buildings Directive (EPBD 2024/1275), which entered into force in May 2024, represents a historic shift in European building regulation. For the first time, EU legislation requires the reporting and eventual regulation of embodied carbon in buildings—not just the operational energy that previous directives addressed.

The directive's requirements unfold in phases. By the end of 2025, the European Commission is establishing a standardized method for calculating the Global Warming Potential (GWP) of buildings using the EN 15978 and Level(s) framework. By January 2027, Member States must adopt national calculation frameworks. From 2028, whole-life carbon disclosure is required for new buildings larger than 1,000 m². From 2030, all new buildings must report whole-life carbon, and Member States must set GWP thresholds that progressively decrease over time.

All new public buildings must be zero-emission buildings (ZEBs) from 2028, and all other new buildings from 2030. The directive also sets renovation targets: Member States must reduce residential energy use by 16% by 2030 and 20-22% by 2035.

The new Directive, for the first time, will require regulation of the carbon embodied in buildings associated with their construction, maintenance, and demolition. Up until now, legislation solely looked at the energy used for operating buildings.

What this means for material choice

Mandatory whole-life carbon reporting creates a regulatory environment that rewards lower-embodied-carbon materials. When every new building must disclose its total carbon footprint including materials, structural timber's 22-50% advantage over concrete becomes a tangible, documented benefit visible in the building's energy performance certificate. This shifts the conversation from theoretical environmental preference to measurable regulatory compliance.

For markets like Germany and Austria where building regulations are already stringent, the EPBD's whole-life carbon requirements add another dimension favoring timber-frame construction. Germany's KfW energy-efficient construction programs already incentivize high-performance buildings; as GWP thresholds take effect, timber's embodied carbon advantage may become a factor in financing and subsidy decisions.

Sustainable forestry: the prerequisite

Timber's carbon benefits depend entirely on the wood being sourced from sustainably managed forests. If forests are harvested faster than they regrow, or if harvesting destroys carbon-rich ecosystems, the lifecycle carbon balance deteriorates. As MIT professor John Fernández noted in his analysis for the MIT Climate Portal, "a forest can end up releasing more carbon than it captures" if management practices are inadequate.

Europe's forestry sector operates under robust certification systems. The Forest Stewardship Council (FSC) and the Programme for the Endorsement of Forest Certification (PEFC) verify that forests are managed according to principles that maintain biodiversity, protect water resources, ensure regeneration after harvest, and respect the rights of local communities. According to Wikipedia's FSC entry, FSC certification covers over 160 million hectares of forest globally.

Sustainably managed forests actually enhance carbon sequestration over time. Harvesting mature trees and replanting creates a cycle where the total carbon stored in wood products plus growing forests exceeds the carbon that would be stored in an unharvested old-growth forest. Young, actively growing trees absorb CO2 more rapidly than mature trees whose growth has slowed. The harvested wood locks that carbon into buildings for decades or centuries, while replacement trees begin absorbing new carbon immediately.

Key Takeaway

Timber's carbon credentials are only as strong as the forestry behind them. Always specify FSC or PEFC-certified timber. BIOBUILDS sources all structural timber from certified European forestry operations, ensuring that every home built contributes to a positive carbon cycle rather than depleting forest carbon stocks.

European forest growth

Europe's forests have been growing more than they are harvested for decades. According to data from the European Environment Agency, European forest area has increased by approximately 9% since 1990. Annual timber growth exceeds annual harvest across the EU, meaning the forest carbon sink is expanding even as the timber industry operates. This structural surplus means increased use of timber in construction does not require net deforestation—it simply makes productive use of a renewable resource that is already growing faster than it is consumed.

Factory construction and carbon optimization

Factory-built modular timber homes offer additional carbon advantages beyond the inherent properties of wood itself. The controlled manufacturing environment enables systematic optimization at every stage of the construction process—from material procurement through assembly to waste management.

Material efficiency in factory settings typically exceeds site construction by a significant margin. Precise cutting with CNC machinery minimizes offcuts. Standardized designs reduce the variety of components required. Just-in-time material delivery eliminates on-site storage and associated waste. According to the Modular Building Institute, factory construction reduces material waste by up to 90% compared to conventional site building—waste that would otherwise represent embodied carbon invested in materials that never become part of the building.

Transport emissions benefit from the compact logistics of modular delivery. Instead of dozens of separate material deliveries over months of site construction, a modular home arrives as completed units in a small number of truck journeys. BIOBUILDS' 21-day factory production timeline means the total construction logistics footprint is concentrated and minimizable, rather than spread across months of site activity with repeated deliveries, equipment mobilizations, and worker commuting.

Combining low embodied and operational carbon

The most impactful carbon strategy combines low-embodied-carbon materials with low-operational-carbon design. BIOBUILDS achieves this by pairing timber-frame construction (minimizing embodied carbon) with Passivhaus certification (minimizing operational carbon). The result is a building that starts life with lower material emissions than concrete or steel alternatives and then consumes 90% less heating energy over its operational lifetime.

This dual approach addresses both halves of a building's carbon equation simultaneously. A concrete building with excellent operational performance still carries high embodied carbon. A timber building without energy performance optimization still generates operational emissions over decades. The combination of timber-frame structure and Passivhaus performance creates a whole-life carbon profile that neither approach achieves alone.

Practical implications for homeowners

For families planning to build a home in Romania, Germany, or Austria, the carbon footprint of construction materials may seem abstract compared to immediate concerns like cost, timeline, and floor plans. But the regulatory and market trends described above are translating environmental performance into tangible financial and practical consequences.

Property value and regulatory compliance

As whole-life carbon reporting becomes mandatory under the EPBD, buildings with lower embodied carbon will carry documented environmental credentials that influence property value. The energy performance certificate already affects property prices across Europe; adding embodied carbon disclosure to this framework creates a new dimension of building quality that timber homes inherently satisfy. Green mortgages from the European Energy Efficient Mortgages Initiative already offer preferential terms for high-performance buildings, and embodied carbon criteria are likely to follow.

Cost considerations

Timber-frame construction at BIOBUILDS is priced at €2,200/m² for Passivhaus-certified homes—competitive with or below the EU average of €2,700-3,500/m² for conventional construction. The carbon benefits come at no cost premium. Combined with energy savings of up to 90% over the building's lifetime, timber-frame Passivhaus construction delivers both lower upfront embodied carbon and lower ongoing operational costs than conventional alternatives.

Making an informed choice

When evaluating construction options, consider asking your builder or manufacturer these questions:

  • What is the embodied carbon of the proposed structure? Builders who cannot answer this question are not yet prepared for the regulatory environment taking effect by 2030.
  • Is the timber FSC or PEFC certified? Certification ensures sustainable sourcing. Without it, timber's carbon benefits are unverifiable.
  • What is the whole-life carbon assessment? The best builders can provide this using EN 15978 methodology, covering both embodied and operational carbon over the building's service life.
  • How does factory production minimize waste? Factory-built homes should demonstrate measurably lower material waste than equivalent site-built construction.
