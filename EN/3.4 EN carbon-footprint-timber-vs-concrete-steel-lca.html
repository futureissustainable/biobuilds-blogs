The building and construction sector consumes 32% of global energy and contributes 34% of global CO2 emissions, according to the UNEP Global Status Report 2024/2025. For decades, the industry focused almost exclusively on reducing operational emissions—the energy used for heating, cooling, and lighting. But as buildings become more energy efficient, a different category of emissions is growing in relative importance: the carbon embedded in the materials themselves.

This article examines what life-cycle assessment data actually shows about the carbon footprint of the three dominant structural materials—timber, concrete, and steel. The findings, drawn from dozens of peer-reviewed studies, carry practical implications for anyone building or commissioning a home in Europe, particularly as new EU regulations begin requiring whole-life carbon disclosure.

What is embodied carbon and why it matters now

Embodied carbon refers to the greenhouse gas emissions associated with building materials throughout their entire lifecycle—from raw material extraction and manufacturing through transportation, construction, maintenance, and eventual demolition or recycling. It excludes the operational emissions from heating and cooling the finished building.

The distinction matters more than ever. According to the UNEP 2024/2025 Global Status Report, embodied carbon now represents more than a quarter of the building sector's total emissions—and that share is growing. As operational energy efficiency improves through better insulation, heat pumps, and renewable energy, the relative contribution of materials-stage emissions increases. Projections suggest operational emissions will decline from 75% to 50% of total building emissions in coming decades, making embodied carbon the dominant challenge.

The materials driving these emissions are familiar. Cement manufacturing produces CO2 both from burning fuel to reach the 1,450°C required for clinker production and from the chemical decomposition of limestone itself. Steel production requires temperatures above 1,500°C, typically achieved with coal or natural gas. Together, cement and steel are responsible for 18% of global emissions, according to the UNEP report—more than the entire aviation and shipping industries combined.

Timber occupies a fundamentally different position in this equation. Rather than releasing carbon during manufacturing, wood stores carbon that trees absorbed during growth. This distinction makes the comparison between structural materials not merely a question of which emits less, but whether the material is a carbon source or a carbon sink.

The data: timber vs. concrete vs. steel

Timber construction produces significantly lower embodied carbon than concrete or steel across every major study published to date. A 2025 analysis in the journal Building and Environment quantified embodied carbon intensities for three frame types: 119 kg CO2-eq/m2 for timber frames, 185 kg CO2-eq/m2 for concrete frames, and 228 kg CO2-eq/m2 for steel frames. Timber's embodied carbon was 36% lower than concrete and 48% lower than steel.

These figures are not outliers. A comprehensive review of 62 peer-reviewed articles focused on mass timber construction found that, on average, approximately 43% of greenhouse gas emissions are avoided when reinforced concrete structures are substituted with mass timber alternatives. The consistency of this finding across different geographies, building types, and study methodologies strengthens the conclusion.

A review published in Frontiers in Built Environment compared median embodied energy values across building types and found timber buildings averaged 2.97 GJ/m2 compared to 3.95 GJ/m2 for concrete and 5.53 GJ/m2 for steel. Timber's embodied energy was 28% lower than concrete and 47% lower than steel.

Regional variations

The carbon advantage of timber is consistent across regions, though the magnitude varies. A study examining CLT buildings in cold climates found a 40% reduction in emissions compared to concrete buildings from cradle to grave. Research on hybrid CLT-concrete structures showed an average 26.5% decrease in global warming potential even when timber replaced only part of the structural system.

These regional differences reflect variations in electricity grids (which affect manufacturing emissions), transportation distances, and local forestry practices. European timber construction tends to perform particularly well because forestry management practices across the EU ensure sustainable sourcing, and shorter transport distances to construction sites keep logistics emissions low.

Carbon storage: timber's unique advantage

Beyond producing fewer emissions during manufacturing, timber does something no other structural material can: it stores carbon. Trees absorb CO2 from the atmosphere during growth through photosynthesis, converting it to organic carbon compounds that form the wood itself. When that wood becomes a building, the carbon remains stored rather than returning to the atmosphere.

Each cubic metre of wood contains approximately 1 tonne of sequestered CO2 equivalent. A five-storey residential building structured in laminated timber stores up to 180 kg of carbon per square metre of floor area—three times the above-ground carbon density of natural high-density forests. A case study of Adohi Hall, a mass timber building at the University of Arkansas, found approximately 2,757 tonnes of CO2 equivalent stored within its structure.

This storage function transforms the carbon accounting of timber buildings. While concrete and steel structures can only minimize their emissions, a timber building can potentially be carbon negative—storing more carbon within its structure than was emitted during its entire lifecycle. The BIOBUILDS homepage cites savings of 337 tonnes of CO2 per house built, reflecting this combined effect of lower manufacturing emissions and active carbon storage.

The construction sector could store more carbon in mass timber than it emits, dramatically shifting the industry's climate impact from source to sink.

The substitution effect

Carbon accounting for timber buildings involves two distinct benefits. The storage effect counts the carbon physically contained in the wood. The substitution effect counts the emissions avoided by not using concrete or steel. Both are legitimate and additive.

When forests are sustainably managed—harvested and replanted on a continuous cycle—the total carbon stored in wood products plus growing forests exceeds the carbon that would be stored in an unharvested old-growth forest. The key requirement is that forest area and biodiversity are maintained, a standard met by FSC and PEFC certification programmes operating across Europe. PEFC-certified forests covered over 280 million hectares worldwide as of 2024.

How life-cycle assessments work

Life-cycle assessment (LCA) is the standardised methodology for comparing the environmental impact of building materials across their entire existence. The European standard EN 15978 defines the framework, dividing a building's lifecycle into distinct stages that are assessed individually and in aggregate.

Understanding LCA stages matters because the boundaries drawn around an assessment significantly affect results. The stages are:

  1. A1-A3 (Product stage): Raw material extraction, transport to factory, and manufacturing. This is where cement and steel generate most of their emissions.
  2. A4-A5 (Construction stage): Transport to site and installation. Factory-built modular homes have lower A4-A5 emissions because components arrive pre-assembled.
  3. B1-B7 (Use stage): Maintenance, repair, replacement, and operational energy over the building's reference service period.
  4. C1-C4 (End-of-life stage): Demolition, waste processing, and disposal. Timber can be recycled, reused, or recovered for energy.
  5. D (Beyond lifecycle): Benefits from reuse, recovery, and recycling. Timber burned for energy displaces fossil fuels; recycled steel avoids virgin production.
Key Takeaway

Most published LCA comparisons focus on stages A1-A3 (manufacturing), where timber's advantage is largest. When the full lifecycle is assessed including end-of-life scenarios, timber maintains its lead—particularly when wood waste is recovered for energy, achieving over 50% lower primary energy input than concrete equivalents.

The EU's Level(s) framework provides a standardised approach to building sustainability assessment, with Indicator 1.2 specifically covering life-cycle global warming potential. This framework is now referenced directly in the revised EPBD as the basis for mandatory carbon reporting—making LCA methodology not just an academic exercise but a regulatory compliance requirement.

EU regulation: the EPBD and whole-life carbon

The revised Energy Performance of Buildings Directive (Directive 2024/1275) marks the first time EU law regulates embodied carbon alongside operational energy performance. The requirements phase in over several years, but the direction is unambiguous: whole-life carbon accounting is becoming mandatory across Europe.

The timeline for compliance is as follows:

  • 2028: Whole-life carbon must be disclosed through Energy Performance Certificates for all new buildings over 1,000 m2
  • 2030: All new buildings must disclose both embodied and operational carbon; member states must set progressively declining whole-life carbon targets
  • 2030: All new buildings must be zero-emission buildings (ZEBs) — producing no on-site emissions

In December 2025, the European Commission published a delegated regulation establishing a Union framework for calculating life-cycle global warming potential, based on EN 15978 and the Level(s) framework. This harmonises how member states measure and report building carbon, creating a level playing field across the EU.

For homebuilders, the practical implication is clear. Buildings constructed with high-embodied-carbon materials will face increasingly stringent targets that may require costly offsets or design modifications. Buildings constructed with timber start from a fundamentally lower carbon baseline and benefit from carbon storage credits that other materials cannot claim. Choosing timber today positions a building favourably for regulations that are already written into law.

National implementation

EU member states must transpose the EPBD requirements into national law, with some flexibility in setting specific thresholds. Several countries are moving faster than the minimum timeline. France's RE2020 regulation already limits embodied carbon in new buildings, with targets decreasing every three years. The Netherlands requires Environmental Performance Assessments for all new buildings. Germany's Qualitätssiegel Nachhaltiges Gebäude (QNG) sustainability seal includes whole-life carbon criteria linked to KfW funding programmes.

These early movers demonstrate that the EPBD's 2030 deadline is not the starting point but rather the catch-up date. Builders in Germany, France, and the Netherlands are already working within embodied carbon constraints. In these markets, timber's carbon advantage translates directly into simpler compliance and access to green building incentives.

Important caveats and nuances

The data consistently favours timber, but honest analysis requires acknowledging the factors that affect the magnitude of the advantage. Several methodological choices significantly influence LCA results, and understanding them prevents oversimplification.

Biogenic carbon accounting

The most significant variable is how studies treat biogenic carbon—the CO2 absorbed by trees during growth. Some LCA methodologies count this stored carbon as a benefit at the point of construction, giving timber a large negative emission credit. Others treat biogenic carbon as climate-neutral over long timeframes, reducing timber's apparent advantage. The approach matters: the most pronounced variation in LCA databases is observed in wood products, where reported emissions span a range of nearly 5,000 kg CO2-eq/m3 depending on the database used.

The European ÖKOBAUDAT database reports negative embodied carbon values for some timber products (reflecting carbon storage), while Australia's EPiC database reports positive values for similar products. Neither approach is wrong—they represent different accounting conventions with different temporal boundaries.

End-of-life assumptions

What happens to building materials at the end of their service life significantly affects lifecycle emissions. A 2025 study published in Building and Environment found that glulam had the lowest global warming potential when modelled with incineration and energy recovery at end of life. However, when recycling was modelled according to current standards, glulam's GWP values fell into the same range as steel, eliminating the clear ranking between materials.

This sensitivity to end-of-life scenarios underscores the importance of designing timber buildings for eventual disassembly and material recovery—a consideration that factory-built modular construction addresses well, since modules can be separated and components recovered more easily than monolithic poured-concrete structures.

Recycled steel narrows the gap

When recycled steel replaces virgin production, the embodied carbon of steel structures drops substantially. Electric arc furnace steelmaking using scrap metal produces roughly 75% less CO2 than primary steelmaking. As global recycled steel use increases, the carbon gap between timber and steel will narrow for the structural frame—though timber retains its unique carbon storage benefit regardless of what happens to steel recycling rates.

Key Takeaway

Timber's carbon advantage over concrete and steel is robust across methodologies, but the magnitude depends on biogenic carbon accounting, end-of-life assumptions, and the recycled content of competing materials. The most conservative comparisons still show timber 17-25% lower in embodied carbon; the most favourable show reductions exceeding 45%. Carbon storage remains a unique and unambiguous timber advantage under any methodology.

Global carbon reduction potential

The individual building comparison scales dramatically when projected across global construction. The world added an estimated 5 billion square metres of new floor space in 2023, according to the UNEP, with building stock expected to double by 2050. The material choices for this construction wave will lock in emissions—or carbon storage—for decades.

Research published in Nature Communications modelled scenarios where timber construction scales globally. If 90% of new urban residents were housed in mid-rise timber buildings, approximately 106 gigatonnes of CO2 could be saved by 2100. A more conservative scenario with 30-60% timber adoption still achieves 25.6-39 gigatonnes of emission reductions—roughly equivalent to one full year of total global energy-related CO2 emissions.

European forests have been growing steadily for decades—the EU's forest area increased by 10% between 1990 and 2020—demonstrating that increased timber harvesting for construction is compatible with expanding forest cover when managed responsibly. What this means for homebuilders

The data carries practical implications for families and developers choosing between building systems in 2026 and beyond. Three factors converge to make timber construction's carbon advantage increasingly relevant to individual building decisions.

Regulatory trajectory

The EU's EPBD timeline means every new building will face whole-life carbon scrutiny by 2030. Germany's QNG sustainability seal already links embodied carbon performance to access to favourable KfW financing. Austria's klimaaktiv programme rewards low-carbon construction with certification that supports planning approval. Romania's alignment with EU-harmonised building standards means these requirements will apply equally across all three markets. Building with timber today means building ahead of requirements that will only tighten.

Financial implications

As carbon pricing extends to construction materials through mechanisms like the EU's Carbon Border Adjustment Mechanism (CBAM), the cost of high-embodied-carbon materials will increase relative to timber. Green mortgage programmes across Europe already offer preferential rates for buildings that demonstrate superior environmental performance. The European Energy Efficient Mortgage Initiative provides a framework for lenders to reward low-carbon construction with better financing terms.

Resale value and future-proofing

Buildings that perform well under whole-life carbon assessment will carry higher Energy Performance Certificate ratings as these expand to include embodied carbon. Research consistently shows that higher EPC ratings correlate with property value premiums. A timber home that stores carbon and produces minimal manufacturing emissions is positioned for the highest possible rating under the expanded assessment framework.

The sector is dependent on materials like cement and steel that are responsible for 18% of global emissions and are a major source of construction waste. Solutions to mitigate embodied carbon emissions have lagged behind operational energy improvements.

For those building a home in Europe today, the choice of structural material is no longer just a question of cost and performance. It is increasingly a question of carbon—one where timber provides an answer that no other material can match.
