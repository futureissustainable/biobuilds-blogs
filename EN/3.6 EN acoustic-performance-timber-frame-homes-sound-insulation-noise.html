One of the most persistent concerns about timber-frame homes is noise. The assumption runs deep: wood is lighter than concrete, so it must transmit more sound. People picture every footstep reverberating through the floor, every conversation leaking through the walls. The concern is understandable—but the engineering reality is substantially more nuanced, and in many cases the opposite of what intuition suggests.

Modern timber construction achieves sound insulation that meets and often exceeds the same European building codes applied to concrete and masonry buildings. The key lies not in any single material property but in how timber assemblies are engineered as multi-layer systems. And one factor that rarely enters the public conversation is perhaps the most significant: the exceptional airtightness of Passivhaus-certified homes eliminates the air leakage pathways responsible for most sound transmission in conventional buildings. Airtight buildings are, by physics, quieter buildings.

How sound travels through buildings

Sound reaches the interior of a building through two fundamentally different mechanisms, and understanding this distinction is essential for evaluating any construction material's acoustic performance.

Airborne sound travels as pressure waves through the air—voices, music, traffic noise, barking dogs. When these waves hit a wall or floor, they cause the surface to vibrate, which re-radiates the sound on the other side. The effectiveness of a barrier against airborne sound depends primarily on its mass, stiffness, and continuity. Heavier, denser materials vibrate less when struck by sound waves, transmitting less energy to the other side. This is the mass law of acoustics: for every doubling of a surface's mass per unit area, its sound insulation increases by approximately 6 dB, according to acoustic engineering principles documented by STEICO's acoustic engineering resources.

Impact sound is generated when an object physically strikes a building element—footsteps on a floor, furniture being moved, children bouncing balls. The vibration transmits directly through the structure rather than through the air, making it harder to control. Impact sound is the greater challenge in lightweight construction because the structural elements vibrate more readily than heavier materials would.

A third pathway is often overlooked: flanking transmission, where sound bypasses a wall or floor by travelling through connected structural elements—along a continuous floor slab, through shared framing, or through gaps in the building envelope. In conventional construction, flanking paths and air leakage account for a substantial portion of total sound transmission. This is where the quality of construction detailing matters as much as the materials themselves—and where factory-built modular homes hold a distinct advantage over site-built alternatives.

Timber vs concrete vs steel: acoustic comparison

The comparison between timber and concrete in acoustic performance is more complex than a simple "heavier is better" conclusion. While concrete undeniably has greater mass—a 150 mm concrete slab weighs approximately 360 kg/m² compared to about 80 kg/m² for a comparable CLT panel—mass is only one factor in a building's acoustic equation.

Research published in PMC (National Library of Medicine) tested the sound insulation of various CLT configurations. A single 120 mm CLT wall achieved a weighted sound reduction index (Rw) of 45 dB. By comparison, 150–180 mm of concrete wall achieves approximately 50 dB. The gap exists—but it closes rapidly with standard construction layers.

When the same researchers tested CLT walls with gypsum board and insulation cavities, sound insulation rose to 49–57 dB depending on configuration. A CLT panel with double gypsum board layers and 50 mm glass wool insulation achieved 57 dB—exceeding the bare concrete benchmark by 7 dB. All double-wall CLT configurations tested achieved at least 50 dB, meeting the International Building Code's minimum requirement for party walls.

There is one area where timber holds a clear acoustic advantage: sound absorption. Concrete and steel surfaces are acoustically reflective—sound bounces off them, creating echo and reverberation that makes spaces feel louder than they actually are. Wood, by contrast, absorbs sound energy. According to Naturally:Wood, timber is "often chosen over steel, concrete and glass" for concert halls, classrooms, and lecture theatres precisely because of this superior sound absorption. The organic cellular structure of wood dissipates sound energy rather than reflecting it, creating interiors that feel quieter and more comfortable even at equivalent insulation ratings.

As with any material, delivering pleasing acoustics in wood buildings requires careful considerations to reduce vibrations and sound transmission. With proper design, occupants can enjoy the same privacy and acoustic performance in wood structures as they have come to expect from steel and concrete buildings.

How timber homes achieve excellent sound insulation

Timber construction compensates for lower inherent mass through three engineering strategies that, when combined, create acoustic performance equal to or exceeding heavier construction types. These strategies are well-established in building science and codified in European standards.

Adding mass

The most direct approach is increasing the total mass of the assembly. In timber floor systems, this typically means adding a concrete or gypsum-based topping of 25–75 mm over the timber deck. According to the WoodWorks acoustic design guide, almost all mass timber buildings include additional material layers specifically for acoustic performance. A concrete topping adds approximately 50–180 kg/m² depending on thickness, dramatically increasing airborne sound insulation.

For walls, additional layers of gypsum plasterboard serve the same function. Each 12.5 mm layer of gypsum board adds approximately 9 kg/m². The PMC research found that moving from single to double gypsum board increased sound insulation by 4–8 dB—a difference clearly perceptible to the human ear.

Decoupling

Decoupling is the most powerful acoustic strategy available to lightweight construction. The principle is simple: if two surfaces are not physically connected, vibration cannot travel directly between them. In practice, this means separating the structural timber from the finished surfaces using resilient channels, isolation mats, staggered studs, or independent frames.

Research documented by the WoodWorks acoustic guide shows that in mass timber floor/ceiling systems, the most common decoupling products are underlayments and mats placed between the timber panel and the topping layer. For walls, isolated double-stud configurations with an air gap between the two wall frames break the structural connection entirely. This approach was successfully implemented in the Wood Innovation and Design Centre in British Columbia, which uses isolated double-stud walls throughout to achieve excellent room-to-room sound control in a full timber structure.

Absorption

Filling cavities within wall and floor assemblies with sound-absorbing insulation reduces sound transmission by converting acoustic energy into heat through friction as sound waves pass through the fibrous material. Dense insulation materials like mineral wool or wood fibre are particularly effective. According to acoustic testing data, sound batts can improve sound transmission class ratings by 4–10 dB when added to timber wall cavities.

The combination of mass, decoupling, and absorption creates what acoustic engineers call a mass-spring-mass system: two dense layers (mass) separated by a resilient, insulation-filled gap (spring). This system is far more effective than a single heavy layer of equivalent total weight. A lightweight timber wall with decoupled gypsum board and insulation cavity can outperform a solid masonry wall of greater total mass—a result that consistently surprises people accustomed to equating weight with sound insulation.

Key Takeaway

Timber construction achieves competitive sound insulation not despite its lower mass but through intelligent engineering. The mass-spring-mass principle—where two dense layers are separated by an insulation-filled, decoupled air gap—outperforms a single heavy barrier of equivalent weight. This is why properly engineered timber walls achieve 50–57 dB, exceeding bare concrete's approximately 50 dB.

European acoustic standards and what they require

Sound insulation requirements for residential buildings in Europe are governed by national building regulations that reference the ISO 717 standard series. ISO 717-1 covers airborne sound insulation (using the weighted sound reduction index, Rw), while ISO 717-2 covers impact sound insulation (using the weighted normalized impact sound pressure level, L'n,w). These standards provide the rating methodology; the specific minimum values are set by each country.

Germany's DIN 4109 is among the most comprehensive national acoustic standards in Europe. According to DIN 4109 research published in 2025, the current requirements (DIN 4109-1:2018) specify:

Building Element Parameter Minimum Requirement
Apartment-separating walls R'w ≥ 53 dB
Apartment-separating floors R'w ≥ 54 dB
Terraced/semi-detached walls (ground floor) R'w ≥ 59 dB
Terraced/semi-detached walls (upper floors) R'w ≥ 62 dB
Apartment floors (impact sound) L'n,w ≤ 50 dB
Indoor noise from facades (day) — ≤ 35 dB(A)
Indoor noise from facades (night) — ≤ 25 dB(A)

These requirements apply equally to all construction types—concrete, masonry, steel, and timber. The building code does not prescribe materials; it prescribes performance. A timber-frame wall assembly that achieves Rw 53 dB is just as compliant as a concrete wall that achieves the same rating. The DIN 4109 revision currently underway (as of 2025) will introduce spectrum adaptation terms C and Ctr to better capture low-frequency performance, a development particularly relevant to lightweight construction.

Enhanced comfort standards like DIN 4109-5 and VDI 4100 recommend higher values for occupants who want noticeably better sound protection. For single-family timber-frame homes—where all rooms belong to the same household—the multi-family acoustic requirements don't apply. However, internal acoustic comfort remains important: nobody wants to hear conversations from the bedroom while sitting in the living room.

Impact sound: solving the footstep problem

Impact sound is the area where timber construction faces its greatest acoustic challenge. When someone walks across a lightweight timber floor, the impact energy transmits through the structure more readily than it would through a heavy concrete slab. The DIN 4109 requirement for apartment-separating floors is L'n,w ≤ 50 dB—a value that bare timber floors cannot achieve alone.

The solution involves floating floor systems. A floating floor sits on an elastic layer—rubber mat, cork, or wood fibre underlayment—that absorbs impact energy before it reaches the structural timber beneath. Research documented in acoustic engineering literature shows that floating floors can increase timber structures' sound absorption by up to 22.2 dB, providing more than enough improvement to meet and exceed code requirements.

The specific composition of the floating floor system determines its effectiveness. A lightweight concrete or gypsum screed of 40–60 mm over an elastic interlayer on the timber deck provides mass to resist impact vibration while the elastic layer prevents that vibration from reaching the structure. For residential applications where a thinner buildup is preferred, engineered underlayment systems achieve substantial improvements with minimal additional height.

Soft floor finishes

The floor covering itself contributes meaningfully to impact sound control. Carpet absorbs a significant portion of impact energy at the point of contact. Hard floor finishes like tile, stone, or hardwood transmit more impact energy to the structure below, but this can be compensated through thicker underlayment or more effective decoupling layers. The choice of floor finish is ultimately a design decision balanced against acoustic performance—both approaches can achieve code compliance with appropriate engineering.

Wood fibre insulation: acoustic and thermal in one

One of the most significant advantages of timber-frame construction for acoustic performance is the use of wood fibre insulation. Unlike mineral wool or polystyrene, wood fibre insulation combines thermal performance with notably high density, making it effective for both energy efficiency and sound control in a single material layer.

STEICO, one of BIOBUILDS' specified material partners, produces wood fibre insulation products with particularly high raw density. According to STEICO's technical documentation, their products combine high bulk density with low thermal conductivity, supporting both energy efficiency and noise protection simultaneously. The fibrous structure and elevated gross density of STEICO insulating materials reduce both external airborne sound (traffic, weather) and internal structure-borne sound (footsteps, vibrations).

Due to many pores in the boards and in the wood structure itself, wood fibreboards function as highly efficient sound-absorbing materials. When combined with harder gypsum boards in a wall or floor assembly, the combination of absorption (wood fibre) and mass (gypsum) creates an effective barrier against both airborne and impact sound transmission.

The Passivhaus acoustic advantage

The most underappreciated factor in timber-frame acoustic performance is the co-benefit of Passivhaus certification. The same design principles that achieve 90% energy savings create homes that are dramatically quieter than conventional construction—a benefit that acoustic specialists have documented but that rarely features in marketing.

Airtightness is acoustic tightness. A Passivhaus-certified building achieves air change rates of 0.6 ACH at 50 Pa pressure—roughly 10 times tighter than standard building regulations require, according to the Passive House Institute. Every gap in a building envelope that allows air to leak is also a pathway for sound to enter. Conventional construction is riddled with these pathways: gaps around window frames, service penetrations, junctions between walls and floors, poorly sealed electrical outlets. Passivhaus construction methodically eliminates every one of these gaps through continuous air barrier systems and meticulous sealing at every junction.

The acoustic impact is measurable. According to research from Pure Haus, Passivhaus buildings demonstrate acoustic performance approximately 20 times better than standard construction in terms of air leakage paths. Studies in educational settings found that Passivhaus classrooms, with their superior sound insulation, create calm, distraction-free spaces that improve student concentration and academic outcomes.

Triple-glazed windows specified for Passivhaus certification provide substantially better sound insulation than the double glazing common in conventional construction. The three glass panes with two insulating gas-filled cavities create multiple mass-spring-mass barriers that reduce both airborne sound from traffic and neighbourhood noise. Window airtightness research published in ScienceDirect (Construction and Building Materials) found that improved airtight window systems provide measurably better noise barriers, with differences of 2–5 dB between standard and high-performance window types.

Continuous insulation wrapping the entire building envelope—walls, roof, and floor—creates an unbroken thermal and acoustic barrier. The thick insulation layers required by Passivhaus standards (typically 200–400 mm depending on climate zone) provide substantial mass and absorption that dampen external noise transmission. STEICO wood fibre insulation, with its combination of high density and excellent acoustic absorption, serves double duty in every BIOBUILDS home.

Thermal bridge-free construction eliminates the structural short-circuits where continuous framing connects interior and exterior surfaces. These same structural connections are acoustic bridges—pathways for vibration to travel from outside to inside. By eliminating thermal bridges, Passivhaus design simultaneously eliminates acoustic bridges, reducing flanking sound transmission through the building envelope.

The same materials that make a home energy-efficient—such as airtight seals, thick insulation, and high-performance windows—can also enhance its acoustic performance. Airtight buildings tend to be quieter due to the disruption of sound transmission pathways.

Factory construction and acoustic quality control

Acoustic performance in buildings depends critically on execution quality. A wall assembly that achieves Rw 55 dB in laboratory testing can perform 5–10 dB worse when built on-site if installation details are imperfect. Gaps in insulation, incomplete sealing, inconsistent fastening, or misaligned framing all create acoustic weak points that degrade real-world performance below tested performance.

This is where factory-built modular construction provides a measurable acoustic advantage. BIOBUILDS' 21-day factory production process means every acoustic-critical detail is executed under controlled conditions:

  • Insulation continuity: Wood fibre insulation boards are cut and fitted with CNC precision, eliminating the gaps and compressions that degrade acoustic performance in site-installed insulation.
  • Airtight membrane integrity: Pro Clima airtightness membranes are installed in factory conditions without wind, rain, or site contamination, ensuring the continuous air barrier that prevents sound leakage.
  • Gypsum board installation: Board joints are taped and sealed systematically, eliminating the inconsistencies that create acoustic weak points in on-site finishing.
  • Quality inspection at every stage: Acoustic-critical details are verified during production rather than relying on periodic site inspections after the fact.
  • No weather exposure: Timber modules are never left exposed to rain during construction, preventing moisture absorption that can cause warping, gaps, and subsequent acoustic degradation over time.

The result is that the acoustic performance measured in laboratory testing more closely matches real-world performance in a factory-built home than in a site-built equivalent. This gap between tested and installed performance—sometimes called the "performance gap"—is one of the most significant and least discussed advantages of modular construction.

Module-to-module junctions

One acoustic detail unique to modular construction is the junction between modules. When two factory-built modules are joined on-site, the connection must maintain the acoustic integrity of the overall assembly. BIOBUILDS addresses this through compressible sealing strips at module interfaces, continuous insulation layers that bridge the junction, and airtight membrane overlaps sealed in accordance with Pro Clima's connection protocols. These junction details are engineered specifically for acoustic and airtight performance, not improvised on-site.

Key Takeaway

The gap between laboratory-tested acoustic ratings and real-world performance is one of the largest quality challenges in construction. Factory-built modular homes close this gap by executing every acoustic-critical detail under controlled conditions with systematic quality inspection. The result: acoustic performance in a BIOBUILDS home more closely matches the design intent than conventional site-built alternatives.

The assumption that timber-frame homes are acoustically inferior is a legacy of older, simpler construction methods that used single-layer timber panels without acoustic engineering. Modern timber construction tells a different story. Properly designed timber wall assemblies achieve Rw 50–57 dB—meeting and exceeding European building code requirements that apply equally to all construction types. Wood absorbs sound better than concrete or steel, reducing echo and creating more comfortable interiors. And Passivhaus certification provides an acoustic co-benefit that is arguably as valuable as the energy savings: a building envelope so airtight that sound simply cannot find a path inside.

BIOBUILDS homes combine all of these advantages: engineered timber assemblies with multi-layer acoustic design, STEICO wood fibre insulation that delivers thermal and acoustic performance simultaneously, Passivhaus-certified airtightness that eliminates sound leakage, and factory-controlled construction quality that ensures the designed acoustic performance is actually delivered.
