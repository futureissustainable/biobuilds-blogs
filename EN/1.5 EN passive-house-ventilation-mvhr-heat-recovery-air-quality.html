Ventilation is the most misunderstood component of a Passive House. People assume an airtight building means stuffy rooms and sealed windows. The reality is the opposite: a certified Passive House delivers better air quality than any conventionally built home — not despite the airtightness, but because of it. The key is MVHR, mechanical ventilation with heat recovery, and it changes everything about how fresh air enters your home.

We've installed and commissioned MVHR systems in every BIOBUILDS home we've built. In our experience, it's the feature that surprises homeowners most — and the one they mention first when describing what makes their home feel different. This guide explains exactly how these systems work, what they cost, and why the Passive House Institute makes them a non-negotiable requirement.

Why ventilation matters in airtight buildings

Every building needs fresh air. The question is how that air gets in. In conventional construction, it enters through gaps in the building envelope — around windows, through wall cracks, via poorly sealed service penetrations. This uncontrolled air leakage is responsible for 30–40% of heating energy losses in residential buildings, according to research published in the journal Energies (2024).

A Passive House eliminates that leakage by achieving an airtightness of ≤0.6 air changes per hour at 50 Pascals — verified through blower door testing. But removing uncontrolled leakage also removes the accidental ventilation that conventional buildings rely on. Without a deliberate replacement, CO₂ would accumulate, moisture would build up, and indoor air quality would deteriorate rapidly.

MVHR solves this by providing controlled, continuous, filtered ventilation. Air enters the building exactly where you want it, at the temperature you want it, filtered to remove pollutants and allergens. It's the difference between leaving your front door ajar in a storm and installing a climate-controlled airlock.

How an MVHR system works

An MVHR unit is a compact appliance, typically the size of a large boiler, installed in a utility room or cupboard. It performs a continuously balanced exchange: extracting stale, humid air from kitchens and bathrooms while simultaneously supplying fresh, filtered outdoor air to living rooms and bedrooms. The two airstreams never mix.

The heat exchanger: where the efficiency happens

At the heart of every MVHR unit is a heat exchanger — a device that transfers thermal energy from the outgoing warm exhaust air to the incoming cold fresh air. In winter, this means fresh air arriving at -5°C outside can be preheated to approximately 16°C or higher before it enters your living space, using only the heat that would otherwise be lost.

The two airstreams pass through thin, thermally conductive plates arranged in a counterflow or crossflow configuration. Heat transfers through the plates, but air does not. There is no recirculation — every breath you take is fresh outdoor air, simply warmed by the outgoing exhaust.

The air distribution network

A network of rigid steel or semi-rigid ducts connects the MVHR unit to supply valves in living spaces and extract valves in wet rooms. The system uses a cascade principle: air flows from supply rooms (bedrooms, living rooms) through transfer openings above doors into extract rooms (kitchen, bathrooms, WC). This creates a one-directional airflow that moves moisture and odors away from occupied spaces.

Properly designed ductwork has a lifespan of 30+ years and requires no replacement under normal conditions. At BIOBUILDS, we install the complete duct network during factory production, ensuring precise routing and sealed connections that would be difficult to achieve on a conventional construction site.

Supply and extract: the balanced flow

A well-designed MVHR system achieves balanced mass flow — the volume of air supplied equals the volume extracted, within a tolerance of less than 10% for Passive House certification. Modern systems typically achieve less than 3% imbalance. This balance prevents pressure differences that could force unconditioned air through the building envelope or create drafts.

Passive House Institute ventilation requirements

The Passive House Institute sets specific performance thresholds that any MVHR system must meet for use in a certified building. These aren't guidelines — they're pass/fail criteria verified during the certification process.

Heat recovery efficiency: ≥75%

The minimum heat recovery rate is 75%, measured as exhaust-side efficiency. This means at least 75% of the thermal energy in the outgoing air is transferred to the incoming fresh air. PHI uses exhaust efficiency (rather than supply efficiency) because the MVHR is considered part of the thermal envelope — it's the heat losses through the unit that matter.

In practice, most PHI-certified units now achieve 85–95% heat recovery. According to analysis by architect Elrond Burrell, a Passivhaus-certified MVHR with 80% efficiency can achieve a coefficient of performance (COP) of 15 — meaning for every unit of electrical energy consumed by the fans, 15 units of heat energy are recovered. This makes MVHR one of the most energy-efficient technologies in residential construction.

Specific fan power: ≤0.45 Wh/m³

The fans that move air through the system must consume no more than 0.45 Wh per cubic metre of transported air. This ceiling ensures the system's electrical consumption doesn't undermine its energy-saving purpose. Many modern units operate below 0.25 Wh/m³, meaning the fans consume less energy than a single LED light bulb.

Noise levels: ≤25 dB

At supply and extract valves, noise must not exceed 25 dB — quieter than a whisper, which registers at approximately 30 dB. The main unit generates more noise, which is why it should be installed in a utility space with acoustic separation from living areas. Duct-mounted silencers further attenuate any residual sound transmission.

Supply air temperature: ≥16°C

Fresh air must enter living spaces at a minimum of 16°C year-round. Below this threshold, occupants perceive the incoming air as a draft, even at low flow rates. In the coldest climates, this may require a small pre-heater (electric or hydronic) upstream of the supply duct — but in most Central European locations, an efficient heat exchanger alone achieves this target.

Non-Certified Units

If a non-PHI-certified MVHR unit is used in a Passive House project, the Passive House Institute applies a 12% penalty to the stated efficiency in PHPP calculations. This penalty accounts for the uncertainty in manufacturer-reported data that hasn't been independently verified. Using certified components avoids this deduction and simplifies the certification process.

Indoor air quality: the health case for MVHR

The air quality argument for MVHR is at least as compelling as the energy argument — possibly more so. According to the World Health Organization's Housing and Health Guidelines (2018), indoor air in poorly ventilated homes can contain pollutant concentrations 2–12 times higher than outdoor air. The WHO has declared clean indoor air a fundamental human right.

CO₂ control

In naturally ventilated homes, CO₂ levels frequently exceed 1,600 ppm in bedrooms overnight — well above the 1,000 ppm threshold associated with cognitive impairment and reduced sleep quality. Research published in MDPI Energies (2024) found that mechanical ventilation with heat recovery maintained average CO₂ concentrations at approximately 700 ppm — well within optimal ranges for human health and cognitive function.

Studies in schools show that doubling ventilation rates improves student performance by approximately 15%, according to research cited by the European Ventilation Industry Association (EVIA). The same principle applies in homes: better air, better sleep, better concentration.

Filtration: pollen, dust and particulates

Every MVHR system includes intake filters. For Passive House applications, the standard is an F7 fine filter on the supply side, which captures pollen, fine dust, mold spores, and particulates down to 1 micron. This is particularly significant for allergy sufferers — in a Passive House, indoor pollen counts are dramatically lower than in any naturally ventilated building, regardless of the season.

A coarser G4 filter on the extract side protects the heat exchanger from kitchen grease and household dust. Together, these filters create a building that actively cleans the air entering it — something no amount of window-opening can achieve in urban or high-pollen environments.

Moisture management

Continuous extraction from bathrooms and kitchens — the primary sources of indoor moisture — prevents humidity from migrating into wall and roof assemblies where it could cause condensation, mold growth, and structural damage. The MVHR system maintains indoor relative humidity within the 40–60% range recommended by the Harvard T.H. Chan School of Public Health's Healthy Buildings Program for optimal respiratory health and minimal microbial growth.

"Improving indoor air quality by increasing ventilation can result in increased work performance, reduced absenteeism, and reduced illness. Measures to increase ventilation can be highly cost effective, with substantial net benefits."

Energy savings and heat recovery performance

Without heat recovery, ventilating a well-insulated building to the required air exchange rate would consume approximately 24 kWh/m² per year in heating energy — more than the entire 15 kWh/m²a heating demand allowed under the Passive House standard. This is precisely why MVHR is non-negotiable: without it, the Passive House energy target is mathematically impossible to meet while maintaining healthy ventilation rates.

Real-world heating cost reductions

The European Commission's assessment of ventilation unit energy performance projects that heat recovery from residential ventilation will save 40 TWh of final energy across the EU by 2030, rising to 61 TWh by 2050. At the household level, MVHR typically reduces heating bills by 20–30% compared to buildings with equivalent insulation but only extract-only or natural ventilation.

A study of residential buildings in the Basque Country demonstrated ventilation heat recovery efficiency of 89%, while Swedish research documented 74% energy reduction for ventilation-related heat losses when using a heat exchanger versus no recovery. These figures align with our operational experience at BIOBUILDS, where our Passivhaus-certified homes consistently achieve heating demands below 15 kWh/m²a with MVHR as the primary ventilation strategy.

The electricity equation

Running an MVHR system continuously costs approximately €30–50 per year in electricity. The Passive House Plus guide to HRV reports annual electrical costs of £40–50 for a medium-sized unit operating year-round. Meanwhile, the heat energy recovered by the system — which would otherwise be lost through exhaust — typically exceeds the electrical input by a factor of 10–15, making MVHR a net energy saver for almost every hour of the heating season.

MVHR costs: installation, running and maintenance

A complete residential MVHR installation — including the unit, ductwork, controls, and professional commissioning — costs between €3,500 and €7,000 for a typical new-build home. The range depends on building size, system specification, and whether the system is PHI-certified.

Cost breakdown

Based on 2025 market pricing across the UK, Ireland, and Central Europe:

  • MVHR unit: €1,500–€4,000 depending on capacity and certification level
  • Ductwork and fittings: €800–€1,200 depending on building layout
  • Design and engineering: €450–€700 for system layout and specification
  • Electrical connection: €300–€600
  • Commissioning and balancing: €400–€800

Retrofit installations cost more — typically 20–40% above new-build pricing — due to the complexity of routing ducts through existing structures. In factory-built modular homes like BIOBUILDS projects, the ductwork is installed during production, reducing on-site labor and ensuring optimal duct routing.

Annual running costs

  • Electricity: €30–50 per year for continuous operation
  • Filter replacement: €25–40 per year (changed every 6–12 months)
  • Total annual cost: approximately €55–90 per year

Return on investment

With heating cost savings of 20–30% and annual running costs under €90, a €5,000 MVHR system in a typical 120m² home typically reaches payback within 8–12 years through reduced heating bills alone — without accounting for the health benefits, improved comfort, or potential property value premium. According to the European Energy Efficient Mortgages Initiative, energy-efficient features including ventilation systems contribute to measurable increases in property valuations.

Maintenance and filter replacement

MVHR systems are low-maintenance by design, but they're not zero-maintenance. A well-maintained system delivers consistent performance for decades. A neglected system loses efficiency, increases energy consumption, and can compromise air quality.

Filter changes: the essential task

Filters should be replaced every 6–12 months, depending on local air quality and pollen levels. The process is straightforward — most units feature tool-free filter access panels — and takes under five minutes. Filter cost is approximately €25–40 per year for a standard two-filter set (G4 extract + F7 supply).

Seasonal guidance:

  • High-pollen areas: Replace supply filter every 6 months (spring and autumn)
  • Urban locations with high particulate levels: Check quarterly, replace as needed
  • Rural areas with clean air: Annual replacement is often sufficient

Annual inspection

Once per year, inspect the heat exchanger for dust buildup and clean if necessary. Most modern heat exchangers can be rinsed under a tap or cleaned in a dishwasher. Check that condensate drainage is clear — the heat exchanger produces condensation in winter as the outgoing warm, moist air meets the cold incoming air.

Professional commissioning check

Every 3–5 years, a ventilation specialist should verify airflow rates, check system balance (supply vs. extract volumes), inspect ductwork connections for leaks, and ensure the unit is operating within specification. This commissioning check typically costs €150–€300 and ensures the system continues to meet its design performance.

Common myths about Passive House ventilation

In eight years of consulting with families building Passive Houses, we encounter the same concerns repeatedly. Here are the most common — and why they don't hold up.

Myth 1: "You can't open windows in a Passive House"

False. You can open every window in a Passive House whenever you choose. The MVHR system ensures you don't need to open windows for fresh air, but nothing prevents it. In summer, many Passive House residents use window ventilation and night cooling as part of their comfort strategy. The difference is choice: in winter, you have fresh air without heat loss. In summer, you have both options.

Myth 2: "The air feels artificial or dry"

MVHR systems deliver 100% outdoor air — not recirculated air. The air you breathe in a Passive House is the same outdoor air available through an open window, minus the pollen, particulates, and temperature extremes. Humidity levels are maintained within the 40–60% relative humidity range recommended for health, because the balanced extract removes excess moisture from wet rooms before it can accumulate.

Myth 3: "MVHR systems are noisy"

PHI-certified systems must operate at ≤25 dB at supply and extract points — below the threshold of perception for most people. The main unit, installed in a utility space, generates approximately 35–45 dB (comparable to a quiet refrigerator). With proper acoustic separation and duct-mounted silencers, occupants typically cannot detect the system is running.

Myth 4: "They spread germs through the house"

The supply and extract airstreams in an MVHR unit never mix. Heat transfers through solid plates in the exchanger — air does not cross over. Furthermore, the F7 supply filter captures bacteria and mold spores. Post-COVID research published in Science (2024) identified mechanical ventilation with adequate filtration as one of the most effective strategies for reducing airborne pathogen transmission in buildings.

Myth 5: "It's too expensive for the benefit"

At €3,500–€7,000 installed, MVHR costs less than most kitchen renovations. It runs for under €90 per year, saves 20–30% on heating bills, filters allergens, maintains consistent CO₂ levels, and lasts 20+ years. The EU projects that improved ventilation standards will save European households a combined €6.2 billion per year by 2030, according to the European Commission's Ecodesign assessment.

The Bottom Line

MVHR is not a luxury add-on — it's the breathing system of a Passive House. It recovers 75–95% of exhaust heat, filters incoming air to remove pollen and particulates, maintains CO₂ below 700 ppm, and costs less than €90 per year to operate. Combined with Passive House airtightness, it delivers indoor air quality and thermal comfort that no amount of window-opening can match. The revised EU Energy Performance of Buildings Directive increasingly mandates heat recovery in new construction — making MVHR not just best practice, but the direction European building regulation is heading.
