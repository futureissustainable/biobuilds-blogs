A Passive House is only as good as its weakest component. The building envelope—the continuous thermal boundary between inside and outside—determines whether a home achieves the Passive House Institute's demanding performance targets or falls short. And within that envelope, windows represent both the greatest thermal vulnerability and the greatest design opportunity. They're the thinnest part of the wall assembly, the primary source of natural light and solar heat gain, and the component where specification errors are most costly.

We've specified windows for over 200 certified Passive House projects at BIOBUILDS, and the difference between a well-detailed envelope and a merely adequate one is measurable in both energy bills and occupant comfort. This guide explains the technical requirements, the physics behind them, and the practical decisions that determine whether your building envelope performs as designed.

What is the building envelope?

The building envelope is the complete physical barrier separating the conditioned interior of a building from the unconditioned exterior. It includes every element that forms this thermal and air boundary: walls, roof, floor slab or basement ceiling, windows, doors, and all the joints and connections between them.

In a Passive House, the building envelope does the heavy lifting that mechanical systems handle in conventional buildings. Rather than compensating for a leaky, poorly insulated shell with large heating and cooling systems, the Passive House approach invests in envelope quality to reduce energy demand to a fraction of the norm. According to the Passive House Institute, the envelope must achieve continuous thermal protection with no weak points—because any break in the insulation layer, any air leak, or any thermal bridge undermines the entire system.

The four envelope components must work as an integrated system:

  • Superinsulated walls, roof, and floor with U-values of 0.10-0.15 W/(m²K), typically requiring 25-30 cm of insulation with conventional materials like mineral wool or wood fibre
  • High-performance windows and doors achieving Uw ≤0.80 W/(m²K) with triple glazing and insulated frames
  • Continuous airtight layer tested to ≤0.6 air changes per hour at 50 Pascals—approximately 5-8 times tighter than conventional construction
  • Thermal bridge-free connections at every junction, penetration, and transition between building elements

Remove any one of these components and the system fails. A perfectly insulated wall with leaky windows still loses excessive heat. A superb window installed with thermal bridges at its frame junction still creates cold spots and condensation risk. The envelope is a system, and its performance is determined by its weakest link.

Passive House window requirements

Windows must achieve a whole-window thermal transmittance (Uw) of ≤0.80 W/(m²K) to meet the Passive House Institute standard. This is the combined performance of glazing, frame, spacer bar, and installation—not just the glass alone. It represents roughly 40-50% less heat loss than the German building code minimum of 1.3 W/(m²K) for new construction under the Gebäudeenergiegesetz (GEG).

According to the PHI Certified Component Database, window performance is evaluated across several parameters beyond the headline U-value:

  • Glazing U-value (Ug): The thermal transmittance of the glass unit alone, typically 0.5-0.7 W/(m²K) for Passive House-suitable triple glazing
  • Frame U-value (Uf): The thermal transmittance of the frame profile, which is often worse than the glazing and must be well-insulated
  • Glass edge thermal bridge (Ψg): Heat loss at the transition between glass and frame, minimised by warm-edge spacer bars
  • Solar heat gain coefficient (g-value): The fraction of solar radiation transmitted through the glazing, critical for passive solar heating
  • Installation thermal bridge (Ψinstall): Heat loss at the junction between window frame and surrounding wall

PHI classifies certified window frames into efficiency classes from phC (basic) to phA+ (highest performance), based on a composite indicator that accounts for frame losses relative to potential solar gains. Narrower frames score better because they allow more glass area—and therefore more potential solar gain—for the same rough opening size.

Key Point

The designation "triple glazing" alone is insufficient for a Passive House window. The decisive factor is the whole-window Uw value including frame, spacer, and installation—not simply the number of glass panes. A poorly framed triple-glazed window can perform worse than a well-engineered one.

Triple glazing: how it works and why it matters

Triple glazing uses three panes of glass separated by two gas-filled cavities, with low-emissivity (low-e) coatings on interior surfaces to reflect heat radiation back into the building. This configuration achieves glazing U-values (Ug) of 0.5-0.7 W/(m²K)—compared to 1.0-1.1 W/(m²K) for standard double glazing. According to Passipedia, the breakthrough for energy-efficient construction came with triple glazing using two low-e coatings combined with inert gas filling.

The physics of triple glazing

Heat transfers through glazing via three mechanisms: conduction through the glass and gas, convection currents within the gas cavities, and radiation between surfaces. Triple glazing addresses all three:

  • Conduction: The gas cavities interrupt conductive heat flow. Argon gas (the standard fill) has 34% lower thermal conductivity than air. Krypton, used in premium units, performs even better but at higher cost.
  • Convection: Optimal cavity widths of 12-16mm minimise convection currents within the gas space. Two narrower cavities (as in triple glazing) suppress convection more effectively than one wider cavity.
  • Radiation: Low-emissivity coatings on two interior glass surfaces reflect up to 95% of long-wave heat radiation back into the building, dramatically reducing radiative heat loss. Each low-e coating typically reduces the glazing U-value by 0.3-0.5 W/(m²K).

Comfort beyond energy savings

The benefit of triple glazing extends beyond reduced heating bills. The inner pane surface temperature of a triple-glazed window stays at approximately 18°C in a room heated to 21°C, compared to about 16°C for double glazing and as low as 1°C for single glazing. This eliminates the cold-spot sensation near windows that causes occupants to raise thermostats, pull furniture away from walls, and close curtains.

Higher inner surface temperatures also prevent condensation. When surface temperatures drop below the dew point of indoor air, moisture forms on the glass—a common problem with single and double glazing in cold climates that leads to mold growth and frame deterioration. Passive House windows eliminate this risk entirely.

U-values explained: glazing, frames, and whole windows

Thermal transmittance (U-value) measures the rate of heat transfer through a building element, expressed in watts per square metre per degree of temperature difference: W/(m²K). A lower U-value means less heat loss. The Passive House standard defines U-value thresholds for every envelope component because they're the foundation of energy balance calculations in the PHPP energy model.

Why whole-window U-value matters most

Window manufacturers sometimes advertise the glazing U-value (Ug) prominently—because it's always the most impressive number. But the whole-window U-value (Uw) is what determines real-world performance, and it's always higher than Ug because it includes heat loss through the frame and the glass-to-frame junction.

The relationship is calculated using the formula: Uw = (Ag × Ug + Af × Uf + lg × Ψg) / (Ag + Af), where Ag and Af are the areas of glass and frame respectively, lg is the perimeter length of the glass edge, and Ψg is the linear thermal transmittance of the spacer bar. In practice, the frame typically has a worse U-value than the glazing, meaning a window with excellent glass but a poorly insulated frame will fail the Passive House threshold.

Frame materials compared

Frame insulation quality varies significantly by material. Each option involves trade-offs between thermal performance, durability, aesthetics, and cost:

  • Timber frames: Natural insulator with Uf values of 0.7-1.2 W/(m²K). Sustainable, biophilic, and well-suited to Passive House timber construction. Requires exterior weather protection or cladding.
  • Timber-aluminium composites: Timber interior with aluminium exterior cladding. Combines the insulation and biophilic benefits of wood with weather-resistant aluminium, achieving Uf of 0.7-1.0 W/(m²K).
  • uPVC (multi-chamber): Widely used in Central Europe. Modern multi-chamber profiles with insulation cores achieve Uf of 0.8-1.1 W/(m²K) at competitive prices.
  • Aluminium (thermally broken): Slimmest profiles but inherently conductive. Requires thermal breaks (polyamide bridges) to achieve Uf of 1.0-1.6 W/(m²K). Suitable for large commercial glazing.

Solar heat gain and window orientation

A Passive House window does two things simultaneously: it prevents heat from escaping and it allows solar energy to enter. The balance between these functions is determined by the solar heat gain coefficient (SHGC, or g-value in European nomenclature)—the fraction of solar radiation that passes through the glazing. According to the Passive House Accelerator, designers "dial in the performance attributes of each window on a Passive House building to optimize solar gains appropriate for the climate and building typology."

A g-value of 0.5 means 50% of solar energy passes through the glass. For cool-temperate climates like Central Europe, Passipedia recommends a g-value of 0.5 or higher for south-facing glazing to maximise free heating from the winter sun. Lower g-values may be appropriate for hot climates or west-facing windows where overheating risk is greater.

"Passive house windows can even gain more heat in winter than they lose. The narrowest possible frames are important for this, because the sun only enters the building directly through the glass."

Passipedia, Passive House Institute

Orientation strategy for the Northern Hemisphere

Window placement and sizing should follow the path of the sun through the seasons. According to the U.S. Department of Energy, a building's long axis should run east-west for optimal solar exposure, and the distribution of glazing should reflect each facade's relationship to the sun:

  • South-facing windows: Maximise glazing area with high g-value glass (0.5-0.6). These windows receive the most winter solar gain because the low winter sun angle allows deep penetration. Roof overhangs or external shading block high-angle summer sun.
  • North-facing windows: Minimise glazing area. North-facing glass receives virtually no direct solar gain in winter, making every square metre a net heat loss. Use the minimum required for daylighting and ventilation.
  • East and west windows: Moderate sizing with external shading. East windows receive morning sun and west windows intense afternoon sun, with limited shading options because the sun angle is low. External blinds or louvers are essential for west glazing.
Design Principle

In PHPP energy modelling, south-facing triple-glazed windows with a g-value of 0.5 can achieve a net energy gain over the heating season—meaning they contribute more free solar heat than they lose through thermal transmittance. This turns windows from a thermal liability into an energy asset, but only when properly oriented and sized.

Preventing summer overheating

Solar gain is welcome in winter but unwanted in summer. The Passive House Institute requires that no more than 10% of annual hours exceed 25°C in living areas. Achieving this in well-insulated, south-glazed buildings requires deliberate shading strategy:

  • Fixed overhangs: Sized for latitude to block high summer sun while admitting low winter sun
  • External shutters or blinds: Block up to 90% of solar radiation before it enters the building—far more effective than internal blinds, which allow heat to pass through the glass before trying to reject it
  • Deciduous planting: Trees provide summer shade and allow winter light, though they're less predictable than architectural shading

Thermal bridges: the hidden energy drain

A thermal bridge is any point in the building envelope where heat bypasses the insulation layer through a material with higher thermal conductivity. According to research cited by Building Enclosure, thermal bridges can account for 17-40% of total building heat loss when unaddressed—turning even well-insulated walls into mediocre performers.

The Passive House Institute quantifies thermal bridge impact using the psi-value (Ψ)—a linear thermal transmittance measured in W/(mK) that describes extra heat loss per metre of junction length. Passipedia defines "thermal bridge free" construction as achieving Ψ ≤ 0.01 W/(mK) at all junctions. Failing to address thermal bridges can add up to 14 kWh/(m²a) to the heating demand—nearly consuming the entire 15 kWh/(m²a) Passive House budget in a single category of loss.

Common thermal bridges in window installations

Windows create thermal bridges at every junction with the surrounding wall. The PHI Window Installation Thermal Bridge Catalogue documents multiple installation scenarios across different wall types and frame positions, quantifying the thermal bridge impact of each:

  • Frame-to-wall junction: Where the window frame meets the wall structure. The installation thermal bridge (Ψinstall) depends on whether the frame is positioned in the insulation plane or at the inner or outer face of the wall.
  • Spacer bar: The component separating glass panes creates a conductive path at the glass edge. Warm-edge spacers using stainless steel or composite materials reduce this loss by 30-50% compared to conventional aluminium spacers.
  • Structural supports: Steel brackets, concrete lintels, or sills that bypass insulation to support the window.
  • Sill and threshold details: Often the most problematic junction, especially where interior and exterior sill levels must accommodate drainage while maintaining insulation continuity.

Steel-frame buildings: a cautionary example

The impact of thermal bridging varies dramatically by construction type. Industry research shows that thermal bridging can reduce the effective R-value of steel-framed walls by 50% or more—meaning a wall that tests well in a laboratory performs half as effectively in the real building. Timber framing creates less severe bridges because wood itself is a moderate insulator. This is one reason timber-frame construction, as used in BIOBUILDS homes, is naturally more compatible with Passive House performance targets.

Window installation in the insulation plane

Where a window sits within the wall assembly matters almost as much as the window itself. The Passive House Institute's thermal bridge catalogue demonstrates that positioning the window frame within the insulation layer—rather than flush with the interior or exterior face—dramatically reduces installation thermal bridges.

Three installation positions

  1. Inner face (flush with interior): The simplest installation but the worst for thermal performance. The frame edge is exposed to the cold outer layers of the wall, creating significant thermal bridging at the perimeter.
  2. Outer face (flush with exterior): Better for thermal performance but complicates weather sealing and structural support. Rarely used in practice.
  3. Within the insulation zone: The optimal position for Passive House construction. The frame is surrounded by insulation on all sides, minimising the installation thermal bridge. This typically means the window is set at approximately one-third of the wall depth from the exterior face.

The PHI catalogue examined four exterior wall types—both solid and timber construction with U-values of 0.12 W/(m²K)—combined with eight frame types and four installation positions. The results demonstrate that moving a window from a non-insulated position to the insulation plane can reduce the installation thermal bridge by 60-80%, depending on wall type and frame profile.

Airtight installation

Beyond thermal performance, window installation must maintain the building's airtight layer. In Passive House construction, the airtight connection between window frame and wall typically uses specialised sealing tapes—such as those manufactured by Pro Clima, BIOBUILDS' airtightness partner—that accommodate building movement while maintaining a permanent seal.

Three layers of sealing are standard practice for Passive House window installation:

  • Interior airtight seal: Vapor-retardant tape or membrane connecting the frame to the interior airtight layer
  • Central insulation zone: Thermal insulation (typically expanding foam or mineral wool) filling the gap between frame and wall structure
  • Exterior weather seal: Vapor-open, wind-tight tape or membrane protecting against rain and wind while allowing moisture to escape outward

Costs, subsidies, and long-term value

Passive House-certified triple-glazed windows typically cost 10-15% more than standard double-glazed units, according to industry analysis. For a 140 m² home with approximately 25-30 m² of glazing, this premium translates to roughly €2,000-4,000 depending on frame material and manufacturer.

According to Passipedia's cost-effectiveness analysis, the total additional investment for building an entire Passive House (140 m²)—including all envelope upgrades, not just windows—was under €9,500 as of 2015. The Institute's conclusion: "building a new residential building and not building a passive house is just foolish from an economic point of view."

  • EU average: Subsidies of up to €18,900 are available on average across EU member states for high-performance new construction meeting near-zero energy building requirements under the Energy Performance of Buildings Directive (EPBD)

Property value impact

Beyond operational savings, high-performance envelopes affect property values. According to research on Energy Performance Certificates, properties with the highest EPC ratings can sell for up to 14% more than those with the lowest ratings. As the EU's revised EPBD requires all new buildings to be zero-emission from 2030, buildings already meeting or exceeding these standards will hold their value while non-compliant stock faces depreciation pressure.

The factory advantage for envelope quality

Design intent means nothing if windows are installed with gaps in the insulation, airtight tapes fail because they were applied to wet substrates, or thermal bridge details are simplified on site because the weather turned bad. The Bottom Line

The building envelope is the foundation of every Passive House. Windows—the most thermally vulnerable component—must meet strict U-value, solar gain, and installation requirements that only triple-glazed, thermally insulated, precisely installed units can achieve. But windows don't work alone. They're part of a continuous thermal and airtight system where every junction, every seal, and every centimetre of insulation matters. Getting this right delivers 75-90% heating energy savings, superior comfort, and long-term property value. Getting it wrong wastes the investment in every other component.

For homeowners, it's the assurance that the products going into your walls have been independently tested—not just marketed as "energy efficient."
