Ventilation is the unsung fifth pillar of Passive House design. Insulation, airtightness, and high-performance windows get the headlines, but it's the mechanical ventilation with heat recovery (MVHR) system that makes the entire concept work—delivering fresh air continuously without surrendering the heat those other elements fought to retain. As the Passive House Institute states: "Health and comfort are the most important elements for the Passive House design, the excellent indoor air quality is indispensable."

The EU's HEALTHVENT project found that indoor air pollution causes the loss of over 2 million healthy life years annually in the European Union alone. The revised Energy Performance of Buildings Directive (EPBD 2024/1275) now mandates heat recovery efficiencies of at least 75% for nearly zero-energy buildings from 2030—a requirement that Passive House builders have met for decades. This guide explains exactly how MVHR works, what efficiency rates to expect, how it improves health, and what ongoing maintenance looks like.

Why passive houses need mechanical ventilation

A Passive House is built to be airtight—≤0.6 air changes per hour at 50 Pascals, which is 5-8 times tighter than conventional construction. This airtightness is essential for energy performance, but it creates a simple problem: without a controlled ventilation strategy, indoor air quality degrades rapidly as CO₂, moisture, and volatile organic compounds (VOCs) accumulate.

The solution is not to make buildings leakier—that would destroy their energy performance. The solution is to control exactly where air enters and exits, and to recover the energy from the outgoing air before it leaves. According to Passipedia, the fundamental approach involves "the principle of a continuous, tight building envelope" combined with purpose-designed ventilation.

The numbers make the case clearly. Without heat recovery, ventilation losses in a well-insulated building amount to roughly 35-40 kWh/m² per year in Central European climates, according to research published in Sustainability journal (2025). The entire Passive House heating budget is just 15 kWh/m²a. Ventilation without heat recovery would blow through more than double the allowable heating demand on its own. MVHR eliminates this problem by recovering the vast majority of that energy.

How MVHR works: the heat exchanger explained

MVHR operates on a straightforward principle: extract stale air from wet rooms (kitchens, bathrooms, utility areas), pass it through a heat exchanger where its warmth transfers to incoming fresh outdoor air, then distribute that preheated fresh air to habitable rooms (bedrooms, living spaces). The two airstreams never physically mix—only thermal energy transfers between them.

The four-step process

  1. Extract: Stale, moisture-laden air is drawn from kitchens, bathrooms, and WCs through dedicated extract ducts
  2. Recover: This warm outgoing air passes through a counterflow or cross-flow heat exchanger, transferring 75-96% of its heat to the incoming fresh air stream
  3. Filter: Incoming outdoor air passes through F7-grade (or higher) filters that remove pollen, fine particulates (PM2.5), and dust before entering living spaces
  4. Supply: Clean, preheated air is distributed to bedrooms and living rooms through supply ducts at a comfortable temperature

During winter, when outdoor temperatures drop to -10°C, a well-performing MVHR unit preheats the incoming air to approximately 16.5°C before it enters living spaces—using no additional energy beyond the fan electricity. The Passive House Institute requires that supply air temperature reaches at least 16.5°C when outdoor air is at -10°C.

In summer, the process works in reverse: cool indoor air transfers its coolness to the incoming warm outdoor air, reducing the cooling load. The system doesn't air-condition—it simply prevents outdoor heat from entering unchecked.

Key Principle

MVHR is not air conditioning and not heating. It is a ventilation system that happens to recover energy. The heat exchanger is essentially a passive device—no combustion, no refrigerant, no compressor. It simply allows thermal energy to transfer from one airstream to another through thin plates, using only fan electricity to move the air.

Efficiency numbers: from 75% to 96%

The Passive House Institute sets clear minimum requirements for MVHR systems used in certified buildings: a heat recovery rate of ≥75%, specific electric power consumption of ≤0.45 Wh/m³, air leakage below 3%, and supply air temperature of at least 16.5°C at -10°C outdoor temperature.

The COP advantage

The effective coefficient of performance (COP) of an MVHR system dwarfs that of heat pumps. According to building physicist Elrond Burrell's analysis, a PHI-certified MVHR with 80% heat recovery efficiency achieves an effective COP of approximately 15. For comparison, a good heat pump achieves a COP of 3-4. The MVHR saves more thermal energy than it consumes in electrical energy whenever outdoor temperatures are below 19°C—which in Central Europe is the vast majority of the heating season.

Indoor air quality: the health case for MVHR

MVHR delivers measurably better indoor air than opening windows—and the evidence base is substantial. A comprehensive ScienceDirect review (2023) of indoor air quality in residential Passive House dwellings found that "pollutant and carbon dioxide concentration are generally lower compared to naturally ventilated homes, presumably due to the requirement to install a balanced MVHR system."

CO₂ and pollutant levels

An earlier MDPI review (2020) examining 648 dwellings across 35 studies found that 74% of Passive House dwellings never measured CO₂ above 1,400 ppm—a level associated with cognitive impairment and fatigue. Swedish research measuring 20 passive houses versus 21 conventional new houses documented NO₂ levels 17% lower in passive houses (10 vs 12 µg/m³), formaldehyde 31% lower (11 vs 16 µg/m³), and ozone 12% lower (9.7 vs 11 µg/m³).

These reductions matter. The World Health Organization reports that approximately 3.2 million people die each year from illnesses attributable to household air pollution. Europeans spend roughly 90% of their time indoors, yet as the European Public Health Alliance notes, Europe still lacks binding indoor air quality standards.

Filtration and allergen control

F7-grade filters—required by the Passive House standard—effectively capture PM2.5 particulate matter, pollen, and airborne allergens before they enter living spaces. UK research documented PM2.5 levels inside a Passive House at approximately half those of a conventional house on the same street. For allergy and asthma sufferers, this filtration provides measurable relief that no amount of window-opening can replicate—opening windows actually invites pollen and outdoor pollution indoors.

However, filter quality matters. Research has shown that Passive House buildings using only basic G4 filters without secondary F7 filters inadequately filtered outdoor PM2.5, resulting in higher indoor concentrations. Proper filter specification is not optional—it's essential.

MVHR provides exceptional indoor comfort, radical energy efficiency, and equally importantly, it gives control over ventilation and indoor air quality. These are things that opening windows can never reliably deliver.

Elrond Burrell, Passivhaus in Plain English

Moisture control and mold prevention

Passive Houses consistently achieve the optimal 30-60% relative humidity range that building scientists recommend. UK studies showed 75% of MVHR-equipped homes met target humidity levels below 7 g/kg absolute humidity, with bathroom moisture clearing within 3 hours of peak generation. This controlled humidity prevents the condensation and mold growth that plague leaky, poorly ventilated buildings—eliminating a major cause of structural damage and respiratory illness.

Choosing the right MVHR system

Not all MVHR units are equal. For a Passive House, the system must be PHI-certified, properly sized for the dwelling, and correctly installed with well-designed ductwork. Here are the critical selection criteria.

PHI certification is non-negotiable

The Passive House Institute Component Database lists every certified ventilation unit with verified performance data. For Passive House certification, your MVHR must appear in this database with a heat recovery rate ≥75% and specific electric power ≤0.45 Wh/m³. As of 2025, the database includes units from manufacturers including Zehnder, Brink, Paul, Dantherm, and Swegon.

Sizing for your home

MVHR systems are sized based on the number of occupants and the dwelling's floor area, ensuring adequate air exchange rates. The Passive House standard typically requires 30 m³/h per person of fresh air supply. For a typical 3-bedroom home with 4 occupants, this means a minimum supply rate of 120 m³/h. Units should be selected so that the normal operating point falls in the mid-range of the unit's capacity—not at maximum, where efficiency drops and noise increases.

Ductwork design

The ductwork matters as much as the unit itself. Poorly designed or installed ducts create noise, reduce airflow, and compromise the system's efficiency. Key principles include:

  • Rigid or semi-rigid ducts preferred over flexible ducts (lower pressure drop, less noise)
  • Balanced airflow between supply and extract volumes
  • Acoustic attenuators at the unit and at room terminals
  • Short, direct duct runs with minimal bends
  • Airtight duct connections to prevent leakage within the system

In factory-built modular construction, ductwork routing is designed and installed in controlled conditions before the modules leave the production facility. This eliminates the site-coordination problems—crushed ducts, wrong-angle bends, forgotten acoustic attenuators—that plague traditional construction.

Maintenance: what homeowners need to know

MVHR systems are low-maintenance, but they are not zero-maintenance. A study of 150 UK homes with MVHR systems found that 66% did not perform annual maintenance, with visible dirt found in 43% of homes and 77% showing dust and dirt in the ductwork. Neglected systems underperform—delivering less fresh air, consuming more electricity, and potentially degrading indoor air quality rather than improving it.

The maintenance schedule

  1. Every 3-6 months: Check filters. Replace when visibly dirty or when the unit's filter indicator signals. Replacement filters typically cost €15-40 per set
  2. Every 6-12 months: Replace supply and extract filters according to manufacturer guidelines. Clean external grilles and intake/exhaust terminals
  3. Annually: Clean the heat exchanger (most are dishwasher-safe). Inspect ductwork connections. Check condensate drain for blockages
  4. Every 5-7 years: Professional inspection of ductwork condition and fan motor performance. Consider duct cleaning if warranted

The total annual maintenance cost is modest: approximately €60-120 per year in filter replacements, plus minimal time for the homeowner. Compare this to the system's energy savings of approximately €300-500 per year in avoided heating costs, and the maintenance investment is negligible.

Practical Tip

Set a calendar reminder to check your filters quarterly. Most modern MVHR units include filter change indicators on the control panel or connected app. When in doubt, hold the filter up to light—if you can't see through it, replace it. Clean filters are the single most important factor in maintaining system performance and indoor air quality.

The "sealed box" myth: can you open windows?

The most persistent misconception about Passive Houses: you can't open the windows. This is completely wrong. You can open any window, any time you want. The MVHR system simply means you don't need to open them for fresh air—it arrives continuously, filtered, and at a comfortable temperature.

On a mild spring day, opening windows is perfectly fine. The MVHR system can be set to a lower speed or bypass mode when outdoor conditions are comfortable. Many units include automatic bypass functions that route outdoor air directly into the building when temperatures are suitable—skipping the heat exchanger to provide free cooling on warm evenings.

Where windows become a poor ventilation strategy:

  • Winter: Opening windows when it's -5°C outside dumps the heat you've carefully retained, forcing your heating system to compensate
  • High pollen season: Natural ventilation brings pollen directly indoors. MVHR filters it out
  • Urban locations: Street noise and air pollution make open windows undesirable, especially at night
  • Security concerns: Ground-floor windows left open overnight create vulnerability
  • Rainy or humid weather: Open windows introduce uncontrolled moisture

MVHR gives you the freedom to keep windows closed when conditions outside are unfavorable—while still enjoying fresh, clean air 24 hours a day. It's not a restriction. It's a choice you didn't have before.
