Passive House Ventilation: How MVHR Works and Why Indoor Air Quality Matters

Mechanical ventilation with heat recovery (MVHR) is the engine behind every Passive House's air quality and energy performance. It recovers 75-95% of heat from outgoing stale air, filters 99% of dust and allergens from incoming fresh air, and reduces ventilation heat losses from approximately 24 kWh/m²a to as little as 1.2 kWh/m²a. Research across 40 studies confirms that Passive House dwellings with MVHR achieve measurably lower CO₂, PM2.5, and VOC concentrations than conventionally ventilated homes.

Ventilation is the most misunderstood component of Passive House design. People grasp insulation intuitively—thicker walls keep heat in. They understand windows—triple glazing outperforms double. But ask how a building that's 30 times more airtight than a conventional house delivers better air quality, and the answer surprises most: mechanical ventilation with heat recovery, a technology that simultaneously solves the energy problem and the air quality problem in a single system.

In conventional buildings, ventilation accounts for roughly 30-36% of total heat loss, according to research on European building stock. Opening a window in January replaces warm indoor air with cold outdoor air, wasting the energy used to heat it. MVHR eliminates this trade-off by recovering up to 95% of the heat from outgoing air and transferring it to the incoming fresh supply. The result is continuous fresh air at near-room temperature, with no energy penalty. This guide explains how the system works, what the data says about its performance, and what to look for when choosing and maintaining one.

Why ventilation is the hidden fifth principle

Ventilation is often listed last among the five Passive House principles, but it's arguably the one that makes the entire concept viable. Without MVHR, a building meeting the Passive House airtightness standard of ≤0.6 ACH50 would have insufficient fresh air exchange for occupant health. Without airtightness, MVHR would be recovering heat from air that's already leaking out through gaps and cracks.

According to Passipedia, the Passive House Institute's knowledge base: "Fresh air has to be supplied to the living room, children's room, sleeping rooms, and workrooms." The MVHR system handles this continuously and automatically, delivering 30 m³/h of fresh air per person in accordance with DIN 1946-6 ventilation standards—without requiring occupants to open windows or remember to ventilate.

Key Distinction

Airtightness and ventilation are complementary, not contradictory. A Passive House is deliberately airtight to control where air enters and exits the building. MVHR then provides fresh air through a defined, filtered, heat-recovering path—delivering better air quality than the random infiltration through cracks and gaps that conventional buildings rely on.

The relationship is bidirectional: airtightness makes MVHR effective (the system controls all air exchange), and MVHR makes airtightness safe (fresh air is always supplied). Remove either element and the Passive House concept fails—either through uncontrolled heat loss or inadequate fresh air supply.

How MVHR actually works

An MVHR unit is mechanically simple. Two separate fans move two separate airstreams through a heat exchanger. The incoming fresh air and outgoing stale air pass within millimetres of each other inside the exchanger but never mix—only heat transfers between them.

The airflow path

The system follows a cascade ventilation principle:

  1. Supply side: Fresh outdoor air enters through an intake on the building exterior, passes through a filter (typically F7/ISO ePM1 grade), flows through the heat exchanger where it absorbs warmth from the outgoing air, and is distributed via ductwork to living rooms and bedrooms
  2. Extract side: Stale air is drawn from kitchens and bathrooms (where moisture and odours concentrate), passes through the other side of the heat exchanger to surrender its heat, and exits the building through an exhaust outlet

This cascade design means fresh air moves naturally from "clean" living spaces toward "dirty" wet rooms. You breathe the freshest air where you spend the most time, and the most polluted air exits the building after giving up its heat.

The heat exchanger

The core of any MVHR unit is its heat exchanger. Two main types exist:

  • Cross-flow exchangers: Air streams cross at 90° angles. Simpler design, lower cost, typical heat recovery efficiency of 70-85%
  • Counter-flow exchangers: Air streams flow in opposite directions along parallel channels. Higher efficiency, typically 85-95% heat recovery, used in most PHI-certified units

The Passive House Institute requires a minimum heat recovery efficiency of 75% for certification. In practice, most certified units achieve 85-95%. This means that when outdoor air is 0°C and indoor air is 21°C, the supply air entering your rooms has already been warmed to approximately 16-20°C—without any additional energy input.

The energy math: why heat recovery changes everything

Without heat recovery, the ventilation required for healthy indoor air in a well-insulated building would consume approximately 24 kWh/m²a in heat losses. That's more than the entire Passive House heating budget of 15 kWh/m²a. In other words, you could build the most insulated house imaginable and still fail the Passive House standard if ventilation heat losses go unrecovered.

MVHR transforms this equation. A unit with 90% heat recovery efficiency reduces ventilation heat losses to approximately 2.4 kWh/m²a—a reduction of 90%. At 95% efficiency, losses drop to just 1.2 kWh/m²a. The system's own electricity consumption for fans typically runs at 0.35-0.45 Wh per cubic metre of air transported, which for a typical house amounts to roughly 300-500 kWh of electricity annually.

This creates a remarkable energy balance. As Passipedia notes: "High-quality ventilation units save much more energy through the prevention of heat losses than they use to run." An MVHR unit with 80% efficiency operating when outdoor temperatures are below 19°C achieves an effective coefficient of performance (COP) of approximately 15—meaning it delivers 15 units of heat energy saved for every 1 unit of electrical energy consumed. For comparison, a typical air-source heat pump achieves a COP of 3-4.

"Ventilation can represent up to 30% to 60% of the total buildings' energy demand. Reducing the ventilation's energy use through heat recovery is a key parameter to further reduce the buildings' energy demand."

At the EU level, heat recovery from ventilation units saved an estimated 17 TWh of final energy on space heating in 2020, according to the European Commission's energy-efficient products database. That figure is projected to reach 40 TWh by 2030 as adoption increases.

Indoor air quality: what the research shows

The energy case for MVHR is compelling, but the health case may be even stronger. A systematic review published in the International Journal of Environmental Research and Public Health (2020) analysed 40 studies on indoor air quality in Passive House dwellings. Of those, 24 concluded that Passive Houses achieve acceptable indoor air quality levels, and 11 studies directly comparing Passive House to conventional buildings found better air quality in the Passive House dwellings.

CO₂ concentrations

CO₂ levels serve as a proxy for overall ventilation adequacy. The review found that 74% of Passive House dwellings with MVHR never exceeded 1,400 ppm CO₂. By contrast, dwellings relying on natural ventilation exceeded 1,400 ppm approximately 25% of the time. A Chinese study measured CO₂ levels of 622-841 ppm in Passive House flats versus over 1,000 ppm in conventional apartments—well below the 1,000 ppm threshold recommended by most indoor air quality standards.

Schools show similar patterns. Research documented that mechanical ventilation in school buildings lowers CO₂ levels by 20-30% compared to natural ventilation, with direct implications for student concentration and learning outcomes. A 2025 study monitoring an Italian high school classroom found statistically significant improvements in both CO₂ levels and student attentiveness after MVHR installation.

UK research documented PM2.5 particulate levels inside a Passive House at approximately half those of a conventional house on the same street. The required F7-grade supply air filters effectively intercept outdoor particulate pollution before it enters living spaces—a benefit that conventional buildings with their unfiltered air infiltration simply cannot match.

Temperature and humidity stability

MVHR contributes to the remarkable temperature stability documented in Passive Houses. Polish monitoring over 2018-2022 maintained indoor temperatures at 22°C average while outdoor temperatures ranged from -28.2°C to 36.2°C. Relative humidity consistently stays within the optimal 30-60% range, mitigating both mould risk (high humidity) and respiratory discomfort (low humidity).

Filters, maintenance, and real-world performance

The air filtration built into every MVHR unit is one of its most practical benefits—but only when maintained. Filters determine what reaches your lungs and what gets intercepted before entering the house.

Filter grades and what they capture

  • G4 / ISO Coarse (extract air filter): Captures large dust particles, fibres, and insects. Protects the heat exchanger from contamination. Replaced annually
  • F7 / ISO ePM1 (supply air filter): Captures fine particles including pollen, mould spores, and PM2.5 particulates. The standard filter grade required by the Passive House Institute. Replaced every 6-12 months
  • F9 / ISO ePM1 80% (enhanced supply filter): Higher-grade filtration for urban environments or allergy sufferers. Captures a greater percentage of ultrafine particles but creates higher pressure drop, slightly increasing fan energy consumption

Research highlighted in the 2020 literature review found that Passive Houses using G4 filters without the required F7 secondary filter showed higher indoor PM2.5 concentrations than properly filtered systems—underscoring that filter grade directly determines air quality outcomes.

Maintenance Reality

The same 2020 review found that 66% of Passive House occupants in the studied dwellings lacked annual maintenance schedules, and 77% had dust or dirt accumulation on ducts. This represents a significant gap between the technology's potential and its real-world performance. Regular filter changes and periodic duct inspection are essential for the system to deliver its designed air quality benefits.

Practical maintenance schedule

  1. Every 3-4 months: Visual check of supply air filter; replace if visibly dirty or after high-pollen seasons
  2. Every 6-12 months: Replace supply air filter (F7 grade); clean or replace extract air filter (G4 grade)
  3. Every 2-3 years: Professional inspection of ductwork, heat exchanger cleaning, and fan motor check
  4. Every 5-7 years: Full duct cleaning and system rebalancing

Annual filter costs typically run €50-120 depending on unit type and filter grade—far less than the energy savings the system delivers.

Certification doesn't just verify the building—it ensures the ventilation system actually performs as designed.

Why installation quality matters more than brand

The most common complaints about MVHR—noise, draughts, insufficient air flow—are almost always installation problems, not equipment problems. A well-designed system with a mediocre unit outperforms a poorly installed system with a premium unit every time.

Critical installation details

  • Duct sizing: Undersized ducts increase air velocity, creating noise and raising fan energy consumption. Ductwork should be sized for air velocities below 3 m/s in occupied spaces
  • Silencers: Acoustic attenuators on both supply and extract ducts near the unit and at room terminals prevent fan noise transmission
  • Vibration isolation: The MVHR unit should never be mounted directly to structural elements. Flexible connections and anti-vibration mounts prevent structure-borne noise
  • Duct airtightness: Leaky ductwork wastes recovered heat and can draw contaminated air from wall cavities. All joints must be sealed
  • Commissioning: After installation, airflow rates must be measured and balanced at each supply and extract point to match design values

This is where factory-built modular construction holds a decisive advantage. When MVHR ductwork is installed in a controlled factory environment, every joint is accessible for inspection and sealing. Duct runs are shorter and straighter (reducing pressure drop and energy consumption). The system is commissioned before the module leaves the factory, eliminating the site-based installation variables that cause most real-world performance issues.

Summer operation and bypass mode

A common concern is whether MVHR works in summer—after all, you don't want to recover heat when it's 35°C outside. Every certified MVHR unit includes a summer bypass mode that addresses this.

How bypass works

When outdoor temperatures exceed a set threshold (typically 22-24°C, adjustable by the user), a motorised damper diverts incoming air around the heat exchanger. Fresh air enters at outdoor temperature without absorbing heat from the exhaust stream. The exhaust air still exits the building, maintaining positive ventilation, but no heat transfers between the two streams.

According to Passipedia, during summer the heat recovery mechanism can actually cool incoming air "almost to room air temperature"—because when outdoor air is warmer than indoor air, the heat exchanger works in reverse, transferring heat from incoming air to the cooler outgoing air. This provides modest passive cooling without any additional energy input.

Supplementary cooling strategies

MVHR alone doesn't replace dedicated cooling in hot climates, but it integrates well with supplementary strategies:

  • Night purge ventilation: Increased airflow rates during cool nights to flush stored heat from the building mass
  • Ground-source pre-tempering: Incoming air routed through a buried earth tube or brine circuit to pre-cool it by 5-8°C before reaching the MVHR unit
  • Integration with heat pumps: Some MVHR units include a small integrated heat pump that provides active cooling when needed

The Passive House summer comfort criterion—no more than 10% of hours above 25°C—is primarily achieved through building design (shading, orientation, thermal mass) rather than MVHR alone. But the ventilation system contributes by maintaining controlled air exchange and enabling night cooling without compromising security.

The Bottom Line

MVHR is not optional equipment in a Passive House—it's a foundational system that makes airtight, super-insulated buildings both habitable and efficient. It recovers 75-95% of ventilation heat losses, filters 99% of airborne allergens and particulates, and delivers measurably better air quality than naturally ventilated buildings. The technology is proven across tens of thousands of certified buildings worldwide. The only variable is installation quality and maintenance commitment—both of which determine whether the system delivers its full potential or merely its minimum performance.

The global MVHR market reached approximately USD 2.7 billion in 2026 and is projected to grow to USD 4.5 billion by 2033, driven primarily by tightening energy performance regulations across Europe. As the revised Energy Performance of Buildings Directive (EPBD) pushes all new construction toward zero-emission building standards by 2030, mechanical ventilation with heat recovery will shift from a Passive House speciality to a standard feature of competent building design.
