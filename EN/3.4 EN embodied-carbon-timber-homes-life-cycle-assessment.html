Every building has a carbon footprint that begins long before anyone switches on the lights. The emissions generated by extracting raw materials, manufacturing structural components, transporting them to site, and assembling the structure are collectively known as embodied carbon—and they account for a growing share of the construction industry's climate impact. As buildings become more energy-efficient in operation, the carbon locked into their materials at the point of construction becomes the dominant factor in their lifetime environmental footprint.

Life-cycle assessment (LCA) provides the scientific framework for measuring and comparing these impacts across different building materials. A growing body of peer-reviewed research—spanning hundreds of case studies across Europe and beyond—consistently reaches the same conclusion: timber-frame construction produces substantially less embodied carbon than concrete or steel alternatives. The data is unambiguous enough that the EU has now embedded whole-life carbon requirements into building regulation.

What is embodied carbon

Embodied carbon is the total greenhouse gas emissions associated with every stage of a building material's existence: extraction of raw resources, manufacturing and processing, transportation, on-site construction, maintenance over the building's life, and eventual demolition or recycling. Unlike operational carbon—the emissions from heating, cooling, and powering a building—embodied carbon is released primarily at the point of construction and cannot be reduced afterward.

In the EU, embodied carbon contributes approximately 10–20% of a building's total lifetime CO2 footprint, according to the Buildings Performance Institute Europe (BPIE). That share is increasing as operational efficiency improves. A Passivhaus-certified home using just 15 kWh/m²/year for heating has minimal operational emissions—meaning the materials it's built from become the primary determinant of its carbon impact.

The concept matters because construction material choices are effectively permanent. You can upgrade insulation, install solar panels, or switch to a heat pump to reduce operational carbon decades after construction. But the concrete in your foundations, the steel in your beams, or the timber in your frame emitted their carbon at manufacture—those emissions are irreversible. Choosing lower-carbon materials at the design stage is the only opportunity to address this impact.

The problem with concrete and steel

Cement production alone accounts for approximately 8% of global CO2 emissions—roughly 1.6 billion tonnes in 2022, according to the World Economic Forum. If the cement industry were a country, it would be the world's third-largest emitter. Steel production adds another 7–8%, with every tonne of steel generating an average of 2.18 tonnes of CO2-equivalent in 2024, according to the World Steel Association. Together, these two materials account for approximately 15% of all human-caused greenhouse gas emissions.

What makes these materials particularly problematic is the nature of their emissions. More than half of cement's carbon output comes from the chemical process itself—heating limestone (calcium carbonate) releases CO2 as a byproduct regardless of the energy source. Even if every cement kiln ran on renewable electricity, the process emissions would remain. One tonne of cement typically produces 0.8–0.9 tonnes of CO2.

Steel faces similar challenges. While electric arc furnaces using recycled scrap can reduce emissions significantly, primary steel production from iron ore requires blast furnace temperatures achievable only through carbon-intensive processes. The UNECE classifies both industries as "hard-to-abate" sectors where full decarbonisation remains technically challenging. Hydrogen-based steelmaking shows promise—the first commercial-scale plants are entering production in 2026—but industry-wide adoption remains decades away.

Decarbonisation of cement, steel and chemicals production is challenging and, in some cases, technically impossible, as energy-intensive industries require high temperatures and chemical processes that are today most efficiently reached by burning fossil fuels.

Timber's carbon advantage by the numbers

Research analysing 127 different building frame configurations—spanning 2 to 19 stories—found that timber frames produce 36% less embodied carbon than concrete and 48% less than steel alternatives. This is not a marginal advantage. It represents a fundamental difference in environmental impact driven by the nature of the materials themselves.

A comprehensive dataset published in the journal Building and Environment (2025) quantified embodied carbon intensities across three structural frame types. The results paint a clear picture:

A separate meta-analysis of 62 peer-reviewed studies confirmed the pattern: substituting reinforced concrete structures with mass timber avoids approximately 43% of greenhouse gas emissions on average. Over a 50-year reference study period, the annual embodied carbon intensity ranges from 0.9–1.1 kg CO2-eq/m² for timber, 2.1–3.0 for reinforced concrete, and 2.7–4.2 for steel.

A review of 100 case buildings across 37 peer-reviewed articles by Frontiers in Built Environment (2022) found that timber buildings demonstrate 28–47% lower embodied energy than concrete and steel respectively, with mean embodied energy values of 2.92 GJ/m² for timber versus 4.08 GJ/m² for concrete and 5.55 GJ/m² for steel. When end-of-life energy recovery from wood waste is included, timber buildings achieve over 50% lower primary energy input than concrete alternatives.

European context

The advantage is particularly pronounced in Europe. A global comparative study found that among steel-framed structures designed to different national building codes, those designed to EU codes had the highest carbon emissions at 142 kg CO2-eq/m². This makes the case for substituting steel with timber even stronger in European markets where building codes are pushing construction toward lower-carbon solutions.

Carbon storage: buildings as carbon sinks

Timber doesn't just emit less carbon during production—it actively removes carbon from the atmosphere. Trees absorb CO2 through photosynthesis, converting it into organic carbon that constitutes approximately half the dry weight of wood. When that wood becomes a building, the stored carbon remains locked away for the structure's entire service life rather than returning to the atmosphere.

Each cubic metre of timber stores approximately 0.9–1 tonne of CO2-equivalent. The calculation is straightforward: a cubic metre of softwood weighs roughly 500 kg, half of which (250 kg) is carbon. Since each kilogram of carbon corresponds to 3.67 kg of CO2 (the molecular weight ratio of CO2 to C), the total sequestered CO2 comes to approximately 917 kg. Even after subtracting roughly 100 kg of production emissions for kiln drying and processing, the net removal is approximately 817 kg per cubic metre.

A typical 200 m² timber-framed home contains roughly 30 m³ of structural wood, storing approximately 27 tonnes of CO2-equivalent, according to data from Scion Research. Larger buildings demonstrate the principle at scale: the Scion Innovation Hub, a three-storey timber building, stores 418 tonnes of CO2-equivalent in its 455 m³ of structural wood.

This storage effect transforms the carbon accounting fundamentally. Concrete and steel release carbon during manufacture. Timber removes it. A life-cycle assessment that includes biogenic carbon storage doesn't just show timber as "less bad" than conventional materials—it shows timber construction as a potential net carbon sink. Research published in Nature Communications modelled scenarios where if 90% of new urban residents were housed in mid-rise timber buildings, 106 gigatonnes of CO2 could be avoided by 2100.

Key Takeaway

Timber is the only major structural material that stores more carbon than it emits during production. Concrete and steel release carbon; timber locks it away. This fundamental difference makes material choice one of the most impactful climate decisions in residential construction.

How life-cycle assessment works

Life-cycle assessment is the standardised methodology used to quantify these comparisons. Governed by ISO 14040 and ISO 14044, LCA evaluates environmental impacts across a product's entire existence. For buildings, the European standard EN 15978 provides the specific framework, dividing the building life cycle into clearly defined stages.

The four main LCA stages for buildings are:

  1. Product stage (A1–A3): Raw material extraction, transport to factory, and manufacturing. This is where the largest differences between timber, concrete, and steel emerge.
  2. Construction stage (A4–A5): Transport to site and on-site assembly. Factory-built modular homes typically show lower A4–A5 emissions due to reduced site activity and optimised logistics.
  3. Use stage (B1–B7): Maintenance, repair, replacement, and operational energy over the building's reference service life (typically 50–60 years for LCA purposes).
  4. End-of-life stage (C1–C4): Demolition, waste processing, and disposal or recycling. Timber's ability to be recycled, repurposed, or used for energy recovery provides additional advantages at this stage.

The EU's Level(s) framework provides a common European approach to building sustainability assessment, with Indicator 1.2 specifically addressing life-cycle Global Warming Potential. This indicator is becoming central to building regulation as the EU moves toward mandatory whole-life carbon reporting.

What LCA reveals about timber construction

When research examines building materials through the full LCA lens, timber's advantages extend beyond embodied carbon alone. The Frontiers in Built Environment review found that timber buildings show 28–47% lower embodied energy than concrete and steel. At end of life, energy recovery from wood waste can push the total life-cycle energy advantage to over 50%.

An important nuance emerges in the LCA data: even in timber-framed buildings, non-timber elements contribute significantly to total emissions. One study found that while the timber structure itself accounted for only 17% of embodied emissions, steel connections contributed 26%, concrete foundations 32%, and non-structural materials like screed 25%. This underscores the importance of holistic design—a timber frame achieves its full carbon advantage only when accompanied by careful specification of every other building component.

EU regulation: the EPBD and whole-life carbon

The revised Energy Performance of Buildings Directive (EPBD 2024/1275), which entered into force on 28 May 2024, marks a historic shift in European building regulation. For the first time, EU law will require the measurement and disclosure of embodied carbon in buildings—not just operational energy performance.

The European Academy of Sciences Advisory Council (EASAC) had called for precisely this broadening of scope, noting that legislation previously looked only at operational energy use. The revised EPBD now requires member states to both disclose whole-life carbon for all new buildings from 2030 and set targets that become progressively stricter over time.

The calculation framework is based on EN 15978 and the EU's Level(s) Indicator 1.2, ensuring methodological consistency across member states. Environmental Product Declarations (EPDs)—standardised documents that quantify a product's environmental impact using verified LCA data—will become essential for demonstrating compliance.

The new Directive, for the first time, will require regulation of the carbon embodied in buildings associated with their construction, maintenance, and demolition. Up until now, legislation solely looked at the energy used for operating buildings.

European Academy of Sciences Advisory Council (EASAC), 2024

This regulatory shift makes timber-frame construction increasingly attractive for developers, architects, and homeowners across the EU. Materials with verified low embodied carbon—documented through EPDs and consistent with the Level(s) framework—will have a measurable advantage as whole-life carbon targets tighten over the coming decade.

Embodied vs. operational carbon

The relationship between embodied and operational carbon evolves as buildings become more energy-efficient. In a conventional building with poor insulation and gas heating, operational emissions dominate the lifetime footprint—embodied carbon may represent only 10–20% of total emissions. But in a high-performance building like a Passivhaus, the equation inverts.

A Passivhaus-certified home operates on just 15 kWh/m²/year—compared to the EU average of approximately 150–350 kWh/m²/year. When operational energy drops by 90–95%, embodied carbon becomes the dominant contributor to the building's lifetime climate impact. In a zero-emission building (ZEB)—which the EPBD requires for all new buildings from 2030—embodied carbon may account for 50% or more of total lifetime emissions.

Key Takeaway

As buildings approach zero operational emissions, material choices become the decisive carbon factor. A Passivhaus built from high-carbon materials can have a larger lifetime footprint than a moderately efficient building made from timber. The most climate-responsible approach combines both: Passivhaus-level energy efficiency with low-embodied-carbon materials.

This convergence explains why leading sustainable building programmes are moving toward whole-life carbon thinking. It is no longer sufficient to optimise operational energy alone. The combined approach—minimal operational carbon through Passivhaus certification plus minimal embodied carbon through timber construction—delivers the lowest total climate impact achievable in residential construction today.

Practical choices for lower-carbon homes

Understanding embodied carbon data is valuable, but the practical question remains: what choices actually reduce a home's carbon footprint? Research points to several high-impact decisions that homeowners and builders can make.

Material selection

The structural frame is the single largest embodied carbon decision. Choosing a timber frame over concrete or steel eliminates 36–48% of structural embodied carbon at the point of design. Beyond the frame, specifying wood-fibre insulation over synthetic alternatives, timber cladding over metal or composite panels, and wood-based interior finishes over plasterboard further reduces the total material footprint.

Certified wood products from sustainably managed forests (FSC or PEFC certification) ensure that carbon storage benefits are genuine. Sustainable forestry maintains forest area and biodiversity, creating a cycle where harvested trees are replaced and the total carbon stored in wood products plus growing forests exceeds what an unharvested forest would store.

Factory vs. site construction

Factory-built modular construction reduces embodied carbon through several mechanisms. Controlled manufacturing environments minimise material waste—modular construction generates up to 90% less site waste than conventional building. Optimised cutting patterns reduce timber offcuts. Centralised logistics reduce transport emissions through efficient batch delivery rather than dozens of individual material shipments.

The precision of factory manufacturing also reduces the need for remedial work that consumes additional materials and energy on conventional sites. Components manufactured to Passivhaus tolerances achieve correct fit the first time, eliminating the rework cycle common in site-built construction.

EPDs: verifying the numbers

Environmental Product Declarations provide the verified evidence that substantiates embodied carbon claims. An EPD is essentially an environmental "nutrition label" for building products—it reports the results of a third-party-verified LCA across standardised impact categories including global warming potential, ozone depletion, and resource consumption.

When evaluating building materials, look for EPDs that conform to EN 15804 (the European standard for construction product EPDs) and are verified by an accredited programme operator. Products with EPDs allow architects and engineers to calculate whole-life carbon at the design stage with verified data rather than generic assumptions—a capability that will become essential under the EPBD's disclosure requirements.

The evidence on embodied carbon leaves little room for ambiguity. Timber-frame construction produces substantially less carbon than concrete or steel, stores carbon absorbed during tree growth, and generates lower life-cycle energy demand across production, use, and end-of-life stages. As the EU moves toward mandatory whole-life carbon reporting, these advantages become not just environmental benefits but regulatory and economic ones.
