The most common objection to timber-frame construction is also the most intuitive: wood burns. It is a reasonable concern. Anyone who has lit a campfire understands that wood is combustible. But the fire behaviour of structural timber in a building bears almost no resemblance to kindling in a firepit. The engineering reality, documented across decades of fire research and codified in European standards, is that heavy timber performs remarkably well in fire—often better than unprotected steel, and comparably to concrete in many scenarios.

Understanding why requires moving beyond the instinct that "combustible equals dangerous" and examining what actually happens when structural timber meets flame. The answer involves predictable physics, a natural insulating mechanism, and a failure mode that is gradual and visible rather than sudden and catastrophic. These properties are not theoretical—they are measurable, testable, and the basis for building codes across Europe that now permit timber construction up to 18 storeys.

The science of charring: how timber protects itself

When structural timber is exposed to fire, the outer surface undergoes pyrolysis—a thermal decomposition process that converts wood into charcoal at temperatures above 300°C. This char layer is the key to timber's fire resistance. Charcoal is a poor conductor of heat, approximately six times less conductive than intact wood, which itself is already a relatively poor heat conductor. The char layer therefore acts as a natural insulating blanket, progressively slowing the rate at which heat penetrates to the structural wood beneath.

According to Eurocode 5 (EN 1995-1-2), the European design standard for timber structures in fire, softwood chars at a one-dimensional rate of 0.65 mm per minute for timber with a density exceeding 290 kg/m³. This means that after 30 minutes of fire exposure, a large-section glulam beam will have developed approximately 20 mm of char on exposed surfaces. After 60 minutes, approximately 39 mm. The rate is predictable, consistent, and well-validated through decades of experimental testing.

The structural wood beneath the char layer remains cool and retains 85-90% of its original strength, according to International Timber's analysis of fire testing data. This is the critical distinction between how people imagine wood burning and how structural timber actually performs: the charred exterior sacrifices itself to protect the structural interior. Fire engineers design timber elements with sufficient cross-sectional area that the residual section—after charring is subtracted—still carries the required structural load.

The reduced cross-section method

The standard engineering approach for fire design of timber structures is the reduced cross-section method, codified in Eurocode 5. The method calculates the effective structural section by subtracting the charring depth plus a zero-strength layer (typically 7 mm) from each exposed face. The remaining wood is assumed to retain its full ambient-temperature strength and stiffness. This conservative approach provides a straightforward way to engineer timber members for specified fire resistance periods.

Research published in Fire Technology (Springer) assessed Eurocode 5's charring rate methods against experimental data and found the approach reliable for standard fire exposures. Charring rates vary somewhat by species—hardwoods with density exceeding 450 kg/m³ char at approximately 0.5 mm/min, while lighter softwoods may reach 0.65-0.7 mm/min—but the variation is well characterized and accounted for in design calculations.

Timber vs steel vs concrete in fire

The comparison between structural materials in fire conditions reveals a counterintuitive hierarchy. Steel, despite being non-combustible, can be more dangerous than timber in a fire. Concrete performs well but carries trade-offs. Understanding the mechanisms explains why.

Steel begins losing structural strength at approximately 300°C, according to the American Institute of Steel Construction. At 550°C, steel retains only about 60% of its yield strength and 45% of its stiffness. At 593°C, it has lost roughly half its load-bearing capacity—the threshold used in standard fire resistance tests. Steel conducts heat efficiently throughout its cross-section, meaning the entire member heats uniformly. When the critical temperature is reached, failure can be sudden and catastrophic: a steel beam can buckle or a column can collapse without visible warning.

This is why steel structures require fireproofing—intumescent coatings, spray-on cementitious insulation, or encasement in concrete or plasterboard. Unprotected structural steel in a fully developed fire can fail within 15-20 minutes. The non-combustible material paradoxically requires more fire protection than the combustible one.

Large section glulam members will not burn out by themselves but need an external flame source to continue burning. The wood below the charred level will be cool and retain 85 to 90 per cent of its structural integrity.

International Timber, fire performance analysis

Concrete performs well in fire due to its low thermal conductivity and high thermal mass. However, concrete can experience explosive spalling at high temperatures when moisture trapped within the material vaporizes and creates internal pressure. Reinforced concrete's fire resistance depends on the cover depth protecting the steel reinforcement—if spalling exposes the reinforcement, the concrete member loses capacity rapidly. Concrete does not burn, but it does degrade, and repair after significant fire exposure is often impractical.

Timber occupies a unique position. While combustible, its failure mode is gradual and predictable. The charring rate is measurable in real time—firefighters can assess remaining structural capacity by observing char depth. Large timber members self-insulate through charring and, critically, will not sustain combustion without an external flame source. When the fire source is removed, large-section timber typically stops burning on its own.

Fire ratings: what REI 120 means for your home

The European classification system for fire resistance, defined in EN 13501-2, evaluates building elements against three performance criteria. R (Résistance) measures load-bearing capacity—the element must not collapse. E (Étanchéité) measures integrity—flames and hot gases must not breach the compartment boundary. I (Isolation) measures insulation—the unexposed side must not exceed 140°C average or 180°C at any point.

A rating of REI 120 means the building element maintains all three functions for at least 120 minutes during a standard fire test. This is the benchmark for many European residential requirements and represents a substantial safety margin for occupant evacuation and firefighting response.

Mass timber products achieve these ratings through a combination of material properties and design. Stora Enso's CLT panels of 150 mm or greater have been classified to REI standards per EN 13501-2. In one notable test, a 5-ply CLT wall panel endured temperatures exceeding 980°C for over 3 hours and 6 minutes—far exceeding the 2-hour standard. Glulam columns have achieved a 3-hour fire resistance rating in independent third-party testing, meeting the most stringent building code requirements.

The classification system ranges from 15 to 240 minutes. For residential construction, REI 60 to REI 90 is typically required depending on the jurisdiction and building height. Mass timber can achieve these ratings through section sizing alone—without any additional fire protection—provided the timber elements are dimensioned to allow for the calculated charring depth plus the zero-strength layer while still carrying the design load.

Key Takeaway

Fire resistance is not about whether a material burns—it is about how long the building element maintains its structural function. A non-combustible material that loses strength rapidly (unprotected steel) provides less fire resistance than a combustible material that degrades slowly and predictably (mass timber). This is why building codes focus on tested performance, not material combustibility alone.

Self-extinguishment: when timber stops burning

One of the most significant research developments in timber fire engineering is the understanding of self-extinguishment—the conditions under which mass timber stops burning after the fire source is consumed or removed. This property has profound implications for fire safety in timber buildings, because it determines whether a structure continues to burn after the contents fire has exhausted its fuel.

Research published in Fire Safety Journal (ScienceDirect) established the conditions for CLT self-extinguishment. When the external heat flux on the timber surface drops below approximately 44.5 kW/m², and the mass loss rate falls below 3.65 g/s/m², flaming combustion ceases. In practical terms, once the room's combustible contents—furniture, possessions, finishes—have burned out and the heat flux drops below the critical threshold, the CLT panels stop burning on their own.

Several conditions support self-extinguishment. The char layer must remain intact and not delaminate. Lamella thickness must be sufficient—thicker lamellae resist delamination better. Airflow over the smouldering surface must be limited to approximately 0.5 m/s. And adhesive type matters: some adhesives maintain bond strength at elevated temperatures, preventing the char layer from falling away and exposing fresh wood beneath.

Delamination: the key variable

The primary risk factor for self-extinguishment failure is delamination—when the adhesive between CLT lamellae fails at elevated temperature, causing charred layers to detach and expose fresh, uncharred wood to the fire. This effectively resets the charring process and can prevent self-extinguishment. Research from the ARC Future Timber Hub is establishing full-scale design criteria for self-extinguishment that account for delamination, encapsulation failure, and rate of timber surface exposure.

European manufacturers including BIOBUILDS' CLT suppliers specify adhesives tested to maintain bond integrity under fire conditions. The second-generation Eurocodes, updated in 2025, incorporate refined guidance on delamination behaviour and self-extinguishment criteria, reflecting the growing body of full-scale fire test data.

European building codes and timber fire design

Eurocode 5 (EN 1995) is the European standard governing the design of timber structures, with Part 1-2 specifically addressing structural fire design. The standard provides calculation methods for determining the fire resistance of timber elements using the reduced cross-section approach, validated charring rates for different wood types, and design rules for connections, which are often the critical detail in fire resistance.

European building regulations have progressively expanded the permitted use of timber in larger and taller buildings. The 2021 International Building Code introduced construction types IV-A, IV-B, and IV-C specifically for tall mass timber, allowing timber buildings up to 18 storeys with varying degrees of required encapsulation and permitted exposed timber. Canada permits 12-storey mass timber buildings under its 2020 code and is extending this to 18 storeys in the 2025 edition. In Europe, individual countries set height limits through national building regulations, with many now allowing 8-10 storeys and some permitting taller structures through performance-based design.

The fires that have caused significant damage to timber buildings occurred during construction, before sprinkler systems and fire compartmentation were operational. This underscores that the riskiest period for any building—timber, steel, or concrete—is the construction phase, and it highlights the advantage of factory-built modular construction where fire-sensitive assembly work occurs in controlled environments rather than on exposed sites.

Germany and Austria: specific requirements

Germany's building regulations (Musterbauordnung and state-level Landesbauordnungen) have historically been conservative regarding timber construction, but recent revisions reflect the engineering evidence. Multi-storey timber buildings are now permitted in most German states, typically requiring REI 60 or REI 90 depending on building class. Austria, through its OIB guidelines (Österreichisches Institut für Bautechnik), has been somewhat more progressive, with a strong tradition of timber construction in Alpine regions and building regulations that accommodate performance-based timber fire design.

Romania's building regulations (Normativ P118) set fire resistance requirements based on building category and occupancy. Residential timber-frame buildings of moderate height comply through standard fire engineering approaches, with factory-built modular construction offering inherent advantages in quality control and fire compartmentation.

Fire protection layers in modern timber homes

While mass timber provides inherent fire resistance through charring, modern timber homes incorporate multiple additional protection layers that work together as a comprehensive fire safety system. No single element works alone—the effectiveness comes from redundancy.

Encapsulation

Gypsum plasterboard is the most common encapsulation material for timber structures. Research published in Fire Technology found that 15 mm fire-rated gypsum plasterboard without joints delays the onset of charring in CLT panels by 30 minutes. Even with joints, the delay is approximately 25 minutes. This means the timber structure doesn't begin charring until the plasterboard protection has failed—adding 25-30 minutes of fire resistance before the timber's own charring resistance even begins.

When building codes require full encapsulation with gypsum board, mass timber can achieve near-damage-free performance during a contents-fire burnout event. The gypsum absorbs heat through dehydration of its crystal water, the plasterboard eventually fails, and then the timber's charring mechanism takes over—creating a sequential protection system with substantial total fire resistance.

Compartmentation

Fire compartmentation—dividing a building into fire-resistant zones that prevent fire spread from room to room and floor to floor—is fundamental to fire safety in all building types. In timber construction, compartment walls and floors are engineered to achieve the required REI rating, ensuring that a fire in one compartment remains contained while occupants in other areas evacuate safely. Factory-built modular homes offer a significant advantage here: each module is constructed as a self-contained compartment with fire-rated boundaries that are assembled and quality-verified before leaving the factory.

Detection and suppression

Smoke detection, alarm systems, and in many jurisdictions automatic sprinkler systems provide active fire protection that complements the passive resistance of the timber structure. The combination of early detection (giving occupants maximum evacuation time), active suppression (controlling or extinguishing fires before structural involvement), and passive resistance (maintaining structural integrity if fire does develop) creates a defence-in-depth approach that far exceeds any single protection measure.

Key Takeaway

Modern timber homes do not rely on the timber's charring resistance alone. The fire safety system includes gypsum encapsulation (delaying charring by 25-30 minutes), fire-rated compartmentation (containing fire spread), smoke detection, and often sprinkler systems. These layers work in sequence: detection alerts occupants, suppression controls the fire, encapsulation delays structural involvement, and the timber's charring resistance provides the final safety margin.

Structural durability: why timber homes last centuries

Fire resistance addresses one concern; longevity addresses another. How long will a timber-frame home actually last? The historical evidence across Europe provides a compelling answer.

Timber-frame buildings dating to the 12th century remain standing throughout Europe. Many half-timbered structures in Germany, France, and England are 500-700 years old and still occupied. Oak-framed buildings routinely survive 200 to 500 years under proper maintenance, according to structural assessments of historic buildings. The natural tannins in oak serve as a protective barrier against environmental degradation and pests, contributing to extraordinary longevity. The oldest known timber-frame structure still in use, the Jokhang Monastery in Lhasa, dates to the 7th century—over 1,300 years old.

Modern engineered timber products are expected to match or exceed these lifespans. A well-built timber-frame home can comfortably last over 100 years with standard maintenance, and there is no inherent reason—given appropriate moisture management—why it should not last considerably longer. By comparison, conventional site-built homes using lightweight framing have typical anticipated lifespans of 50-60 years before major structural work is needed.

What affects timber durability

The factors that shorten timber lifespan are well understood and entirely manageable:

  • Moisture: Persistent moisture causes rot. The solution is straightforward: design for drainage, maintain the building envelope, and control interior humidity. Passivhaus-certified homes with mechanical ventilation naturally maintain optimal humidity levels of 30-55%, reducing moisture-related degradation risk.
  • Pests: Termites and wood-boring insects can damage untreated timber. Modern structural timber is treated during manufacturing to resist pest attack. In Central and Northern Europe, termite risk is minimal compared to tropical or subtropical regions.
  • UV exposure: Sunlight degrades exposed timber surfaces over time. This affects appearance rather than structural integrity, and is addressed through appropriate surface treatments or cladding that shields the structural timber from direct weather exposure.
  • Quality of construction: Precision joinery and correct detailing—particularly at connections, junctions, and points where different materials meet—determine long-term performance. Factory-built construction provides inherent advantages in this regard, with every connection executed under controlled conditions to documented tolerances.

The Passivhaus fire safety advantage

Passivhaus certification creates fire safety benefits that are rarely discussed but practically significant. The same design principles that achieve 90% energy savings also improve fire performance in several ways.

Airtightness limits oxygen supply. A Passivhaus-certified building achieves air change rates of 0.6 ACH or less at 50 Pa pressure. This exceptional airtightness means that in a fire scenario, the supply of fresh oxygen to the fire is restricted compared to a leaky conventional building. Fires in well-sealed environments develop differently—often more slowly—than in ventilation-rich spaces. While airtightness alone is not a fire safety strategy, it provides a measurable advantage in limiting fire growth rate.

Mechanical ventilation with heat recovery (MVHR) systems include automatic fire dampers that close when smoke or elevated temperature is detected, preventing fire and smoke from spreading through ductwork between rooms or floors. The Zehnder MVHR systems specified in BIOBUILDS homes incorporate these fire safety features as standard.

Continuous air barrier construction, achieved through products like Pro Clima's airtightness membranes, creates fire-rated envelope details. The meticulous attention to sealing every junction and penetration that characterizes Passivhaus construction also eliminates the hidden air pathways through which fire and smoke can travel undetected in conventional buildings.

High-performance insulation using materials like STEICO wood fibre products provides thermal protection that also slows heat transfer in a fire scenario. The same insulation that keeps winter heat in and summer heat out also impedes fire progression through the building envelope.

Key Takeaway

Passivhaus construction creates a fire safety co-benefit: superior airtightness limits oxygen supply to fires, MVHR fire dampers prevent smoke spread through ductwork, and continuous air barriers eliminate hidden fire pathways. The same engineering that delivers 90% energy savings simultaneously improves fire safety performance—an advantage unique to high-performance timber buildings.

The question is not whether wood burns—it does. The question is whether a timber-frame building provides adequate structural fire resistance, occupant safety, and long-term durability. The evidence answers clearly on all three counts. Timber chars at a predictable 0.65 mm/min, retaining 85-90% of its structural strength behind the char layer. Mass timber achieves REI 120 fire ratings under European standards. No code-compliant mass timber building has suffered catastrophic fire failure. And timber-frame buildings across Europe have demonstrated lifespans measured in centuries.
