The Passive House standard achieves its remarkable 90% energy reduction through two interdependent systems working in concert: an airtight building envelope that prevents uncontrolled air leakage, and mechanical ventilation with heat recovery that provides fresh air while recapturing the heat it contains. Neither system works without the other. An airtight building without proper ventilation would trap stale air and moisture. Ventilation without airtightness would waste energy trying to heat or cool air that escapes through gaps in the structure.

Understanding how these systems function together explains not only why Passive Houses use so little energy, but also why they deliver better indoor air quality, more stable temperatures, and quieter interiors than conventionally built homes. For homeowners considering a Passive House, this guide covers what the requirements mean in practice, how they're achieved and tested, and what ongoing maintenance involves.

Why airtightness matters for energy and comfort

In modern buildings, 35% of heat loss can be attributed to ventilation and air leakage. Every gap in a building's envelope—around windows, at floor-wall junctions, through electrical penetrations—allows warm air to escape in winter and enter in summer. This uncontrolled air exchange forces heating and cooling systems to work harder, wastes energy, creates drafts, and introduces outdoor pollutants and humidity into living spaces.

The Passive House standard requires air leakage of no more than 0.6 air changes per hour at 50 Pascals (0.6 ACH@50Pa). To understand what this means: at 50 Pascals pressure—roughly equivalent to a 32 km/h wind blowing on all sides of the building simultaneously—the building can lose no more than 60% of its total air volume per hour through leakage. This is approximately 10-25 times tighter than conventional construction, which typically measures 5-15 ACH@50Pa.

According to the Passive House Institute, this target has been met by over 25,000 certified Passive House buildings in Europe, as well as buildings certified in the U.S. The standard represents the most aggressive airtightness requirement in mainstream construction, yet it has proven achievable across climate zones, building types, and construction methods.

Beyond energy: comfort and durability

Airtightness delivers benefits beyond energy savings. Drafts—the uncomfortable sensation of moving air—require temperature differentials and air movement. In an airtight building, neither exists in uncontrolled form. Surface temperatures remain within 1°C of room temperature throughout the envelope, eliminating the cold spots near windows and exterior walls common in conventional buildings.

Moisture control improves dramatically as well. In conventional buildings, warm moist air migrating through gaps in the envelope reaches cold surfaces where it condenses, creating conditions for mold growth and structural damage. An airtight envelope with controlled ventilation manages moisture through the MVHR system instead, maintaining optimal humidity levels year-round.

The building envelope of a Passive House must be designed and constructed so that air may only enter through the ventilation system. Uncontrolled infiltration compromises comfort, energy performance, and long-term durability.

The blower door test explained

Every Passive House must pass a blower door test to verify its airtightness. The test uses a calibrated fan installed in an exterior doorway to either pressurize or depressurize the building to 50 Pascals. By measuring how much air the fan must move to maintain this pressure differential, technicians can calculate the building's total air leakage rate.

According to 475 High Performance Building Supply, the Passive House Institute requires both a depressurization test and a pressurization test, with the result being the average of the two ACH values. The lowest pressure point allowed is 10 Pascals, or at least 5 times the baseline pressure.

Testing protocol for Passive House certification

Air leakage testing for Passive House is based on the international standard ISO 9972 with additional requirements specific to Passive House projects. The testing must be performed at two stages of construction:

  1. Intermediate test: When the airtight layer is still accessible for remedial work. This allows identification and repair of leaks before finishes are applied.
  2. Final test: At overall completion, to verify the finished building meets the 0.6 ACH@50Pa requirement.

For EnerPHit retrofit projects, if the blower door test results exceed 0.6 ACH@50Pa, the team should continue improving the results. If the test can be brought below 1.0 ACH@50Pa but not below 0.6 ACH@50Pa, a declaration may be submitted with certification, acknowledging the practical constraints of existing construction.

Achieving 0.6 ACH in practice

The 0.6 ACH@50Pa target requires meticulous attention to every penetration, junction, and service entry in the building envelope. Air leakage typically occurs at predictable locations: window and door frames, floor-wall junctions, roof-wall connections, electrical outlets and switches, plumbing penetrations, and HVAC duct connections.

The airtight layer concept

Every Passive House includes a continuous airtight layer—typically a membrane or sealed sheathing—that wraps the entire heated volume without interruption. Critical junction details

The membrane alone doesn't create airtightness—the connections between membrane sections and between membrane and other building elements determine success or failure. Common failure points include:

  • Window and door frames: Require airtight tape or sealant between frame and membrane
  • Electrical penetrations: Need sealed junction boxes or membrane grommets
  • Service entries: Water, gas, electrical mains require specialized sealing solutions
  • Floor-wall junctions: Must maintain membrane continuity across the joint
  • Roof-wall connections: Often the most complex junction requiring careful detailing
Key Insight

How mechanical ventilation with heat recovery works

An MVHR system continuously extracts stale air from wet rooms (kitchens, bathrooms) and supplies fresh filtered air to living spaces (bedrooms, living rooms). Before the extracted air exits the building, it passes through a heat exchanger where it transfers its thermal energy to the incoming fresh air. The two airstreams never mix—only heat transfers between them.

Energy performance implications

According to the Passivhaus MVHR energy analysis, a Passive House certified MVHR with 80% efficiency could achieve a coefficient of performance (COP) of 15—meaning for every unit of electricity consumed, 15 units of heat energy are recovered. An efficient MVHR system reduces heating demand from approximately 35 kWh/m²a to 15 kWh/m²a at equal ventilation rates compared to natural ventilation.

The system operates on minimal electricity. Certified units typically consume 0.35-0.45 Wh/m³ of air moved. For a typical home ventilating at 150 m³/hour, this translates to approximately 50-70 watts continuous draw, or roughly €50-80 annually in electricity costs—a fraction of the heating energy saved.

Summer bypass operation

In summer when outdoor temperatures are pleasant or when cooling is needed, MVHR systems include a bypass function that routes incoming air around the heat exchanger. This allows the system to provide ventilation without unwanted heat recovery. Advanced units like the Zehnder ComfoAir Flex feature 100% modulating summer bypass with adaptive comfort technology that automatically adjusts based on indoor and outdoor conditions.

Indoor air quality benefits

Perhaps the most underappreciated benefit of the airtightness-MVHR combination is dramatically improved indoor air quality. Research consistently shows that mechanically ventilated buildings outperform naturally ventilated ones across multiple air quality metrics.

CO₂ concentration management

A review of Passive House indoor environments found that 74% of Passive House dwellings never exceeded 1,400 ppm CO₂. By contrast, naturally ventilated new Scottish homes averaged approximately 1,500 ppm overnight with peaks reaching 5,000 ppm. Research cited by MDPI Buildings found that employing mechanical rather than natural ventilation in schools lowers CO₂ levels by 20-30%.

The significance of CO₂ levels extends beyond comfort. Research has established associations between elevated CO₂ and reduced cognitive function, with 1,400 ppm representing a threshold for approximately 15% cognitive function reduction. Schools studies found statistically significant associations between high CO₂ levels and lack of concentration in children.

Pollutant filtration

MVHR systems include filters on the supply air stream—typically F7 grade or higher for Passive House applications. These filters remove:

  • Particulate matter: PM2.5 and PM10 particles reduced by approximately 50% compared to naturally ventilated buildings
  • Pollen: Significant benefit for allergy sufferers, with pollen counts approaching zero in supply air
  • Outdoor pollution: NO₂, ozone, and traffic-related pollutants substantially reduced

Swedish research comparing 20 passive houses to 21 conventional new houses found NO₂ levels 17% lower in passive houses (10 vs 12 μg/m³), formaldehyde 31% lower (11 vs 16 μg/m³), and ozone 12% lower (9.7 vs 11 μg/m³).

Research Finding

A Dutch study tracking 800 employees found workers in healthy green buildings experienced 2% lower sick leave prevalence and 21.6% fewer Sick Building Syndrome symptoms. While this data comes from office settings, the underlying air quality principles apply equally to residential buildings with MVHR systems.

Humidity control

Passive Houses achieve the optimal 30-60% relative humidity range consistently through controlled ventilation. UK studies showed 75% of MVHR-equipped homes met target humidity levels below 7 g/kg absolute humidity, with bathroom moisture clearing within 3 hours. This controlled humidity mitigates both pathogen spread (associated with high humidity) and virus transmission plus skin problems (associated with low humidity).

Ongoing maintenance requirements

The airtight envelope itself requires no maintenance—the membranes and seals are designed for the building's full lifespan. The MVHR system does require regular attention, but the requirements are straightforward.

Filter replacement

The supply air filters require replacement every 6-12 months depending on local air quality and occupancy. Filters cost €50-100 annually and can be changed by homeowners in most units—no technician required. Most modern MVHR units include filter change indicators that alert occupants when replacement is needed.

System cleaning

Supply and extract valves accumulate dust over time and benefit from periodic cleaning—wiping with a damp cloth once or twice per year. The heat exchanger itself should be inspected annually and cleaned if necessary, though in most installations significant buildup is rare.

Professional servicing

Annual professional inspection is recommended but not strictly required for residential systems. A technician can verify fan operation, check ductwork connections, measure airflow balance, and confirm heat recovery efficiency. Zehnder and other manufacturers offer service contracts for comprehensive maintenance.

Common concerns addressed

"You can't open windows in a Passive House"

This persistent myth is simply false. Windows in Passive Houses function normally and can be opened at any time. The MVHR system provides continuous filtered fresh air without relying on window opening, but occupants retain full control. The difference is that thermal comfort doesn't depend on remembering to open and close windows—the building maintains optimal conditions automatically regardless of window state.

"The ventilation system is noisy"

Properly designed and installed MVHR systems are inaudible during normal operation. Noise complaints typically indicate either undersized ductwork (causing high air velocities), poor unit location, or inadequate acoustic isolation at installation. Systems like the Zehnder ComfoAir range are designed for 25-30 dB(A) operation—equivalent to a whisper.

"What happens during a power outage?"

The MVHR system stops during power outages, but the building remains safe. The thermal mass and superinsulation maintain comfortable temperatures for extended periods without active heating or cooling. For ventilation, windows can be opened as in any building. In practice, power outages lasting more than a few hours are rare, and the building's thermal stability means occupants rarely notice brief interruptions.

"Airtight buildings trap pollutants"

The opposite is true. Natural ventilation relies on random air exchange through gaps—which may or may not provide adequate fresh air depending on weather, wind direction, and occupant behavior. MVHR provides consistent, controlled ventilation at rates designed for the building's occupancy, with filtration that natural ventilation cannot match. Research consistently shows lower pollutant levels in mechanically ventilated buildings.

"The technology is too complex"

Modern MVHR units are designed for minimal user interaction. Set-and-forget operation handles most situations automatically. Controls allow occupants to boost ventilation when cooking or showering, reduce it when away, or switch to bypass mode in summer—but none of these adjustments are required for normal operation. The system complexity is in the engineering; the user experience is simple.
