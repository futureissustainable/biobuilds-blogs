Sound insulation is one of the first concerns people raise about timber-frame construction—and one of the most misunderstood. The assumption runs like this: wood is light, light materials transmit more sound, therefore timber homes must be noisy. This reasoning contains a grain of physics truth about bare materials, but it misrepresents how modern timber-frame buildings are actually designed and built. A properly engineered timber-frame wall assembly is not a bare panel of wood. It is a multi-layer system of structure, insulation, membranes, and finishes—each contributing to acoustic performance in ways that, when combined, deliver sound insulation exceeding regulatory requirements across Europe.

Understanding how this works requires looking at two things: the physics of sound transmission through building elements, and the specific design strategies that make timber-frame construction acoustically competitive with—and in some configurations superior to—conventional concrete and steel.

Why sound insulation matters for health

Noise is not merely an annoyance. The World Health Organization’s Environmental Noise Guidelines for the European Region (2018) identify environmental noise as one of the top environmental hazards to physical and mental health in Europe. Chronic exposure leads to sleep disturbance, cardiovascular stress, cognitive impairment in children, and measurable reductions in quality of life.

The WHO recommends that indoor bedroom noise should not exceed 30 dB at night for healthy sleep. Road traffic noise should stay below 53 dB (Lden) at the building facade. An estimated 145 million Europeans—over 30% of the EU population—are exposed to transportation noise levels above WHO thresholds, according to the European Environment Agency (2024).

The building envelope—walls, floors, roof, and windows—is the primary barrier between occupants and environmental noise, and its acoustic performance directly affects long-term wellbeing.

How sound travels through buildings

Sound reaches building interiors through two distinct mechanisms, each requiring different design responses. Understanding this distinction is essential for evaluating any construction system’s acoustic performance.

Airborne sound

Airborne sound travels as pressure waves through the air—speech, music, traffic noise, aircraft. When these waves strike a wall or floor, they cause the surface to vibrate, which re-radiates sound on the opposite side. Airborne sound insulation is measured in Europe using the weighted sound reduction index (Rw), defined by EN ISO 717-1. A higher Rw means better insulation. The key physical principle governing airborne sound is the mass law: heavier building elements block more airborne sound because they resist the pressure waves more effectively.

Impact sound

Impact sound (also called structure-borne sound) originates from physical contact with the building structure—footsteps, dropped objects, vibrating appliances. The impact creates vibrations that travel through the solid structure and radiate as audible sound in adjacent rooms. Impact sound is measured using the weighted normalised impact sound pressure level (Ln,w), defined by EN ISO 717-2. Unlike airborne sound, a lower Ln,w indicates better performance.

Flanking transmission

The third and often most problematic sound path is flanking transmission—sound that bypasses the primary separating element by travelling through connected structures. A wall may achieve excellent laboratory ratings, but if sound travels around it through the floor slab, ceiling joists, or service penetrations, the in-situ performance will be significantly worse. Flanking is particularly relevant in lightweight construction, where continuous structural connections can create efficient sound paths between rooms.

Key Takeaway

Effective sound insulation must address all three transmission paths: airborne sound through mass and sealing, impact sound through isolation and resilient layers, and flanking through structural decoupling and careful junction detailing. A single weakness in any path compromises the entire assembly.

Timber vs. concrete and steel: the acoustic comparison

The honest starting point: a bare timber element provides less airborne sound insulation than a bare concrete element of similar thickness. A 150 mm concrete slab weighs approximately 360 kg/m² and achieves an STC of around 53. A 175 mm (7-ply) bare CLT panel weighs approximately 86 kg/m² and achieves an STC of approximately 44, according to acoustic testing compiled by WoodWorks. Concrete’s mass advantage is real.

But buildings are not built from bare elements. The relevant comparison is between complete wall and floor assemblies—the full build-ups that occupants actually live with. And here the picture changes substantially.

European sound insulation standards

Sound insulation requirements for residential buildings in Europe are governed by national building regulations that reference the EN ISO 717 measurement framework. The standard defines two key descriptors: Rw (or R’w in field conditions) for airborne sound, and Ln,w (or L’n,w) for impact sound. Despite this common measurement framework, actual requirements vary significantly across European countries.

Research published in Applied Acoustics surveying 35 European countries found that seven different descriptors for airborne sound and five for impact sound are currently in use. The difference between the strictest and most lenient minimum requirements is 10 dB for airborne sound and 20 dB for impact sound. Austria and Scotland have the most stringent requirements.

Country Airborne sound (min.) Impact sound (max.) Descriptor used
Austria 55 dB 48 dB R’w / L’n,w
Germany 53 dB 53 dB R’w / L’n,w
UK (England & Wales) 43 dB 64 dB DnT,w / L’nT,w
Sweden 52 dB 56 dB DnT,w + C50-3150
Romania 51 dB 55 dB R’w / L’n,w

Sweden stands out as the only European country to include low-frequency spectrum adaptation values from 50 Hz in its building code—a critical detail because low-frequency impact sound (heavy footsteps, bass music) is the most common acoustic complaint in lightweight buildings. Most other countries’ standards only measure performance down to 100 Hz, potentially missing the very frequencies that generate occupant dissatisfaction.

The key implication for timber-frame construction: the assembly must be designed not just to meet the Rw single-number rating, but to perform adequately across the full frequency spectrum—including the low frequencies where lightweight construction faces its greatest challenge.

Why timber-frame assemblies work acoustically

The acoustic success of timber-frame construction relies on principles that are, counterintuitively, enhanced by the material’s lightness. While mass helps block airborne sound, decoupling and absorption are equally important mechanisms—and timber-frame systems excel at both.

Decoupling: breaking the sound path

When two surfaces of a wall are structurally connected, vibrations transfer efficiently between them. This is why a single solid wall—regardless of material—transmits more sound than two separate leaves with an air gap. Timber-frame construction naturally accommodates double-stud and staggered-stud configurations that decouple the inner and outer wall surfaces. Each leaf vibrates independently, dramatically reducing sound transmission through the assembly.

According to the Passive House Plus guide to structural timber, double-stud walls are common in Passivhaus construction because they eliminate thermal bridging—the ratio of timber to insulation drops from approximately 25% in a single-stud wall to 15% in a double-stud configuration. This same structural separation that improves thermal performance also provides superior acoustic decoupling. Two independent stud walls with an insulated cavity between them create a mass-air-mass system that outperforms a single heavy wall of equivalent total mass.

Absorption: converting sound to heat

The deep insulation cavities required for Passivhaus-level thermal performance—typically 300–400 mm of wall depth—also serve as acoustic absorption chambers. Fibrous insulation materials within these cavities convert sound energy into negligible quantities of heat through friction as sound waves pass through the fibrous matrix. This absorption is particularly effective at mid and high frequencies, complementing the mass-based attenuation that addresses lower frequencies.

Airtightness: sealing every gap

Sound follows the path of least resistance. Even a 1 mm gap under a door can reduce a wall’s acoustic performance by 10 dB or more. The airtightness required by Passivhaus certification—below 0.6 air changes per hour at 50 Pa—eliminates the gaps, cracks, and service penetrations through which sound most readily travels. An airtight building envelope is inherently a sound-tight building envelope.

Key Takeaway

Timber-frame construction excels at decoupling and absorption—two of the three fundamental mechanisms of sound insulation. When combined with the airtightness that Passivhaus certification demands, the result is a building system that addresses all three sound transmission paths simultaneously: mass (through layered assemblies), decoupling (through separated stud structures), and sealing (through the airtight membrane).

Wood fibre insulation: thermal and acoustic performance

The choice of insulation material affects acoustic performance as much as thermal performance. Wood fibre insulation—the type used by STEICO, a BIOBUILDS partner—combines properties that make it particularly effective for sound insulation in timber-frame walls and floors.

Wood fibre insulation achieves Noise Reduction Coefficient (NRC) ratings of 0.6–0.85, comparable to mineral wool. The NRC measures how much sound a material absorbs on a scale from 0 (total reflection) to 1 (total absorption). The high absorption results from wood fibre’s combination of high bulk density and porous internal structure—sound energy enters the fibrous matrix and is converted to heat through internal friction.

What distinguishes wood fibre from lighter insulation materials like expanded polystyrene or even some mineral wools is its density. As the acoustic principle states: in sound insulation, the weight of the building component is a primary factor alongside its composition. Wood fibre boards can achieve densities of 160–240 kg/m³—significantly heavier than most competing insulation products. This mass adds to the overall assembly weight, improving airborne sound insulation in accordance with the mass law, while the fibrous structure simultaneously provides absorption.

For floor assemblies, STEICO Ecosilent—a wood fibre impact sound underlay—improves impact sound insulation by up to 19 dB. STEICO’s therm SD board serves as a classified impact sound insulation board for dry construction applications. These products address the specific low-frequency impact sound challenge that lightweight floor systems face, providing resilient separation between the floor finish and the structural timber beneath.

The dual-performance advantage

Conventional construction often treats thermal and acoustic insulation as separate problems requiring separate materials. Wood fibre insulation addresses both simultaneously. The same material properties—high density, fibrous structure, thermal mass—that deliver a wall U-value of 0.10–0.15 W/m²K (Passivhaus-grade) also deliver excellent acoustic absorption. This eliminates the need for additional acoustic treatment layers, simplifying the wall build-up and reducing material usage while achieving superior performance on both metrics.

The Passivhaus acoustic advantage

The Passivhaus standard was designed for energy efficiency, not acoustics. But the engineering requirements that achieve extreme energy performance create acoustic benefits that are substantial, consistent, and—unlike in conventional construction—inherent rather than added.

Thick, continuous insulation

Passivhaus walls typically require 300–400 mm of insulation depth to achieve U-values between 0.08 and 0.15 W/m²K. This depth of insulation provides far more acoustic absorption than the 100–150 mm found in standard construction. The insulation is continuous—wrapping the entire building without breaks—which eliminates the acoustic weak points that occur at junctions in conventional cavity wall construction.

Extreme airtightness

Passivhaus requires airtightness below 0.6 ACH at 50 Pa—approximately 30 times more airtight than a conventional home. Every junction, penetration, and service opening is sealed with tape, membrane, or gasket. This level of sealing eliminates the flanking paths through which sound most efficiently travels. A blower door test doesn’t just verify energy performance—it effectively verifies acoustic integrity.

High-performance windows

Triple-glazed windows with Rw values of 32–38 dB are standard in Passivhaus construction. These provide substantially better noise reduction than the double-glazed units typical in standard construction (Rw 25–30 dB). Windows are often the weakest acoustic element in any facade, so upgrading from double to triple glazing can make a noticeable difference to interior noise levels, particularly in urban environments or near roads.

Mechanical ventilation instead of open windows

Passivhaus homes use mechanical ventilation with heat recovery (MVHR) instead of opening windows for fresh air. This is acoustically significant because an open window reduces the facade’s sound insulation to near zero at the opening. MVHR systems deliver filtered fresh air through ducted supply points while keeping the building envelope completely sealed—maintaining full acoustic performance 24 hours a day, including at night when WHO bedroom noise limits apply.

Practical acoustic design strategies

Achieving excellent acoustic performance in timber-frame construction requires attention at the design stage, not as an afterthought. The following strategies, applied systematically, deliver sound insulation that meets or exceeds European regulatory requirements across all frequency ranges.

Wall assemblies

The most effective timber-frame wall for sound insulation uses a double-stud or split-stud configuration. Two independent rows of studs, separated by an air gap filled with fibrous insulation, create a decoupled system where each wall leaf vibrates independently. According to testing reported in Passive House Plus, this approach achieves 63–69 dB in party wall applications—exceeding the 53 dB regulatory target by 10–16 dB.

Key details for wall assemblies:

  • Stud separation: A minimum 20–50 mm gap between inner and outer stud lines prevents structural sound bridging.
  • Cavity insulation: Fill the entire cavity with fibrous insulation (wood fibre, mineral wool). Partially filled cavities create resonances that can actually worsen performance at certain frequencies.
  • Board mass: Use dense board materials (gypsum fibreboard, cement board) on the inner face to add mass. Two layers of 12.5 mm gypsum board significantly outperform a single layer.
  • Airtight membrane: Seal the membrane continuously at all junctions, penetrations, and connections. Any unsealed gap is a sound path.

Floor assemblies

Floors face the dual challenge of airborne and impact sound. A floating floor system—where the floor finish sits on a resilient layer rather than being fixed directly to the structure—is the primary strategy for impact sound control.

  • Resilient underlay: Wood fibre or rubber underlayments (such as STEICO Ecosilent) between the structural floor and the walking surface isolate impact vibrations. This alone can improve impact sound performance by 15–19 dB.
  • Added mass: A thin concrete or gypsum screed (25–50 mm) on top of the resilient layer adds mass for airborne sound insulation while maintaining impact isolation below.
  • Suspended ceiling: On the underside, a ceiling suspended from resilient hangers with insulation in the cavity addresses both airborne and impact sound from above. The air gap and insulation provide additional absorption, while the resilient hangers break the structural connection.

Junction detailing

The junction between wall and floor is where flanking transmission occurs. In timber-frame construction, specific details prevent sound from bypassing the primary separating elements:

  • Resilient connections: Use acoustic resilient strips at every structural junction to prevent direct vibration transfer.
  • Service penetrations: Seal every pipe, cable, and duct penetration through acoustic separating elements with flexible sealant. A single unsealed penetration can reduce wall performance by several dB.
  • Structural isolation: Where party walls meet external walls or floor joists, the connection should include a resilient break to prevent flanking through the structure.

Factory precision: the quality variable

Acoustic performance depends critically on build quality. A wall assembly that achieves 65 dB in the laboratory can drop to 50 dB on site if gaps are left unsealed, insulation is poorly fitted, or resilient layers are bridged by fixings. This is where factory manufacturing provides a measurable advantage. Every membrane junction, insulation fit, and resilient connection is executed under controlled conditions, inspected before the module leaves the factory, and protected from weather exposure during construction.

On-site construction introduces variables that laboratory testing doesn’t account for: weather conditions, varying trade skill levels, time pressure, and the difficulty of inspecting concealed elements once finishes are applied. Factory production eliminates these variables systematically, which is why the gap between laboratory ratings and in-situ performance tends to be smaller for factory-built assemblies than for site-built equivalents.

The acoustic evidence for timber-frame construction follows the same pattern as the fire safety, thermal, and sustainability evidence: bare material comparisons understate the performance of complete, engineered assemblies. A 175 mm CLT panel will not match a 150 mm concrete slab on airborne sound insulation. But a properly designed timber-frame Passivhaus wall—with decoupled studs, deep wood fibre insulation, airtight membranes, and factory-precision assembly—achieves 63–69 dB. That exceeds the European regulatory minimum and matches or betters what conventional construction delivers in practice.
