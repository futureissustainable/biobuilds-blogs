Buildings are responsible for 34% of global CO2 emissions, according to the UNEP Global Status Report 2024/2025. Within that figure, embodied carbon—the emissions locked into materials and construction processes—accounts for roughly 9.8 gigatonnes of CO2 annually. Cement and steel alone generate 18% of global emissions. The material you build with is no longer just a structural choice. It's a climate decision.

Timber-frame construction offers a fundamentally different carbon equation. While concrete and steel release CO2 during production, wood absorbs and stores it. This isn't marketing—it's photosynthesis. And as the EU begins mandating whole-life carbon reporting for buildings, the regulatory advantage of timber is about to become a financial one.

The carbon problem in construction

The construction industry's carbon footprint is enormous, and most of it is invisible to homeowners. According to the UNEP Global Status Report for Buildings and Construction 2024/2025, the sector consumed 32% of global energy demand in 2023 and generated a record-breaking 9.8 gigatonnes of CO2.

The problem breaks down into two categories. Operational carbon comes from heating, cooling, and powering buildings over their lifetime. Embodied carbon comes from extracting raw materials, manufacturing building products, transporting them to site, and constructing the building itself. For decades, the industry focused almost exclusively on operational energy. That's changing.

As buildings become more energy-efficient, embodied carbon represents an increasingly large share of their total lifetime emissions. In a Passivhaus-certified building with near-zero operational energy, embodied carbon can account for 50% or more of the building's total life-cycle emissions. The material question becomes the dominant climate question.

Embodied carbon: timber vs concrete vs steel

Timber frames produce approximately 119 kg CO2-equivalent per square metre, compared to 185 kg CO2-eq/m² for concrete frames and 228 kg CO2-eq/m² for steel frames. That's 36% less than concrete and 48% less than steel. These figures come from an assessment of 127 different frame configurations for buildings ranging from 2 to 19 storeys, published in MDPI Buildings (2024).

The gap widens further when you look at specific building types. A comparative analysis of mid-rise structures found that cross-laminated timber (CLT) buildings produced just 26-40 kg CO2-eq/m² at the cradle-to-gate stage, compared to 111-121 kg CO2-eq/m² for reinforced concrete equivalents. That's a reduction of up to 70%.

The reasons are rooted in material science. Cement production requires heating limestone to approximately 1,450°C in kilns, releasing CO2 both from the energy consumed and from the chemical decomposition of calcium carbonate. According to research on embodied carbon, producing a cubic metre of concrete emits around 300 kg of CO2. Steel production is even more carbon-intensive, emitting up to 2 tonnes of CO2 per tonne of steel produced in traditional blast furnace processes.

Timber production, by contrast, is a low-energy process. The raw material extraction stage emits about 12 kg CO2 per cubic metre, according to life-cycle assessment data. Transportation adds approximately 17 kg CO2/m³ for regional supply chains. But the critical difference is that timber carries a carbon credit from the CO2 the tree absorbed while growing—a credit that concrete and steel simply cannot match.

On average, almost 43% of GHG emissions are avoided if reinforced concrete structures are substituted with mass timber alternatives.

A note on recycled steel

Recycled steel narrows the gap. Electric arc furnace (EAF) steelmaking using scrap reduces emissions by roughly 75% compared to primary production. This is a legitimate advancement—but even recycled steel frames typically produce more embodied carbon than timber, and the global steel supply still relies heavily on virgin production. As of 2025, only about 30% of global steel comes from recycled sources.

How wood stores carbon (and for how long)

Each cubic metre of timber stores approximately 0.9 to 1.0 tonnes of CO2. This isn't a theoretical projection—it's the measured result of photosynthesis. Trees convert atmospheric CO2 into cellulose and lignin as they grow, locking carbon into their wood fibre. When that wood is used in construction, the carbon remains stored for the lifetime of the building.

The scale of this storage is significant. According to NC State University research, a five-storey residential timber building can store up to 180 kg of carbon per square metre—three times more than the above-ground biomass of natural forests with high carbon density. The Ascent MKE tower in Milwaukee, at 25 storeys, sequesters approximately 7,200 metric tonnes of CO2.

A typical timber-frame home uses 15-30 cubic metres of structural timber, depending on size and design. That means 15-30 tonnes of CO2 stored in the structure alone, before accounting for timber used in cladding, interior finishes, or insulation. For context, the average European emits approximately 6 tonnes of CO2 per year. A single timber home stores the equivalent of 3-5 years of one person's total emissions.

How long does the carbon stay locked in?

As long as the timber remains intact. Modern timber-frame homes, properly maintained, last 100-200 years. Medieval timber buildings across Europe remain standing after 500+ years. The carbon stored in structural timber isn't released until the wood decomposes or burns—and at end of life, timber can be recycled into new products, extending the storage period further.

This is fundamentally different from carbon offset schemes that depend on future forests growing. The carbon in a timber building is already stored. It's already removed from the atmosphere. The climate benefit is immediate and measurable.

The full life-cycle picture

A fair carbon comparison must look beyond material production to the entire building life cycle, from raw material extraction (stage A1) through construction (A5), operation (B), and end of life (C). When researchers apply this comprehensive approach, timber's advantage typically grows.

An analysis of residential buildings in Germany and Austria between 2006 and 2014 found that timber structures showed 35-56% lower lifecycle GHG emissions in single and two-family houses compared to mineral-material construction. In multi-storey buildings, the reduction ranged from 9-48%, varying with building height and design specifics.

A separate peer-reviewed study found that mass timber buildings lowered global warming potential (GWP) by an estimated 39-51% compared to reinforced concrete and 28-34% compared to structural steel, when accounting for cradle-to-grave system boundaries.

Key Takeaway

The carbon advantage of timber increases as you expand the assessment boundary. At the material level, timber is lower-carbon. At the building level, it stores additional carbon. Over the full life cycle, it avoids emissions from both production and (in Passivhaus buildings) operation. Each layer compounds the benefit.

What about transport emissions?

Transportation adds approximately 17 kg CO2 per cubic metre of timber for regional supply chains. For longer distances, this can rise to 38 kg CO2/m³. These emissions are real and should be minimised through local sourcing. However, they remain a small fraction of the embodied carbon difference between timber and concrete or steel—a gap measured in hundreds of kilograms per square metre, not tens.

European supply chains have an advantage here. Timber-frame manufacturers in Central Europe typically source wood from within a 500-kilometre radius, keeping transport emissions modest. Partners like STEICO and Stora Enso maintain production facilities across the continent.

EU regulation is accelerating the shift

The revised Energy Performance of Buildings Directive (EPBD, EU 2024/1275) transforms embodied carbon from a voluntary consideration into a legal requirement. The directive introduces mandatory whole-life carbon reporting for buildings across the European Union.

From 2028, the life-cycle Global Warming Potential (GWP) will need to be calculated and disclosed in energy performance certificates for all new buildings with a floor area larger than 1,000 m². From 2030, this extends to all new buildings. Member states must also set GWP thresholds based on the Level(s) framework and the EN 15978 standard.

This regulatory shift has significant implications for material choice. When builders must report the carbon footprint of their materials—and eventually meet carbon limits—low-embodied-carbon options like timber gain a structural market advantage. Buildings that would have been built with concrete or steel on autopilot will increasingly face a carbon accounting reckoning.

The EPBD works alongside the European Green Deal, the Construction Products Regulation (which mandates product-level carbon footprint data), and the Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive. Together, these create a regulatory ecosystem where carbon transparency is not optional—it's the law.

What this means for home buyers

For anyone building a new home in the EU after 2030, the carbon footprint of your building will appear on your energy performance certificate. Timber-frame homes will show dramatically lower numbers than concrete or steel equivalents—a visible, verifiable selling point that affects both marketability and potentially property value as carbon-aware buyers enter the market.

The sustainable forestry condition

Timber construction is only carbon-beneficial when the wood comes from sustainably managed forests where harvested trees are replaced. Without this condition, the entire argument collapses. If forests are cleared without replanting, the carbon released from deforestation far exceeds any benefit from using the wood in construction.

Europe's forests provide a strong foundation for sustainable timber sourcing. The EU's forest area has been growing for decades—increasing by roughly 9% since 1990, according to European Environment Agency data. European forestry removes more carbon than it emits, and most EU timber comes from forests managed under FSC or PEFC certification schemes that require sustainable replanting.

The carbon-negative potential of mass timber is realised only when the trees used are harvested from responsibly managed forests where new seedlings replace each harvested tree. Without robust governance, increased demand could exacerbate deforestation.

ScienceDaily, based on Potsdam Institute for Climate Impact Research, 2020

Certification matters. When evaluating a timber-frame home, ask about the chain of custody for the wood. Reputable manufacturers maintain full traceability from forest to factory. Products carrying FSC or PEFC certification provide independently verified assurance that the timber came from well-managed sources.

Can global demand be met sustainably?

Research published in ScienceDaily based on Potsdam Institute modelling estimates that if timber were used in 50% of new buildings over the next 30 years, global annual carbon storage could reach 0.37 billion tonnes of CO2—while simultaneously reducing cumulative emissions from steel and cement manufacturing by at least half. The study concluded this is achievable with existing forest resources, provided sustainable management practices are maintained.

Combining low embodied and operational carbon

The most powerful carbon strategy combines low-embodied-carbon materials with low-operational-carbon design. Timber-frame construction paired with Passivhaus certification achieves exactly this.

Passivhaus-certified buildings reduce heating demand to 15 kWh/m² per year or less—compared to the EU average of 350 kWh/m² per year for existing buildings. That's a reduction of roughly 95% in operational energy for heating and cooling. When this radical efficiency is built into a timber frame that also stores carbon, the result approaches carbon negativity across the full building life cycle.

According to BIOBUILDS, each of their homes saves approximately 337 tonnes of CO2 compared to conventional construction. This figure accounts for carbon stored in the timber structure, the reduced emissions of using 98% organic materials instead of concrete and steel, and the dramatically lower operational energy of Passivhaus-certified design over the building's lifetime.

The combination works because each element addresses a different slice of the carbon pie. Timber handles embodied carbon. Passivhaus handles operational carbon. Together, they target the full lifecycle emissions profile—something neither approach achieves alone.

The factory precision advantage

Factory-built timber homes add another layer of carbon benefit. Controlled manufacturing conditions enable tighter building envelopes with superior airtightness, directly reducing operational energy loss. Modular construction also generates significantly less waste than site-built alternatives—the Modular Building Institute estimates up to 90% waste reduction in some factory settings, which further reduces embodied carbon.

What this means for your next home

The carbon case for timber-frame construction is supported by extensive peer-reviewed research, measured in real buildings, and increasingly reinforced by EU regulation. For families considering a new home, the implications are practical and immediate.

Lower environmental impact. Choosing a timber-frame home over a conventional concrete or masonry structure reduces your building's embodied carbon by approximately one-third to one-half. This is the single largest carbon decision most families will ever make.

Carbon storage as an asset. Your timber home doesn't just emit less—it actively stores carbon. The 15-30 tonnes of CO2 locked in a typical timber frame remain there for the building's entire 100+ year lifespan. As carbon accounting becomes more sophisticated, this stored carbon may gain financial value through carbon credits or preferential financing.

Regulatory alignment. With the EU's EPBD mandating whole-life carbon reporting from 2030, timber-frame homes are positioned on the right side of regulation. Properties with lower reported carbon footprints may gain advantages in green mortgage programmes, KfW energy-efficient construction loans, and resale value.

The Bottom Line

BIOBUILDS builds Passivhaus-certified timber-frame homes using 98% organic materials—combining the lowest possible embodied carbon with the lowest possible operational carbon. The result: homes that save an estimated 337 tonnes of CO2 over their lifetime compared to conventional construction. That's a home that genuinely works with the climate, not against it.

The construction industry accounts for a third of global CO2 emissions. Timber-frame construction offers a proven, scalable alternative that stores carbon instead of emitting it. The data is clear: 36-48% lower embodied carbon, roughly 1 tonne of CO2 stored per cubic metre, and full regulatory support from the EU's most significant building legislation in decades.

The question isn't whether timber-frame homes are better for the climate. The peer-reviewed evidence settled that years ago. The question is how quickly the construction industry will shift—and whether your next home will be part of the problem or part of the solution.
