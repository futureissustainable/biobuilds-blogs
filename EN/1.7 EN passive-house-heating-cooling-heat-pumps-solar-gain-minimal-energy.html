Passive House Heating & Cooling: Heat Pumps, Solar Gain & Why You Barely Need Either

A certified Passive House limits heating demand to ≤15 kWh/m²a — roughly 90% less than a conventional building. This ultra-low demand means a 150 m² home needs just 2,250 kWh of heating per year, deliverable by a compact heat pump smaller than a kitchen appliance. Free solar gains through south-facing windows and internal heat from occupants and appliances cover a large portion of that demand. In summer, external shading, night ventilation, and the insulated envelope keep interiors below 25°C without conventional air conditioning.

The most surprising thing about living in a Passive House isn't the insulation thickness or the airtightness test results — it's the heating system. Or rather, the near-absence of one. Where a conventional European home needs a 15–20 kW boiler and a network of radiators, a certified Passive House often gets by with a compact heat pump no larger than a microwave oven and no radiators at all. The building itself does most of the work.

We've specified and commissioned heating systems in every BIOBUILDS home we've delivered. The conversation always starts the same way: clients expect to discuss boiler brands and radiator layouts. Instead, we explain that their home's superinsulated envelope, airtight construction, and heat-recovery ventilation have already eliminated roughly 90% of the heating demand. What remains is a problem so small that it opens up heating options most people have never considered.

The Passive House heat balance explained

Every building gains and loses heat continuously. The Passive House standard achieves its 15 kWh/m²a heating target by dramatically reducing losses while maximising the use of free gains. The fundamental energy balance equation, as defined by the Passive House Institute (PHI), is:

Heating Demand = (Transmission Losses + Ventilation Losses) − Utilisation Factor × (Solar Gains + Internal Gains)

In a conventional building, transmission losses through walls, roof, and floor might account for 60–70% of total heat loss, while ventilation losses — both mechanical and through air leakage — contribute the remaining 30–40%. The total is so large that free gains from sun and occupants make barely a dent.

In a Passive House, the equation is fundamentally different. According to the Passive House Institute's heating load analysis, superinsulation reduces transmission losses to a fraction of conventional levels. MVHR systems recover 75–95% of ventilation heat. What remains is a tiny residual demand that solar gains and internal heat gains nearly — and sometimes entirely — cover.

Passive solar gain: free heating from the sun

Passive solar gain is heat that enters the building through windows without any mechanical system or fuel consumption. In a well-designed Passive House, solar gains typically contribute approximately 15 kWh/m²a to the annual heat balance — enough to cover or nearly cover the entire 15 kWh/m²a heating demand during shoulder seasons and mild winter days. According to Passive House design analysis by architect Mark Stephens CPHD, solar heat gains should represent roughly one-third of the annual heat balance in a residential building.

Orientation and window placement

South-facing windows in the Northern Hemisphere receive the most useful winter solar radiation. The low winter sun angle allows direct light deep into living spaces, while the high summer sun angle naturally reduces summer solar gain. According to the International Passive House Association (iPHA), even with south-facing glazing areas below 14% of the floor area, overheating thresholds are not reached in summer — making south-facing glazing the most thermally favourable orientation year-round.

East and west-facing windows are more problematic. The low morning and afternoon sun angles make shading difficult, and these orientations contribute disproportionately to summer overheating risk without proportional winter benefit. The Passive House design approach typically limits east and west-facing glazing and compensates with larger south-facing openings.

Glazing specifications that matter

Not all triple-glazed windows perform equally for solar gain. Two specifications are critical:

  • U-value (≤0.80 W/m²K): Determines how much heat escapes through the window. Lower is better for reducing losses.
  • g-value (solar heat gain coefficient): Determines how much solar energy passes through the glass. For south-facing windows in heating-dominant climates, a g-value of 0.50–0.62 is typical, balancing winter gain against summer overheating.

The Passive House Institute certifies window components that meet both requirements simultaneously — high thermal resistance and adequate solar transmittance. The rule of thumb from the UK Passivhaus Trust: glazing (excluding frames) should be approximately 15–20% of the Treated Floor Area as a starting point for PHPP modelling.

Design Principle

Passive solar design in a Passive House is not about adding south-facing glass walls. It's about optimising the ratio of solar gain to heat loss for each window, accounting for orientation, shading, and climate. In PHPP modelling, every window is evaluated individually — a south-facing window with excellent solar gain may justify a larger area, while a north-facing window must earn its place through daylight contribution alone.

Internal heat gains: people, appliances and lighting

Every person in a building generates approximately 80–100 W of heat at rest — equivalent to a bright incandescent light bulb. In a Passive House, where total heating loads are measured in single-digit watts per square metre, this body heat is not negligible. A family of four generates 320–400 W continuously during occupied hours, contributing meaningfully to the indoor temperature.

Beyond occupants, every electrical device generates heat as a byproduct: refrigerators, ovens, computers, lighting, televisions. According to research presented at the 18th International Passive House Conference, the PHI's standard value for internal heat gains in residential Passive Houses is 2.1 W/m² of treated floor area. This accounts for a standardised mix of occupancy, appliances, and lighting.

For a 150 m² home, 2.1 W/m² translates to approximately 315 W of continuous free heat — or roughly 2,760 kWh per year. Combined with solar gains, these internal sources often cover 50–80% of the already-reduced heating demand, leaving the mechanical heating system to handle only the shortfall on the coldest, darkest winter days.

Heating systems for Passive Houses

Because the peak heating load is limited to 10 W/m², Passive Houses can use heating systems that would be hopelessly undersized in conventional buildings. A 150 m² Passive House has a peak demand of just 1.5 kW — less than most electric kettles. This opens up three categories of heating approach.

1. Heated supply air via MVHR

The simplest approach: a small heating coil (electric or hydronic) integrated into the MVHR supply air duct warms the incoming fresh air by a few additional degrees. Because the ventilation system already delivers air to every room, no additional distribution network is needed. This works when the total heating demand can be met within the thermal capacity of the ventilation airflow — typically achievable in well-insulated buildings in moderate climates.

The limitation: ventilation airflow rates are sized for air quality, not heating. In very cold climates (design temperatures below -10°C), the supply air alone may not carry enough thermal energy without exceeding comfortable supply temperatures. In those cases, a supplementary system is required.

2. Compact heat pump units

The concept first published by Wolfgang Feist in 1995 integrates ventilation, space heating, and domestic hot water into a single appliance. These compact units extract residual heat from the MVHR exhaust air using a small heat pump, then use it to heat water for both the supply air coil and a domestic hot water tank.

According to the Passipedia entry on compact HVAC systems, the residual heat in the exhaust air is modest, but in a Passive House the heating requirement is so small that it can be covered almost entirely by this exhaust air enthalpy. These units have become increasingly popular in multi-family Passive House buildings across Europe and China, where heating and cooling must be addressed in a single integrated system.

3. Standalone heat pumps

For buildings where a compact unit is insufficient — larger homes, colder climates, or higher hot water demands — a standalone air-source or ground-source heat pump provides both space heating and hot water. Even in this configuration, the heat pump is far smaller than what a conventional building would require. A 3–5 kW unit typically suffices for a large Passive House, compared to the 10–20 kW systems common in standard construction.

"The residual heat in exhaust air isn't particularly high, but in a Passive House, the heat requirement is so small that it can be covered almost completely by this residual exhaust air enthalpy."

Exhaust-air heat pumps

The most popular choice for single-family Passive Houses. These compact units extract heat from the MVHR exhaust air stream (which leaves the building at approximately 20°C even in winter), amplify it via a refrigeration cycle, and deliver it to the supply air and hot water tank. Because the heat source temperature is stable and relatively warm, efficiency remains high year-round. Typical COP values of 3.0–4.5 mean that for every kWh of electricity consumed, the unit delivers 3.0–4.5 kWh of useful heat.

Air-source heat pumps

Air-source heat pumps draw heat from outdoor air using an external unit. Modern cold-climate models maintain full heating capacity down to approximately -15°C and continue operating at reduced output to -25°C. According to the European Heat Pump Association (EHPA), approximately 2.31 million heat pumps were sold across 19 European countries in 2024, bringing the installed stock to 25.5 million units — with air-source models dominating residential applications.

The European heat pump market showed signs of recovery in early 2025, with sales up 9% in H1 2025 compared to the same period in 2024, according to EHPA market data. Norway leads European adoption with 632 heat pumps per 1,000 households, followed by Finland at 524.

Ground-source heat pumps

Ground-source systems extract heat from soil or groundwater via buried loops or boreholes. The ground temperature at 2–3 metres depth remains stable at approximately 8–12°C year-round in Central Europe, providing a consistently warm heat source that enables the highest COP values of any heat pump type: 4.0–5.5 according to industry data. The trade-off is higher installation cost due to borehole drilling or trench excavation, but operating costs are the lowest of any option.

Summer cooling: staying comfortable without air conditioning

As Europe faces rising temperatures — the Copernicus Climate Change Service recorded 66 days with at least "strong heat stress" in southeastern Europe during 2024 — summer comfort is becoming as important as winter heating. Contrary to a common misconception, the Passive House envelope works in both directions: the same insulation that keeps heat inside during winter keeps heat outside during summer.

As the UK Passivhaus Trust explains: "It is often assumed that because Passivhaus buildings are well-insulated and airtight they are more likely to overheat. However, this is not the case, as Passivhaus works using building physics similar to that of a thermos flask."

The Passive House standard requires that indoor temperatures do not exceed 25°C for more than 10% of annual hours. In practice, many designers aim for a stricter target of 5% or less. Achieving this relies on a hierarchy of passive cooling strategies.

1. External solar shading — the most critical measure

According to Passipedia's analysis of summer comfort, overheating during summer is "mainly caused by (much too) high solar gains" — and "can be avoided by professional planning and with the help of simple components available on the market." External shading is far more effective than internal blinds because it blocks solar radiation before it passes through the glass and becomes trapped heat.

Effective shading strategies include:

  • Motorised external blinds: The most versatile option, allowing automated or manual control. Popular in Passivhaus buildings across Europe for their effectiveness in both cooling and optional winter heat retention.
  • Fixed horizontal overhangs: For south-facing windows, overhangs of 1.2–1.6 m depth block high summer sun while allowing low winter sun to enter. Balconies serve this purpose naturally.
  • Vertical fins: For east and west-facing glazing, slanted vertical fins address the low sun angles that horizontal overhangs cannot block.

2. Night ventilation

When outdoor temperatures drop below indoor temperatures after sunset, opening windows creates cross-ventilation that flushes accumulated heat from the building's thermal mass. This strategy is most effective in climates with a significant day-night temperature swing — common across most of Central and Northern Europe. The building's thermal mass absorbs heat during the day and releases it to the cooler night air, ready to absorb heat again the following morning.

3. MVHR summer bypass

During summer operation, the MVHR system activates a bypass mode that routes incoming air around the heat exchanger rather than through it. This prevents the system from warming already-cool incoming air with the warm exhaust stream — effectively switching from heat recovery to free cooling when outdoor air is cooler than indoor air.

4. Minimising internal heat loads

Efficient appliances, LED lighting, and thoughtful kitchen ventilation all reduce the internal heat gains that become unwanted loads in summer. The same 2.1 W/m² of internal heat that assists winter heating becomes 2.1 W/m² that must be managed in summer — making energy-efficient equipment doubly valuable in Passive Houses.

When active cooling is necessary

Passive cooling strategies handle summer comfort effectively in cool-temperate and warm-temperate climates across most of Northern and Central Europe. However, as the Passive House Accelerator notes, "active cooling in all climates warmer than warm-temperate has increasingly become standard."

When active cooling is required, the Passive House approach limits the demand dramatically. The cooling energy budget mirrors the heating criterion: ≤15 kWh/m²a for sensible cooling, with climate-specific allowances for dehumidification. Because the insulated envelope reduces heat gain and passive strategies handle the majority of cooling load, the active system needs to cover only the residual — typically deliverable by a small split system or a reversible heat pump operating in cooling mode.

The cooling load in a Passive House is typically 3–5 kW for a 150 m² home — a fraction of what a conventionally built home in the same climate would require. Properties with photovoltaic panels (required for Passive House Plus and Premium classes) can often self-supply this minimal cooling energy on hot days, when solar generation peaks precisely when cooling demand is highest.

Annual heating and cooling costs

The financial impact of minimised heating and cooling demand is substantial. A 150 m² Passive House with a heating demand of 15 kWh/m²a consumes a maximum of 2,250 kWh of heating energy annually. With a heat pump COP of 3.5, this translates to approximately 643 kWh of electricity for heating — less than a typical household uses for cooking.

At European average electricity prices of €0.25–0.30/kWh (2025 data from Eurostat), annual heating costs for that 150 m² home range from €160 to €193. Compare that to a conventional building of the same size consuming 100 kWh/m²a with a gas boiler at 90% efficiency:

Real-world performance data

Modelled performance is only valuable if it matches reality. According to field measurements compiled by the Passive House Institute, certified Passive Houses consistently deliver on their energy promises.

A study of a Passive House in Bucharest measured actual energy consumption at 13 kWh/m²a against a simulated demand of 14 kWh/m²a — a variance of less than 8%, according to research published in Energy and Buildings (ScienceDirect). A refurbishment project in Frankfurt achieved a measured value of 15.7 kWh/m²a, or just 11.2 kWh/m²a when adjusted for a standardised indoor temperature of 20°C. Consumption measurements of three Passive House developments in the same study showed consistent results of approximately 13 kWh/m²a each, compared to 65 kWh/m²a for a reference conventional settlement.

For summer performance, a monitored Passive House installation in Athens — a climate far warmer than Central Europe — achieved a measured maximum indoor temperature of 25.7°C in summer and a minimum of 20°C in winter, according to iPHA monitoring data. This closely matched the PHPP design predictions, confirming that the combination of insulation, shading, and ventilation strategy performs as modelled even in Mediterranean climates.

The Bottom Line

Passive House heating and cooling represents a fundamental inversion of conventional building logic. Instead of oversizing mechanical systems to compensate for a poorly performing envelope, the Passive House approach minimises the problem first — through superinsulation, airtightness, heat recovery, solar design, and shading — then solves the tiny remaining demand with a compact, efficient system. The result: annual climate control costs under €275 for a 150 m² home, verified comfort in both winter and summer, and near-total independence from volatile energy markets. The building does the heavy lifting. The heating system handles the details.

Passive Houses use a hierarchy of passive cooling strategies: external solar shading (motorised blinds or fixed overhangs with 1.2–1.6 m depth) blocks up to 70–80% of solar heat before it enters, the well-insulated envelope acts like a thermos keeping heat out, night ventilation flushes stored heat through open windows when temperatures drop, and the MVHR bypass prevents heat recovery when outdoor air is cooler. Photovoltaic (PV) panels generate electricity rather than direct heat, and are required for Passive House Plus (≥60 kWh/m²a generation) and Premium (≥120 kWh/m²a generation) certification classes. The electricity from PV can power the heat pump, effectively creating solar-powered heating.
