You can insulate a building to extreme levels, install triple-glazed windows, and specify the best heat pump on the market—and still watch a significant portion of your heating energy escape through invisible gaps in the building envelope. Air leakage is the silent performance killer in residential construction, and addressing it is what separates a genuinely high-performance home from one that merely looks the part on paper.

In our experience guiding over 200 households through Passivhaus certification at BIOBUILDS, airtightness is the criterion that generates the most questions—and the most misunderstandings. People worry that an airtight home will feel stuffy, trap moisture, or somehow compromise indoor air quality. The reality is the opposite. A properly sealed building with mechanical ventilation delivers fresher, cleaner air than any leaky building relying on random cracks for ventilation.

What airtightness means in construction

Building airtightness is the resistance to inward or outward air leakage through unintentional openings in the building envelope. It measures how well the outer shell of a building—walls, roof, floor, windows, and doors—prevents uncontrolled air movement driven by pressure differences from wind, temperature differences (stack effect), and mechanical ventilation systems.

Airtightness is measured in air changes per hour at 50 Pascals (ACH50), also expressed as the n50 value. This number tells you how many times the entire volume of air inside the building would be replaced in one hour when a 50 Pascal pressure difference is applied across the envelope. A lower number means fewer leaks and a tighter building.

The concept is straightforward: every gap, crack, and unsealed penetration in the building envelope is a pathway for conditioned air to escape and unconditioned air to enter. These pathways are almost never visible to the naked eye—a 1mm gap running along a 10-metre wall-to-roof junction creates a leak area equivalent to leaving a window permanently cracked open.

Key distinction

Airtightness is not the same as insulation. Insulation resists heat transfer through materials (conduction). Airtightness prevents heat loss through air movement (convection). A building needs both—superinsulation without airtightness is like wearing a thick wool jumper with the zip wide open in a windstorm.

Why airtightness matters for energy and health

Air leakage accounts for 25–40% of heating and cooling energy consumption in a typical residence, according to research compiled by the Rocky Mountain Institute (RMI). In older, poorly sealed buildings, infiltration losses can reach up to 50% of total heat loss. For a well-insulated building, this proportion actually increases—because once you've minimised conductive heat loss through walls and windows, air leakage becomes the dominant remaining pathway.

This isn't a marginal issue. According to the Air Infiltration and Ventilation Centre (AIVC), in Central European climates, each unit increase in n50 adds approximately 3–4 kWh/(m²·a) to the heating demand of a passive house. For a building targeting ≤15 kWh/m²a, even small leaks make the difference between passing and failing certification.

Energy consequences

The energy penalty of air leakage is particularly severe in winter. Warm indoor air escaping through gaps carries thermal energy directly outdoors, bypassing the insulation entirely. Cold outdoor air infiltrating through different gaps must be heated from ambient temperature, placing additional load on the heating system. According to the U.S. Department of Energy's Energy Star programme, residential infiltration accounts for enough energy waste to power over 600 coal-fired power plants across the sector.

In a Passive House, reducing infiltration also enables smaller heating and cooling equipment. Since infiltration is driven by temperature difference and wind speed, it can be a significant portion of peak demand. Eliminating it allows the entire mechanical system to be downsized—reducing capital cost alongside operating cost.

Moisture and structural consequences

Energy loss is only half the story. When warm, moisture-laden indoor air leaks outward through gaps in the envelope, it can reach the dew point inside the wall assembly. The resulting condensation creates conditions for mould growth, timber rot, and insulation degradation—often invisible until structural damage is advanced. According to Passipedia, this moisture transport through air leakage paths is significantly more damaging than vapour diffusion through intact materials.

In cold climates, exfiltration (warm air leaking out) is the primary risk. In hot-humid climates, infiltration (moist outdoor air leaking in) causes the same problems in reverse—warm moist air condensing on cold air-conditioned surfaces inside the wall.

Indoor air quality consequences

Leaky buildings draw in unfiltered outdoor air through random cracks, bringing dust, pollen, pollutants, and—in urban settings—vehicle exhaust directly into living spaces. According to the World Health Organisation's Housing and Health Guidelines (2018), inadequate ventilation and uncontrolled air infiltration are directly linked to respiratory conditions and sick building syndrome.

The counterintuitive truth: a deliberately airtight building with controlled mechanical ventilation provides better indoor air quality than a leaky building relying on random infiltration. The airtight envelope ensures all incoming air passes through filters, is tempered to comfortable temperatures, and is distributed at controlled rates to every room.

The Passive House airtightness requirement

The Passive House Institute (PHI) requires an airtightness of ≤0.6 air changes per hour at 50 Pascals (n50 ≤ 0.6 h⁻¹) for new construction certification. This is a hard pass/fail criterion—there is no margin, no averaging with other criteria, and no alternative compliance path. Fail the blower door test and you don't get certified.

For EnerPHit retrofit certification, recognising the practical constraints of working with existing structures, the threshold is relaxed to ≤1.0 ACH50.

To put these numbers in perspective: a typical code-minimum new home achieves 3–5 ACH50. The German Building Energy Act (GEG) requires no more than 3 h⁻¹ for buildings without ventilation systems and 1.5 h⁻¹ for buildings with mechanical ventilation. Older buildings often exceed 10 ACH50. An average existing home in many European markets tests at 8–15 ACH50. The Passive House threshold is approximately 5–8 times tighter than standard construction.

As of January 2025, over 25,000 certified Passive House buildings across Europe have met or exceeded this airtightness target, according to the Passive House database—proving that ≤0.6 ACH50 is consistently achievable across a wide range of construction types, climates, and building sizes.

How the blower door test works

The blower door test is the internationally recognised method for measuring building airtightness. It is mandatory for Passive House certification and uses a standardised procedure to produce repeatable, comparable results.

The equipment

A blower door apparatus consists of three components: a calibrated variable-speed fan, an adjustable frame that seals into an exterior doorway, and a manometer (pressure gauge) connected to sensors inside and outside the building. The entire setup can be installed and removed without any modification to the building.

The testing procedure

Testing follows the ISO 9972 international standard (which replaced EN 13829). The procedure runs as follows:

  1. Preparation: All exterior doors and windows are closed. Intentional openings (ventilation ducts, combustion air inlets) are sealed. Interior doors remain open to equalise pressure throughout the building.
  2. Baseline measurement: Before the fan starts, the manometer records the natural pressure difference between inside and outside over a 30-second period. Wind speed must be below 6 m/s (Beaufort scale 3) for valid results.
  3. Pressurisation test: The fan blows air into the building, creating a series of pressure steps from approximately 10 Pa to 60 Pa. At each step, the airflow rate through the fan is recorded. The fan must generate enough flow to maintain 50 Pa—the volume of air flowing through the fan at this pressure equals the volume of air leaking out through the envelope.
  4. Depressurisation test: The fan is reversed, pulling air out of the building. The same pressure steps are repeated. This tests for leaks that may behave differently under negative pressure (flaps, backdraft dampers, or poorly sealed elements that open under suction).
  5. Calculation: A regression analysis determines the airflow at exactly 50 Pa for both tests. The certification result is the average of the pressurisation and depressurisation ACH50 values.

The entire test typically takes 1–2 hours. The 50 Pascal reference pressure is equivalent to a 32 km/h wind hitting all sides of the building simultaneously—a stress test that exposes leaks which might not be noticeable under everyday conditions.

When to test

For Passive House certification, testing should occur at two stages. The first test is conducted when the airtight layer is complete but still accessible—before interior finishes cover the membrane and make repairs difficult. This allows any leaks to be identified and fixed while the work is still exposed. The second, final test confirms the as-built performance after all services are installed and penetrations are sealed.

What happens if the test fails

If the result exceeds 0.6 ACH50, diagnostic techniques are used to locate leaks. Smoke pencils (or theatrical fog generators) placed near suspected leak locations reveal air movement as visible wisps of smoke pulled toward gaps. Thermal imaging cameras show cold spots where outdoor air infiltrates through the envelope. Once leaks are found and repaired, the building is retested.

Where buildings leak: common air leakage paths

Air doesn't leak randomly—it follows predictable paths through the building envelope, concentrated at junctions, penetrations, and transitions between building elements. Understanding these pathways is essential for preventing them during construction.

Service penetrations

Every cable, pipe, duct, and conduit that passes through the airtight layer creates a potential leak. Electrical boxes, plumbing stacks, ventilation duct penetrations, and telecommunications cables are among the most common offenders. A single unsealed electrical box can leak enough air to measurably affect a blower door result.

Junction details

Where different building elements meet—wall to roof, wall to floor, wall to window—the airtight layer must transition continuously from one surface to another. These junctions are the most challenging details in airtight construction because they involve different tradespeople, different materials, and sometimes different construction sequences. A gap at a wall-to-roof junction running the full perimeter of a building can create a leak area of hundreds of square centimetres.

Window and door frames

The connection between the window frame and the surrounding wall is critical. The frame must be sealed to the airtight layer with compatible tape or sealant on the interior side, and to the weather-resistive barrier on the exterior side. According to Pro Clima, a specialist in airtight construction systems, the window-to-wall junction is the single most common source of warranty callbacks related to air leakage.

Floor-to-wall connections

Ground-floor-to-wall junctions, particularly where concrete slabs meet timber-frame walls, require careful detailing to bridge the transition between different material types. Differential movement between materials over time can open gaps that were sealed during construction.

Roof penetrations

Chimneys, flues, vent pipes, roof windows, and antenna mounts all penetrate the roof-level airtight layer. Each penetration needs a dedicated sealing solution that accommodates the specific material, temperature range, and movement characteristics of the element passing through.

Practical insight

The vast majority of air leakage problems originate during construction, not from material failure. A membrane that is properly installed and sealed will maintain its airtightness for the life of the building. Problems arise from gaps left during installation, trades working after the membrane is installed (electricians and plumbers cutting through sealed layers), and insufficient quality control at junction details.

Materials and systems for airtight construction

Achieving ≤0.6 ACH50 requires a continuous airtight layer that wraps the entire heated volume of the building—every wall, every ceiling, every floor that borders the exterior or an unheated space. The materials and systems used for this layer vary by construction type, but the principles are universal.

Airtight membranes

In timber-frame construction—the dominant method for Passive House buildings—the primary airtight layer is typically a polyethylene or polyamide membrane installed on the warm side of the insulation. Modern "intelligent" membranes, such as those produced by Pro Clima, adjust their vapour permeability based on humidity conditions: tighter in winter when the risk of interstitial condensation is highest, more open in summer to allow drying. Pro Clima describes this as the "mini-max principle" and specifies verified adhesion durability of up to 100 years.

Sealing tapes and adhesives

Membranes alone don't create an airtight layer—the joints between membrane sheets, the connections to windows, and the transitions to other building elements all require specialised sealing. Purpose-designed airtightness tapes, butyl-based adhesives, and pre-formed sealing collars for pipe penetrations are essential. Standard construction tapes and sealants are not suitable—they lack the long-term adhesion, flexibility, and temperature resistance required.

Board-based airtight layers

In some construction systems, oriented strand board (OSB) or similar structural panels serve as the airtight layer. When panel joints are sealed with appropriate tape and all penetrations are detailed correctly, board-based systems can achieve excellent airtightness. The advantage is structural—the airtight layer doubles as a load-bearing or bracing element.

Concrete and masonry

In solid construction, plastered masonry or poured concrete inherently provides good airtightness over large surface areas. The challenge shifts to junctions—where walls meet roofs, floors, or windows—and penetrations, where the same sealing principles apply as in timber-frame construction.

Why factory-built homes achieve better airtightness

The controlled environment of factory production fundamentally changes the achievability of extreme airtightness. Every variable that makes on-site airtight construction difficult—weather, coordination between trades, surface cleanliness, material storage conditions—is eliminated or controlled in a factory setting.

Controlled conditions

Airtight membranes and tapes have specific temperature and humidity ranges for optimal adhesion. On a construction site, membrane installation in rain, frost, or extreme heat compromises bonding quality. In a factory, temperature and humidity are controlled, surfaces are clean and dry, and materials are stored correctly until the moment of use.

Repeatable precision

Factory production follows standardised processes with consistent quality control. At BIOBUILDS, modules are manufactured with robotic precision in a controlled environment, with each unit following the same verified sequence of membrane installation, sealing, and inspection. This repeatability is impossible to achieve across different construction sites with different crews working in different conditions.

Pre-delivery testing

Factory-built modules can be tested for airtightness before leaving the production line, when any defects are still easily accessible for repair. On-site construction only allows testing after the building is substantially complete, when remediation may require removing finished surfaces.

Fewer site junctions

The most leak-prone details in any building are junctions between elements. A factory-built modular home has fewer on-site junctions because most connections are made in controlled factory conditions. The remaining site connections—typically module-to-module joints and module-to-foundation connections—are limited in number and can be given concentrated attention during assembly.

Airtightness and ventilation: how they work together

The most common misconception about airtight construction: "Won't the house suffocate?" The answer is an emphatic no—and understanding why requires distinguishing between air leakage (uncontrolled, unintentional) and ventilation (controlled, intentional).

A Passive House is deliberately made airtight to eliminate uncontrolled air exchange, then provides fresh air through a mechanical ventilation system with heat recovery (MVHR). This combination delivers several advantages over the leaky-building approach:

  • Filtered air supply: All incoming air passes through F7 or higher-grade filters, removing dust, pollen, and fine particulates. BIOBUILDS homes use Zehnder MVHR systems that filter 99% of dust and allergens.
  • Heat recovery: The MVHR transfers up to 90% of the heat from outgoing stale air to incoming fresh air. In a leaky building, all the heat in escaping air is lost completely.
  • Controlled distribution: Fresh air is delivered to bedrooms and living rooms at calculated rates, while stale air is extracted from kitchens and bathrooms. Every room receives the right amount of fresh air regardless of wind direction or outdoor temperature.
  • No drafts: Incoming air is tempered to near room temperature by the heat exchanger. There are no cold drafts from random gaps in the envelope.
  • Consistent humidity: Controlled ventilation rates prevent the excessive drying in winter (from over-ventilation through leaks) and excessive humidity in shoulder seasons (from under-ventilation when windows are closed).

The Passive House Institute requires MVHR systems to achieve a minimum ≥75% heat recovery efficiency. In practice, certified units from manufacturers like Zehnder consistently achieve 85–95% efficiency, meaning that in a building with 0.6 ACH50 airtightness, ventilation heat losses are reduced to a fraction of what they would be in a naturally ventilated building.

The bottom line

Airtightness without ventilation would be a problem. Airtightness with controlled mechanical ventilation and heat recovery is the solution. The Passive House approach doesn't compromise indoor air quality—it substantially improves it while reducing energy consumption by up to 95% compared to conventional buildings.

The gap between code requirements and the Passive House standard reflects a fundamental difference in philosophy. Code requirements set minimum acceptable performance—the floor below which construction is not permitted. The Passive House standard targets optimal performance—the point at which further tightening yields diminishing returns when combined with mechanical ventilation and heat recovery.

Research from the AIVC confirms this optimisation point: below approximately 0.6 ACH50, further airtightness improvements contribute negligible additional energy savings in a building with effective MVHR. Above 0.6 ACH50, infiltration heat losses begin to measurably increase heating demand, with each unit of n50 adding 3–4 kWh/(m²·a) in Central European climates.

The cost of poor airtightness in well-insulated buildings

There is a critical point that designers sometimes overlook: the better the insulation, the more airtightness matters. In a poorly insulated building, heat loss through walls, roof, and windows dominates, and air leakage is just one contributor among many. In a superinsulated Passive House, conductive losses are minimised to such a degree that air leakage becomes the single largest remaining heat loss pathway.

A study cited by the AIVC on passive houses in Central European climates demonstrated this clearly: in a single-family passive house, increasing n50 from 0.6 to 1.6 h⁻¹ raised heating demand by approximately 4 kWh/(m²·a)—a 27% increase on the 15 kWh/m²a target. In a multi-family building, the increase was approximately 3 kWh/(m²·a) per unit of n50. Superinsulation without matching airtightness is an incomplete investment.

Airtightness is not a single product or a single detail—it is a design philosophy that runs through every decision from the first sketch to the final inspection. It requires planning the continuous airtight layer from the outset, coordinating all trades to respect that layer, and verifying the result through rigorous testing.

For homeowners considering a high-performance home, airtightness is the one criterion that cannot be improved after construction. Insulation can be added, windows can be upgraded, and heating systems can be replaced—but retrofitting an airtight envelope into a completed building is prohibitively expensive and disruptive. Getting it right the first time, verified by a blower door test, is the only practical approach.
