The most common question we hear from prospective homeowners about Passive Houses isn't about insulation or triple glazing—it's about breathing. "If the building is airtight, how do you get fresh air?" The answer is one of the most elegant engineering solutions in modern residential construction: mechanical ventilation with heat recovery, or MVHR. It's the system that makes the entire Passive House concept work, simultaneously solving the problems of fresh air supply, energy efficiency, and indoor air quality in a way that opening windows never could.

According to the Passive House Institute's ventilation guidelines, high-quality MVHR units "save much more energy through the prevention of heat losses than they use to run." This isn't a marginal benefit—an MVHR system with 80% heat recovery efficiency achieves a coefficient of performance (COP) of 15, meaning for every unit of electrical energy consumed, it saves 15 units of heat energy. No other residential building technology delivers that ratio.

Why Passive Houses need mechanical ventilation

A Passive House is deliberately built to be airtight—≤0.6 air changes per hour at 50 Pascals, which is approximately 5–8 times tighter than conventional new construction. This airtightness is essential for the energy strategy to work. Without it, warm air leaks out through gaps in the building envelope, wasting the energy that superinsulation and triple glazing are designed to retain.

But airtightness creates an obligation. In a leaky building, fresh air enters randomly through gaps, cracks, and poor-fitting windows. It's uncontrolled, inefficient, and weather-dependent—but it happens automatically. In an airtight building, that accidental ventilation is deliberately eliminated. Fresh air must therefore be provided through a controlled, designed system.

This is where a critical distinction matters: airtight does not mean sealed. A Passive House exchanges its entire air volume multiple times per day—through the MVHR system. The building is airtight to uncontrolled leakage but fully ventilated through a designed path. As Passipedia explains, stale air is removed constantly from kitchens, bathrooms, and rooms with air pollution, while only outdoor air is supplied—never recirculated air.

Key Distinction

Airtightness and ventilation are complementary, not contradictory. Airtightness controls where air enters and exits; MVHR controls how much, when, and at what temperature. Together they deliver far better indoor air quality than random infiltration through building fabric.

How MVHR works: the heat exchange principle

An MVHR system operates on a simple thermodynamic principle: warm outgoing air transfers its heat to cool incoming air without the two airstreams mixing. The system consists of four air paths, two fans, a heat exchanger, and filters—all housed in a single compact unit typically smaller than a kitchen appliance.

The four airstreams

Understanding MVHR means understanding four distinct air paths:

  1. Supply air (outdoor → indoors): Fresh air drawn from outside, filtered, pre-warmed through the heat exchanger, then delivered to living rooms, bedrooms, and workrooms
  2. Extract air (indoors → heat exchanger): Stale air drawn from kitchens, bathrooms, and utility rooms where moisture and odours concentrate
  3. Exhaust air (heat exchanger → outdoors): The cooled extract air after its heat has been transferred, expelled outside
  4. Outdoor air intake: Fresh air drawn in, filtered, and pre-warmed before entering living spaces

The two airstreams—supply and extract—pass through a counter-flow heat exchanger in opposite directions, separated by thin plates. Heat transfers from the warm outgoing air to the cool incoming air through these plates. The two streams never mix, meaning no stale air is recirculated. According to PHI guidelines, the supply air is pre-warmed to approximately 16°C in winter, which means the building needs only an additional 4–5°C from space heating to reach the standard 20°C comfort temperature.

Counter-flow vs. cross-flow exchangers

The type of heat exchanger determines peak efficiency. Counter-flow exchangers—where the two airstreams flow in opposite directions along the same axis—achieve the highest heat transfer rates because the temperature gradient between streams is maintained along the full length of the exchanger. Cross-flow exchangers, where streams intersect at right angles, are simpler but less efficient. The highest-performing PHI-certified units use counter-flow designs, which is why they reach efficiencies above 90%.

Heat recovery efficiency: the numbers that matter

The Passive House Institute requires a minimum heat recovery efficiency of 75% for MVHR systems. In practice, PHI-certified units consistently exceed this threshold—many achieving 85–96% efficiency in laboratory testing.

How efficiency is measured matters. The Passive House Institute uses exhaust air efficiency rather than supply air efficiency, because the MVHR is effectively part of the thermal envelope. This measurement accounts for heat gains from the fans, losses through the MVHR casing, flow imbalance, and air leakage from the unit itself. It's a more conservative and realistic measure than manufacturer claims based on supply air temperature alone.

The certification penalty

If a non-PHI-certified MVHR unit is used in a Passive House project, the Passive House Institute applies a 12% penalty to the manufacturer's stated efficiency in PHPP calculations. A unit claiming 93% efficiency would be calculated at only 81%. This penalty reflects the uncertainty in unverified performance claims and creates a strong incentive to specify certified components.

Electrical consumption

MVHR systems consume very little electricity. The PHI sub-criterion limits specific power consumption to no more than 0.45 Wh/m³ of air transported. The best units, like the Zehnder ComfoAir Q450, achieve 0.21 Wh/m³—less than half the maximum allowed. For a typical 120 m² home with an airflow rate of 150 m³/h, annual fan energy consumption is approximately 275 kWh—comparable to running a refrigerator. The heat energy saved is many times greater.

Indoor air quality: what the research shows

The air quality benefits of MVHR in Passive Houses are well documented. A comprehensive 2024 review published in Energy and Buildings found that CO2 concentrations are generally lower in Passive House dwellings compared to naturally ventilated homes—a direct result of the continuous balanced ventilation provided by MVHR systems.

Research reported by the Passive House Accelerator quantified this difference: in homes using the Passive House ventilation approach, 74% never measured CO2 concentrations above 1,400 ppm. By contrast, dwellings without MVHR that relied on natural ventilation had CO2 levels exceeding 1,400 ppm nearly 25% of the time.

The certification quality gap

Installation quality varies dramatically between certified and non-certified projects. A UK study found that more than 80% of PHI-certified dwellings had appropriately balanced MVHR systems meeting supply-air guidelines. Among non-certified Passive House dwellings with MVHR, only 30% met both criteria. This gap underscores why the certification process itself—not just the components—matters for real-world performance.

Filtration, allergens, and particulate matter

Every breath of air entering a Passive House through the MVHR system passes through filters designed to remove particulates, pollen, and allergens. This is one of the most practical health benefits of the system, particularly for allergy sufferers and households in areas with poor outdoor air quality.

Filter grades and what they capture

MVHR systems use two filter stages:

  • Supply air filter (F7/ePM1 grade or higher): Captures fine particulate matter down to PM2.5, pollen, mold spores, and most airborne allergens. This is the primary health protection filter.
  • Extract air filter (G4/ISO Coarse grade): Protects the heat exchanger from household dust and fibres. This is a maintenance filter rather than a health filter.

The distinction between F7 and G4 supply filters has measurable consequences. Research from the University of Colorado found that Passive House buildings using G4 filters instead of the recommended F7 on the supply side showed inadequately filtered outdoor PM2.5 and higher indoor concentrations. Using the correct filter grade is not optional—it's the difference between effective and ineffective particulate protection.

Filtration Reality

MVHR filtration is highly effective against outdoor-origin pollutants—pollen, traffic particulates, industrial emissions. However, research shows it does not address indoor-origin particulates from cooking or cleaning. A recirculating cooker hood combined with boost-mode ventilation during cooking provides the complementary solution for indoor sources.

Impact on allergy sufferers

For households with asthma or hay fever, MVHR filtration transforms indoor air quality. During pollen season, a conventionally ventilated home forces a choice: open windows for fresh air and accept pollen exposure, or close windows and accept rising CO2 and humidity. MVHR eliminates this dilemma by providing continuously filtered fresh air regardless of outdoor conditions. The pollen stays outside; the fresh air comes in.

Summer operation and bypass cooling

MVHR systems are designed for year-round operation, not just winter heat recovery. In summer, when outdoor temperatures exceed indoor temperatures, recovering heat from exhaust air would be counterproductive—you'd be pre-heating incoming air when you want to keep it cool.

PHI-certified MVHR units include a summer bypass that routes incoming air around the heat exchanger when outdoor air is cooler than indoor air—typically during evening and nighttime hours. The bypass opens automatically based on temperature sensors, delivering cooler air directly into the building without passing through the heat exchanger. On hot days when outdoor air is warmer than indoor air, the heat recovery function works in reverse, actually cooling the incoming supply air to nearly room temperature.

Night purge ventilation

The summer bypass enables passive night cooling. During warm periods, the system increases airflow overnight when outdoor temperatures drop, flushing the building with cool air that is absorbed by the building's thermal mass—concrete floors, timber structure, and interior surfaces. This stored coolness then moderates indoor temperatures during the following day, reducing or eliminating the need for active cooling. The Passive House Institute notes that on hot days, the heat recovery system can cool supply air "almost to room air temperature."

Installation, commissioning, and maintenance

The best MVHR hardware delivers its promised performance only when correctly installed and commissioned. Research consistently identifies installation quality as the critical variable separating high-performing systems from underperforming ones.

Installation best practices

Several factors determine installed system performance:

  • Location within thermal envelope: The MVHR unit and all warm-side ductwork must be installed inside the insulated building envelope. Installing in an unheated loft or garage reduces effective efficiency significantly because heat is lost from the ductwork before reaching living spaces.
  • Duct sealing: PHI requires duct leakage not to exceed 3% of average airflow. Poorly sealed ducts allow conditioned air to escape and unconditioned air to infiltrate, undermining both efficiency and air quality.
  • Duct runs: Short, direct duct routes with minimal bends reduce pressure drop and fan energy. Every 90° elbow increases resistance and noise.
  • Acoustic attenuation: Sound attenuators in ductwork ensure the system operates near-silently in occupied spaces.

Commissioning

Correct commissioning—balancing supply and extract airflows at each room terminal—is essential. An unbalanced system pressurizes or depressurizes the building, which forces air through the building fabric rather than through the MVHR, defeating the purpose of airtight construction. PHI certification requires documented commissioning with measured airflow rates at every supply and extract valve.

Maintenance requirements

MVHR maintenance is straightforward but must happen regularly:

  • Filter inspection: Every 3–6 months; replacement at least annually
  • Heat exchanger cleaning: Annually—most units allow removal for washing
  • Duct inspection: Every 5–7 years; interior duct cleaning if required
  • Fan and motor check: Annual inspection for wear

A study of 150 homes with MVHR systems found that 66% did not undertake annual maintenance and visible dirt was found in 43% of homes. This maintenance gap reduces air quality and system efficiency over time. Clear homeowner education during handover is critical for long-term system performance.

MVHR vs natural ventilation: the evidence

The comparison between mechanical and natural ventilation in airtight buildings isn't close. Natural ventilation depends on wind pressure, temperature differences, and occupant behaviour—all of which are unpredictable and seasonally inconsistent.

Choosing the right MVHR system

Selecting an MVHR system involves matching the unit to the building's volume, occupancy, and climate. The key specification parameters are:

Airflow capacity

The system must deliver sufficient fresh air for the building's occupants. PHI guidelines require approximately 30 m³/h per person as a baseline ventilation rate. For a four-person household, this means a minimum capacity of 120 m³/h, with the ability to boost to 150–200 m³/h for cooking or gatherings.

HRV vs ERV

Two types of heat recovery exist:

  • HRV (Heat Recovery Ventilation): Recovers sensible heat only—temperature transfer. Best for cool-temperate climates like Central Europe where winter humidity is adequate and summer humidity is moderate.
  • ERV (Energy Recovery Ventilation): Recovers both heat and moisture. Suited to climates with extreme humidity differentials—very dry winters or very humid summers. The Zehnder ComfoAir Q450 ERV achieves a PHI-certified humidity recovery rate of 71%.

PHI certification

Specifying a PHI-certified unit eliminates the 12% efficiency penalty in PHPP calculations and provides verified performance data for heat recovery, electrical consumption, leakage, and noise. The PHI component database lists all certified ventilation units with their tested performance parameters.
